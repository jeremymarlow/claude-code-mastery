## Insights (ordered by significance)

1. **The whole panel was designed around token economy, and the math was done with a tool, not a guess — this is the standout economy moment of the session.** When the human asked "have you considered the token load for reading the conversations for all subagents? Or do they share memory of the file from the context?", Claude first corrected a real misconception (subagents get a fresh context window; the transcript is read once *per reviewer*, ~11×) and then **measured the actual corpus** instead of hand-waving. _Evidence:_ "## 🤖 Assistant / **Subagents do not share the parent's context.**" followed by the `du`/`wc` sweep and a Python aggregation (`REV = 11`, `whole corpus: 11 x 1,047,797 = ~11,525,764 tokens`). The conclusion — feasible (largest transcript = 12.7% of a 1M window), the ~11× multiplier is the price of genuine reviewer independence, and the control would be invalidated by the "cheap" single-pass alternative — is exactly the right-sized analysis: it priced the rigor rather than reflexively cutting it. This is the economist's lens applied *by* Claude, ironically while specifying the `tooling-economist` agent. [claude] [process]

2. **Model fit was correct but never examined — the session ran entirely on Opus 4.8 for work that was largely mechanical edits.** The actual labor here was a long sequence of small `Edit` calls, count-fixes, `grep`/`awk` correctness sweeps, and commit plumbing — the kind of work where Sonnet would have been materially cheaper at no real quality cost. The genuinely Opus-worthy parts (the panel rebalance deliberation, the matrix-vs-single-global tradeoff, the cost analysis) were a minority of the turns. _Evidence:_ the Context Usage panels report `Model: claude-opus-4-8` throughout, and the bulk of the transcript is edit/verify churn (e.g. the "Eight reviewers" count fix, the §13.7 renumber, the `_global.md` stale-path fix). Neither party raised model choice — a missed economy lever, especially notable since Claude was simultaneously authoring a reviewer whose mandate is "opus-vs-sonnet appropriateness per session." Honest caveat: a single interactive session can't switch models mid-stream without friction, so this is a soft fault, not a blunder. [claude] [process] [human]

3. **Tool selection was consistently right-sized — light tools for light jobs, no heavy machinery misapplied.** Claude used `Grep`/`grep`/`awk` for targeted scans (the `§13` subsection-header check, the stale-count sweeps), `Read` with offsets for precise insertion-point reads, `Edit` for surgical changes, and `Write` once for the new P9 file. Crucially, it did *not* reach for subagents, plan mode, or other heavy capability during a pure spec-authoring session where they'd add overhead — correct restraint. _Evidence:_ the repeated `grep -n '^### 13\.'` / `awk 'NR>=670' ... grep -niE` correctness sweeps, and reading exact anchor text before every `Edit` ("the file's shifted through many edits, so each replacement matches precisely"). The one batching win worth naming: context-gathering was parallelized at the start ("Let me gather context in parallel" with multiple `Bash` calls issued together). [claude] [process]

This is leverage used to its potential: the cost of re-cutting 242 leaf evals is exactly what justified pausing to ask rather than assume. [claude] [comms]

Some of this is inherent to interactive design iteration, but a more economical pattern would have been to express counts *once* via a single source (e.g. "the panel below" + a generated count) rather than hardcoding "Eight"/"twelve"/"264" in prose that then drifts — precisely the single-sourcing discipline this very repo preaches (`{{vd:key}}`, generated checklists). ## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** Tool selection was disciplined and right-sized throughout: targeted searches, precise anchored edits, parallel context-gathering, and a refusal to over-tool a spec-authoring session. The token-load analysis was the high point — a real, measured economy argument that validated (rather than blindly cut) the design's rigor, and correctly identified that the ~11× read multiplier is the *cost of the experiment's validity*, not waste. The `check-evaluations` reversal showed it could be argued out of its own earlier position on sound cost/verifiability grounds.

**Claude — didn't.** Model fit went unexamined: a session that was mostly mechanical edits ran wholly on the most expensive model, and neither party flagged it — a conspicuous miss given Claude was literally authoring the lens that judges model fit. The panel-size churn (8→12→11) generated avoidable count-rework that hardcoded numerals into prose, drifting against the repo's own single-sourcing ethos and forcing repeated sweeps (one caught by the human). Its communication, while clear, ran long — the recap blocks after each gate were thorough but token-heavy, a minor economy cost in their own right.

**Human — worked.** The human was a model of economical steering: low-token, high-leverage interventions placed at the cheapest moment to change a decision (before build), plus the single most valuable question of the session — the token-load probe — which forced the cost analysis that should accompany any fan-out design. Catching the stale "Eight reviewers" count was a free correctness contribution. The gate-by-gate cadence kept rework bounded.

**Human — didn't.** Little to fault on the economy axis. The one observation: the human drove the panel through three sizes via successive questions, which (combined with hardcoded counts) was the root cause of the count-churn; a single "let's settle the panel before I commit to a number anywhere" framing earlier might have collapsed two rounds into one. Minor, and arguably the right way to deliberate an important, expensive-to-reverse choice.

## Bottom line

A tightly-run, economical planning session whose defining feature is that economy itself became a first-class design input — the token-load probe and its measured answer are exactly the discipline this reviewer prizes, and the right-sized tool use was consistent. The two leverage points left on the table are model fit (Opus used for mostly-mechanical work, unexamined) and single-sourcing of counts (hardcoded numerals churned across the panel resizing, forcing repeat sweeps). Neither is severe; both are the kind of thing the very `tooling-economist` agent being authored here would flag on a future pass.
