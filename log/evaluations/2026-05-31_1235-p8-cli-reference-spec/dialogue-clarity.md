## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human author** ran this gate-driven session about as well as a human can. Instructions were scoped, decisive, and — crucially — corrective at the level of *principle*, not just the instance: the anti-shoehorn steer reshaped enforcement design, and the "store it in the repo, not machine memory" instruction fixed a durability mistake at the right altitude. The human also held the line on process (rejecting the premature design commit with a one-line rule) without micromanaging the work. If there is a weakness, it is minor: the human occasionally let Claude carry a lot of restated context forward without trimming it, but that is Claude's verbosity to fix, not the human's.

**Claude** communicated with strong structure — lede-first verdicts, scannable tables, explicit "here are your options" closings, and genuine surfacing of tradeoffs rather than compliance. It reported a latent enforcement gap honestly and recovered from its one real misstep (the premature design commit) without dressing it up. The two costs: (a) the premature-commit gate violation, which contradicted its own just-stated process; and (b) persistent verbosity that re-litigates settled decisions across consecutive turns, making each new message carry more re-paid context than necessary. The faint sycophantic openers ("the most valuable thing you've said") are a minor third item — cheap, but trimmable.

## Bottom line

A clean, well-run spec-driven exchange where the human's scoped, principle-level instructions met a Claude that mostly surfaced tradeoffs instead of complying. The through-line is high bidirectional clarity with two blemishes on Claude's side: one honest-but-real gate violation (attempting to commit an unreviewed design), and a steady verbosity that buries new signal under restated decisions.
