## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Search/verify economy was excellent: batched probes, anchored greps, slice reads, file-grounded claims, and `make check` used as the correct fast inner-loop gate with strict/hook deferred to commit. Once a course of action was set, execution was clean and self-checking (grep sweeps confirming zero stray references after every swap). The reasoning replies were genuinely sharp — the command/skill-boundary teaching beat and the discovery that a checked box contradicts a pending L7 entry are both real, non-obvious insights. Communication was candid about cost and scrupulous about not over-claiming.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The defining flaw is sequencing: it built artifacts before settling whether they were the *right* artifacts, then paid for it with two rebuild-and-rework cycles on a committed unit. The `/new-unit` dogfood was self-defeating theater — a tool simulated, demonstrated, and deleted in one session. It under-used two capabilities it had in hand: AskUserQuestion was spent on the build-vs-reference question instead of the which-artifacts question, and plan mode was skipped entirely on a multi-step plan that begged for a reviewable gate.

**Human — strengths.** The human supplied the session's highest-leverage thinking through pointed, Socratic questions, each of which exposed a genuine defect (prop artifacts, error-prone edit path, L7 contradiction). They enforced the working agreements precisely — per-change commit approval and explicit push authorization — and chose to author U13 in a fresh session specifically to truly dogfood the new skills, which is exactly the right validation instinct.

**Human — weaknesses.** The steering, though excellent, was reactive: the value-of-artifact questions arrived after commits, converting what could have been one clean commit into three plus a fix. A single up-front "let's nail down the two artifacts and confirm they have real consumers before you build" would have captured the same insight at a fraction of the rework.

## Bottom line

This session reached a good final destination — a clean, pushed U12 with two genuinely-used dogfood artifacts and a correctly-gated `close-unit` — but took an expensive, switchback route to get there. The through-line is **build-before-deciding**: Claude's tool *execution* (batched search, anchored greps, self-verifying edits, disciplined `make check`) was consistently economical, while its tool *strategy* left real leverage on the table — AskUserQuestion aimed at the wrong question, plan mode unused, and a "dogfood" command that the session itself proved was a prop. The human's Socratic review was the corrective force and the source of the best ideas, but because it landed after each commit, it turned one unit's worth of work into three commits and a fix. The economics would have been markedly better had the "which artifacts actually have a consumer?" conversation happened in plan mode before the first `Write`.
