# Content review — technical-editor
Reviewer: technical-editor — prose register, density, rhythm, skimmability, structure
Date: 2026-06-09
Scope read: all 16 rendered units + requirements §1 + workflows.md + learner docs

## Headline

The individual sentences are good — often very good — and that is the problem: there are no other kinds of sentences. All 16 units are written in a single relentless register (aphorism, antithesis, em-dash, bold) that reads brilliantly for one unit and becomes white noise by unit nine. The course's structural skeleton is excellent and the section discipline is genuinely strong, but the prose inside it was generated to a formula, and the formula shows: verbatim boilerplate repeated across six units, machine-spliced version-data tokens that break grammar mid-sentence, and — fatally for trust — raw tool-call XML (`</content>`, `</invoke>`) shipped at the bottom of four rendered units. A skeptical senior reader ("Sam," who "resents hand-holding") will spot these tells, and a course whose central lesson is "verify the AI's output" cannot afford to look like unverified AI output. The fix is not a rewrite; it is one mechanical cleanup pass and one de-intensification pass.

## Cross-cutting findings

**F1 — Raw generation artifacts are shipped in four learner-facing units.** Severity: high.
Evidence: `course/units/01-onboarding-first-win/unit.md:173-174` ends with literal lines `</content>` and `</invoke>`; same leak at `02-explore-a-codebase/unit.md:157`, `03-operate-safely/unit.md:215-216`, `05-ship-a-feature/unit.md:190-191`. These are AI tool-call closing tags that escaped into the rendered output. Why it matters: this is the single worst trust failure available to this particular course. U1 — the learner's very first page, whose lesson is "read the diff before you accept it" — ends with unread AI residue. Related smaller artifacts: stray single-space orphan lines breaking bullet lists at `12-commands-and-skills/unit.md:28`, `14-hooks/unit.md:26`, `16-automate-and-scale/unit.md:30`.

**F2 — Version-data tokens are spliced into sentences raw, breaking grammar and duplicating prose.** Severity: high.
The `{{vd:key}}` mechanism pastes pre-written fact-strings into running prose, and the seams are visible everywhere. Examples:
- `03-operate-safely/unit.md:65`: "(Plan mode (`--permission-mode plan`): Claude plans without writing changes until you approve.)." — a full sentence with its own period nested inside parentheses inside another sentence.
- `04-memory-and-context/unit.md:56`: "**1 — Project memory (`CLAUDE.md`).** Project memory via CLAUDE.md, auto-discovered at startup (auto-memory). A `CLAUDE.md` is a file…" — a verbless fragment that restates the heading, then the real sentence restates both.
- `05-ship-a-feature/unit.md:67`: "Plan mode (`--permission-mode plan`): Claude plans without writing changes until you approve. In plan mode Claude investigates and proposes a plan **without writing any files**" — the same fact twice, back to back.
- Worst case, `12-commands-and-skills/unit.md:65, 97, 217`: the same ~70-word token ("Custom slash commands are user-defined skills… Gotcha: `$1` is the SECOND arg, not the first…") is pasted verbatim **three times in one unit**, including its internal "Gotcha:" sentence. `15-mcp-and-vetting/unit.md:75/229` and `16-automate-and-scale/unit.md:108/224` repeat their tokens twice each.
Before (U4:56): "Project memory via CLAUDE.md, auto-discovered at startup (auto-memory). A `CLAUDE.md` is a file Claude auto-discovers and loads as standing instructions…"
After: "A `CLAUDE.md` is a file Claude auto-discovers at startup and loads as standing instructions — your project's conventions, architecture, and gotchas, stated once. (The CLI calls this *auto-memory*.)"
Why it matters: the single-source mechanism is sound, but tokens must be either (a) set off as quoted reference callouts/code blocks, or (b) absorbed by prose written around their exact wording. Inline splicing produces sentences no human wrote, and the reader can tell.

**F3 — One rhetorical register, never varied: every paragraph lands an aphoristic antithesis.** Severity: high.
The house move is "X, not Y": I count ~99 ", not <word>" constructions across the 16 units, plus variants — "a lead, not a verdict" (U11, three times: lines 40, 105, plus "as leads" at 67), "enforced, not invoked" (U14:105), "a decision, not a formality" (U5:73-74), "decisions, not documentation" (U4:70), "observed, not assumed" (U14:198, U16:177), "Be a scientist, not a guesser" (U7:24, again at workflows.md:55). "Green tests are necessary, not sufficient" appears in full in U1:62, U3:110-111, U5:84, U6:86, U11:31/99. Individually these are good lines; the problem is dosage. When every second paragraph closes on an epigram ("Green check = first win. That is the whole unit." U1:34; "The mechanics are easy; the judgment is the skill." U15:145-146; "Automation amplifies whatever you point it at, including mistakes." U16:102), emphasis stops carrying information — the prose *performs* mastery instead of teaching it, and by U11 the reader has built an immunity. This is also the most identifiable LLM tell in the text: the antithesis-closer is the model's default cadence, and 16 units of it reads generated.
Before (U10:98-99): "A spec you didn't review is theater. The entire value of spec-driven development is the *reviewed alignment* — three artifacts Claude generated and you rubber-stamped are worse than no spec, because they look like alignment without being it."
After: "A spec you didn't review is theater. If Claude generated all three artifacts and you skimmed them, you have the costs of the process and none of the alignment." (One aphorism, then plain statement — let the line breathe instead of immediately re-explaining it in a second antithesis.)

**F4 — Bold and em-dash density defeats the skimmability it is meant to serve.** Severity: high.
Counts per unit: em-dashes 23–64 (steady ~1 per 40 words across all 16 units — a fingerprint, not a choice); bold spans 35–88 per unit (U14: 88 bold spans in 2,310 words). Bold is routinely applied to entire sentences ("**read the one-line diff before accepting it**", U1:33; "**the harness fires it deterministically** — it can't be forgotten", U14:43-44) and to multiple phrases per paragraph, so a skimming reader gets no figure/ground — everything is highlighted, so nothing is. Em-dash chains regularly stack three clauses in one sentence: "Delegate it and the reading happens in the subagent's context — only the *answer* (file:line list + a sentence) returns. This is the highest-value reason in a long session: it keeps your main thread clean." (U13:73-75 — this one is fine; U13:88-91, with two bolds, a parenthetical, and an em-dash inside one sentence, is not.) Why it matters: the audience is defined as a skimmer ("a quick study who resents hand-holding," requirements.md:31). The course's *section* structure serves that reader superbly; the *intra-paragraph* formatting fights them.
Before (U3:53-56): "That power is exactly the risk. The expensive failure in AI-assisted coding is not a wrong *answer*; it's a confidently *executed* wrong or hostile *action*."
After: "That power is the risk. The expensive failure isn't a wrong answer — it's a wrong action, confidently executed." (Same idea, one emphasis device instead of four italics.)

**F5 — Skip-checks are single 55–77-word sentences — the section that most needs to be scannable is the densest prose in each unit.** Severity: medium.
Word counts of the skip-check body: U6=77, U14=75, U16=74, U13=72, U9=71 — each a single sentence with three to five embedded clauses and an em-dash coda. E.g. U9:49-54 is one ~90-word sentence ("take an unfamiliar, untested, messy codebase, build and *validate* a mental model… — rather than rewriting in one big swing and *hoping* nothing changed."). A skip-check is a triage instrument; a senior reader should be able to scan it in five seconds.
Before (U6:44-48, one sentence).
After: "Skip this unit if all of these are habit: (a) you write the test before the implementation; (b) you run it and confirm it fails for the *right* reason — an assertion failure, not a typo; (c) you read the green implementation for hard-coded shortcuts." Three checkable bullets beat one breathless sentence.

**F6 — Boilerplate repeated verbatim across units reads as template-fill, and the cross-reference load escalates until late units read like an index.** Severity: medium.
"The generalized pattern lives once in [`meta/workflows.md`]…; read it there. The short version: … The rest of this section is what's specific to *applying* it" appears nearly word-for-word in six units (U5:56-59, U6:60-63, U7:58-62, U8:57-61, U10:62-66, U11:65-68). The deferral to workflows.md is the right architecture, but each unit then restates the pattern anyway ("The short version: …"), so the deferral saves nothing and the repeated frame is pure tell. Meanwhile inline cross-unit links climb from 0 (U1–U3) to 11 (U13, U14) to **22** (U16): U16's concept section opens by re-listing all four prior units with links (lines 57-61), and its safety section cites [Hooks] and [Operate safely] four times each. Reinforcement is pedagogically right; doing it as a parenthetical link-litany after every clause makes late-course paragraphs nearly unreadable aloud. Severity medium — the early units prove the team knows how to weave a callback into prose ("the habit from **Explore a codebase**, now load-bearing," U5:63 — good); the late units stopped weaving and started citing.

**F7 — Worked examples increasingly point the learner at the course's own build instead of showing an artifact.** Severity: medium.
"This repo/this course is itself the worked example" is the move in U5:99, U6:104, U7:101, U8:101, U9:124, U10:115, U11:117, U12:101, U14:121, U16:113. Some of these are excellent (U14's `check-on-edit` walkthrough is the best worked example in the course; U15's runnable MCP server is second). But U6's worked example contains no code, no test, no red output — it *describes* a TDD episode about the course's own `check-version-refs` tooling, in terms (`vd:` keys, fixture files) a learner six units in has no stake in. U7:101-110 and U11:117-125 retell the same internal anecdote (the doubled-brace `vd:` false positive) from two angles. For Sam, who has never seen a real session, a worked example should show the thing: a short transcript excerpt, an actual failing-test output, an actual diff. Telling the learner "we did this while building what you're reading" ten times is dogfooding as self-regard; once or twice is authenticity.

**F8 — Where the register does work, credit it: the metaphor system is coherent and the structural signposting is excellent.** Severity: low (a strength to preserve, with one trim).
"Fence / blast radius / gate" is introduced in U3 and reused accurately through U13 ("blast radius designed to zero," U13:148) and U14 — that is real editorial discipline, rare in generated text. The eight-section template (objectives → TL;DR → skip-check → concept → worked example → lab → pitfalls → going deeper) is identical in all 16 units and is the course's best skimmability asset; a reader can always find their way back to a fact. One trim: late units pile near-synonyms — fence, gate, net, guardrail, backstop all appear in U14/U16 — and the system blurs exactly where it should pay off.

## Top 5 enhancement recommendations

1. **Mechanical cleanup pass, today:** strip `</content>`/`</invoke>` from U1/U2/U3/U5 (`unit.src.md` + re-render) and the orphan space-lines in U12/U14/U16; then add a render-time check that fails on any `</`-prefixed XML-ish line or trailing non-markdown in rendered units, so this class of leak is structurally impossible.
2. **Change the vd-token contract from "inline sentence constituent" to "quoted reference."** Render tokens either as a set-off line (e.g. a `>` callout: "*CLI surface (verified 2.1.159):* …token…") or require the source prose to absorb the token's exact words grammatically. Audit the ~20 splice points listed under F2 first; U12's triple paste should become one callout plus two short references.
3. **A de-intensification edit of U9–U16 (and U11 especially):** budget one aphorism per section and one bold phrase per paragraph; convert whole-sentence bolds to plain prose; cut the ", not X" antithesis count roughly in half by letting the second half of each pair go unstated where context carries it. The maxims to keep in full: one statement of "green is necessary, not sufficient" (U1), one "a lead, not a verdict" (U11) — thereafter use the shorthand only.
4. **Restructure all 16 skip-checks as 3–4 checkable bullets** (pattern in F5). This is the highest skimmability win per word changed, and it makes the skip decision genuinely fast for the audience the spec defines.
5. **Replace the two weakest worked examples (U6, U7) with artifact-bearing ones:** a real 10-line red-test output plus the green diff for U6; a real session excerpt confirming the wrong-format date comparison for U7. Cap "this repo is the worked example" to the units where the learner can open and run the artifact (U4, U10, U12, U14, U15); retell the internal `vd:`-false-positive anecdote once, not twice (U7 vs U11 — keep U11's version).

## Out-of-scope flags

- **No unit ever shows a Claude session transcript.** The learner is told how to prompt, steer, and verify for 16 units without once seeing what a good session looks like on screen. This smells like a missing learner outcome (example-modeling), not just an editorial gap — flagging for the curriculum reviewers.
- **Stated reading times look underestimated** for the rendered density (U13: "13 min" for 2,918 words of heavily formatted text; U12: "10 min" with the triple token paste). Where the estimate comes from is a tooling/meta question, not prose.
- **The render pipeline (`tools/render-units`) is where F1/F2 fixes belong** — the prose findings above are symptoms of generator behavior I did not review.
- **workflows.md deferral architecture:** units defer to it and then restate "the short version" anyway, so the learner reads each pattern twice and workflows.md a third time if they click through. Whether the deferral should remain is a design question; the editorial symptom (verbatim frame, F6) is fixable either way.
- **stuck.md and progress-checklist.md are written in a plainer, calmer register than the units** — and read better for it. Worth noting as the voice target, but learner-doc consistency strategy is beyond unit prose.
- The 11-reviewer-panel passage in U13 references `course/case-studies/collaboration-retrospective.md`, which I did not read; if that doc doesn't deliver what U13 promises, the cross-reference oversells — someone in scope for case studies should check.
