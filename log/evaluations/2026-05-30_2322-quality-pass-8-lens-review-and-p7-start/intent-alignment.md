## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session about as well as a deliberately-loose quality pass can be run. The under-specification was intentional and well-judged: a tight up-front spec would have pre-empted the discovery that the dominant defect was a code-citation reflex nobody had named yet. They specified precisely when concrete artifacts appeared (the ID-confusion criterion, the sandbox-U1 strategy, the title critique), and — crucially — they were the alignment backstop, catching both the scope drift and the methodology lapse in a single, precisely-worded turn (line 4393). They also gated correctly: "Show me the rendered U1 before I approve" (line 4964), then "approved. Move to stage B" (line 5042) — explicit per-change approval, never blanket. The one mild cost of their style is that it leans on Claude to police scope, and here Claude wasn't watching that boundary.

**Claude** was strong on everything downstream of "what to inspect." Its lens framework, baseline-first instinct, severity-ranked report with line-level evidence, and per-change scoping were all high quality, and its alignment *recovery* (the Stage A/B/C plan, refusing to invent new requirements) was genuinely excellent. Its communication was a standout all session: every action was narrated with its rationale, options were concrete and testable, and it volunteered honest caveats (admitting the earlier "Lens 8 accessibility ✅" was wrong, line 3404; flagging the ragged source line-wrapping and the un-de-"win"-ed `use_case` at approval time, lines 5037–5038). The one real weakness is process-level intent alignment: it let an explicitly lightweight pass grow new tooling, a file rename, and Makefile changes without pausing to confirm that crossing from "polish" into "infra" was sanctioned — the very boundary the human had fuzzily drawn. An "this is bigger than polish; spec it or proceed ad-hoc?" checkpoint at line 3404 would have caught what the human had to catch a thousand lines later.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning collaboration whose one fault line is illuminating: Claude excelled at surfacing and resolving ambiguity about *what to build*, but missed the meta-question of *how much process the building deserved* — exactly the boundary the human had deliberately left fuzzy. The human caught it, named it cleanly, and both converged fast on a disciplined recovery (formalize as P7, branch, clean commits). The session lands as did-well overall because the misalignment was caught early enough to cost no committed history and was repaired without over-correction — but the lesson is that under-specified *process* scope needs a Claude-initiated checkpoint the moment the work changes character, not just well-formed questions about content.
