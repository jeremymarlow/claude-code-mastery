## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** On responsible-use grounds this is among the cleaner sessions I could review. The commit/push approval discipline is flawless across five separate authorization points; there is no inferred permission, no "I'll just push since you said commit," no merge-to-main reach. Blast radius was consciously managed (feature branch, scoped adds, no force-push on pushed history) and *verbalized* so the human could audit the reasoning. The one genuinely dangerous primitive used — `git commit --amend` — was gated on a verified-unpushed check. And critically, when its own operational flakiness cancelled a push, Claude refused to claim success and re-verified with `git status`/`ls-remote` before asserting anything outward-facing. That instinct — never report an irreversible action you haven't confirmed — is the heart of safe practice.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The repeated oversized parallel batches are a real, if non-safety-critical, fault: they produced two cascade failures, a wasted-call storm, and a spurious `AskUserQuestion` that confused the human. From a safety lens the concern is that flakiness *near* git operations raises the odds of acting on stale state — and indeed Claude reported "Pushing now" before confirming the first push had even run. It recovered correctly each time, but the noise eroded the human's ability to trust the running narrative, which is itself an oversight cost. Claude diagnosed this honestly and self-corrected, but only after three incidents.

**Human — strengths.** The human set a crisp guardrail regime (codified in CLAUDE.md, reinforced live) and applied it with per-change approvals that were never vague. They stayed alert: the "no changes to add" rejection caught a silent no-op edit before it could mislead a commit, and choosing "push only" over the merge option kept `main` protected behind a deliberate human decision. This is the kind of oversight that makes the approval model actually work rather than rubber-stamping.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault on the safety axis. If anything, the human could have intervened earlier on the parallel-batch flakiness (the project memory already warns about it) rather than absorbing the friction across two incidents — but that is an efficiency nudge, not a safety lapse.

## Bottom line

A disciplined, low-blast-radius session where the approval contract was honored on every single commit and push, the one history-rewriting operation was verified-safe first, and the merge-to-main temptation was explicitly handed back to the human. The only real blemish — self-inflicted parallel-call cascades — was an operational nuisance that briefly risked acting on stale git state, but Claude consistently re-verified before claiming any irreversible or outward-facing result and named the problem candidly. On the responsible-use lens, both parties earned high marks.
