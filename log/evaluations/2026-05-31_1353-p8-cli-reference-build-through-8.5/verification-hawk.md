## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a strong verification performance. The dominant pattern is read-the-artifact: parsed trees dumped and spot-checked by name, rendered pages grepped for leaked tokens, gates proven to *fail* on tampered input, a dormant feature exercised via a synthetic bug-injection. The provenance discipline was airtight — every version-specific fact was fetched from the canonical docs/changelog with URL + date, redirects followed rather than guessed, removed commands excluded against the installed CLI. When checks behaved unexpectedly (the changelog fail-case passing), Claude debugged the *check* rather than trusting it. And the 8.4 self-correction was honest and state-verified rather than excuse-accepting.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two soft spots. The R8 traceability discrepancy was flagged and partially verified (predates edits) but its claimed contradiction with the decisions log was left as speculation for the whole session. And the communication carries a self-congratulatory edge — "All validations pass," "8.5 is solid" — that subtly asserts a verdict the human's review is the real arbiter of; the 8.4 over-commit grew from the same commit-per-slice momentum.

**Human — strengths.** The human is the verification conscience of this session. Distrusting the green ("how is make check green if the version bumped?"), reviewing the *rendered* artifact and finding the missing version history, and demanding a line number rather than accepting a hand-wave are textbook verify-don't-trust. The 8.4 process catch was precise, and the human probed root cause rather than just scolding — then accepted Claude's evidence-based correction of the stale-memory hypothesis gracefully. Approvals were explicit and per-change.

**Human — weaknesses.** Few in this lens. The human approved the auto-diff `added_in` approach "as-is" without independently exercising it, but Claude's synthetic test covered that. Reasonable trust given Claude's demonstrated rigor.

## Bottom line
