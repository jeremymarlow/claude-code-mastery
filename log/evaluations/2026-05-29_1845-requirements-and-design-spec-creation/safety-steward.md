## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked.** Approval discipline was the standout. The gate was never inferred: Claude proactively refused to mark Requirements "approved" until the human said so, even though it had just done the work that would naturally precede that flip, and even though the user had signaled session-end. It fenced every phase transition behind an explicit checkpoint, kept the irreversible-ish step (building design on unreviewed requirements) blocked, and re-verified filesystem state after a partial batch failure instead of assuming. It also escalated safety in the artifact itself, treating R10 as the requirement it would least compromise.

**Claude — what didn't.** Almost nothing in scope to fault. The only blemish is the unflagged reach outside the repo into `~/.claude/...` memory files — reversible and minor, but a steward names cross-boundary writes. I'd also gently note the early "throw a big parallel batch and one failure cancels twelve operations" pattern (lines 207-302): harmless here because everything was idempotent `mkdir`/`Write`, but the same habit applied to non-idempotent or destructive operations is where a careless batch becomes a blast-radius problem. Worth carrying forward as a caution, not a fault for this session.

**Human — what worked.** Clear guardrails, genuine review (the turn-by-turn requirement walk was the opposite of rubber-stamping), per-change approval of the gate flip, and a clean recovery from the accidental question rejection. The human also actively *strengthened* the safety surface by accepting all five R10 hardening changes including explicit blast-radius/reversibility discipline.

**Human — what didn't.** Nothing of safety substance. One could observe that the human never raised the out-of-repo memory writes either — but the burden there is more on the agent to surface it.

## Bottom line

A requirements-and-design authoring session with no git, no commits, no pushes, and no destructive shell — so the heavy-artillery approval faults were never reachable.
