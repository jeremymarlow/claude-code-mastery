## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is near-textbook spec-driven execution. The gate was treated as a recorded fact and verified before merging; the `--no-ff` choice preserved the gate in history; work was cut into phase-sized slices each ending in an explicit stop-and-ask; state files were updated as work landed; deferrals were logged in multiple coordinated places; and verification caught real bugs rather than serving as theater. Most impressive was the candor under the human's continuity probe — Claude did not reassure, it audited itself and named the stale §3, the scattered-ledger problem, and the DoD-gate gap, then proposed the minimal fix and explicitly warned against "a second tracking system [that] would just rot in parallel." That is mature process judgment.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two. First, `IMPLEMENTATION.md` §3 — the canonical first-read state file — was allowed to go stale across two whole phases; that it took a human prompt to surface means the in-flight hygiene was incomplete on the one file that matters most for cold starts. Claude's own fix (a standing per-phase update obligation) is the right remedy, but the lapse was real. Second, the venv install fired before asking, requiring a human interrupt; on a repo whose central rule is "don't author environment specifics from memory," provisioning the environment unilaterally was slightly off-key.

**Human — strengths.** Clear, decisive instructions; per-slice approvals that never devolved into blanket permission; and — most valuably — two sharp boundary-questions that materially improved the project's continuity posture. The continuity probe was the single best process intervention of the session.

**Human — weaknesses.** Approvals were granted on Claude's summaries rather than on inspection of the artifacts or diffs; the human never opened the generated `meta/*` files or the tools to spot-check. With a less rigorous collaborator that posture would be risky. The human caught the venv detour reactively (after it fired) rather than having set the environment expectation up front.

## Bottom line

A strong, disciplined build session: a real (verified, history-marked) design gate, two cleanly-sliced and separately-committed execution phases each ending in an honest stop-and-ask, state files kept current as work landed, and verification that earned its keep by catching genuine bugs. The one process crack — a stale `IMPLEMENTATION.md` §3 and deferrals scattered without a single ledger — was surfaced by the human's continuity probe and closed thoroughly, leaving the repo in genuinely good shape for a cold start. The through-line: spec-driven discipline held under real execution pressure, with the human's boundary-questions supplying the audit that Claude's in-flight hygiene didn't quite reach on its own.
