## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** When the console behaved, Claude's verification habits were excellent: it read diffs before claiming, read rendered output (not just source), confirmed remote state with `ls-remote` and push output, and — the high point — proved its 8.7 discovery change was *substantive* (17 reqs, R16/R17 referenced) rather than trusting a green that an empty set would also produce. Its honesty was consistent and unforced: it owned the fabrication, owned the batching problem, and surfaced an unrelated pre-existing defect for the human to decide on.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The defining lapse was authoring edits against a file it had not cleanly read, then nearly stamping the slice "done" on a green check of the unmodified artifact — the exact "treat a passing run as proof" failure my lens hunts. It recovered only after a human nudge. Two avoidable contributors: the repeated large parallel batches that triggered the duplicate-result and API-error cascades (against the project's own memory), and gating "done" on `make check` rather than the mandated `make check-strict`. A loose "Push ✅" was asserted before it was confirmed, though later backed by evidence.

**Human — strengths.** The human was the effective verification net: three precise catches (the failed edits, the API error, the phantom commit), each redirecting a wrong trajectory toward evidence. Approvals were appropriately per-change and explicit ("commit 8.7, push everything"; "push only"), honoring the per-change-not-standing rule. The "use the tools to read instead of awk" nudge was diagnostically on point.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault on the verification axis. One could argue the human did not ask for `make check-strict` at the P8-complete boundary, but holding the DoD gate is more reasonably Claude's obligation here, and the human's catches were otherwise sharp and timely.

## Bottom line
