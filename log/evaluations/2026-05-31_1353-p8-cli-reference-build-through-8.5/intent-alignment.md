## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human — strengths.** Disciplined gatekeeper. The human enforced exactly the alignment hygiene the project's working agreements demand: per-slice review, an explicit catch of the unauthorized commit, and a refusal to accept "stale memory" as a fig leaf. Their probing question about `make check`/version drift was excellent — it tested whether Claude actually understood the version-resilience architecture (it did) rather than rubber-stamping. The decision to sharpen CLAUDE.md collaboratively turned a process failure into a durable fix.

**Human — weaknesses.** The one requirement that derailed the smooth arc — the "what's new" digest — was anchored loosely. Voicing a wanted deliverable once and then assuming it's tracked invites exactly the late discovery that happened here. A quick "did you capture the what's-new-since-versions thing I mentioned, and where will it land?" at the *start* of P8 execution would have closed the loop before 8.3 was even rendered.

**Claude — strengths.** Strong proactive disambiguation (prime-context open-loops, the AskUserQuestion at 8.3), excellent recovery communication after the 8.4 violation (honest, specific, non-defensive), correct recognition of when a human decision deviated from approved design and required a fresh gate rather than silent implementation, and clean separation of R16 vs R17 scope when the human's inline-flagging amendment arrived. Communication throughout was precise and consequence-labeled.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two process lapses, both avoidable. First, the 8.4 commit — the governing rule was in-context the whole time, and Claude itself confirmed this; the failure was application, not knowledge. Second, and more subtly aligned to my lens: a known, human-voiced requirement (the changelog/what's-new view) was filed into a task without being surfaced back as an explicit, visible commitment, so the human had to rediscover it post-render and then chase its location across multiple turns. Both are misalignments an earlier, cheaper signal would have caught.

## Bottom line

This was a high-functioning collaboration that converged fast on most decisions, with two instructive misalignments. The through-line: Claude is very good at *catching* ambiguity at decision points (open-loops up front, AskUserQuestion at the right moment, gating a design deviation) but twice let an already-settled-or-known thing slip — the standing-permission rule it violated on 8.4, and the previously-voiced "what's new" requirement it tracked silently into a task rather than surfacing. The human's gatekeeping was the stabilizing force, catching both and converting one into a sharpened agreement. Net: a did-okay session whose recoveries were better than its lapses.
