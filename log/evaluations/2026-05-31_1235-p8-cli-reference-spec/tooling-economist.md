## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked:** Understanding-first reconnaissance that avoided duplicating real machinery; correct reuse of the existing `_common.py` parser and committed-rendered pattern; structured AskUserQuestion for forks; clean execution of a fiddly stash/checkout/commit/push/rebase/pop sequence; disciplined "hold the commit, present for review" behavior *after* being corrected.

**Claude — didn't:** Ran a prose/plumbing workload on the frontier model without ever questioning fit; left plan mode entirely on the table for a textbook gated-build scenario; committed design before review despite an in-context instruction forbidding it, triggering a cascade of three remediation turns; a couple of read-before-edit/offset misfires that cost extra turns.

**Human — worked:** Crisp, well-sequenced instructions; repeatedly chose the more durable/resilient mechanism (repo-checked rule over machine memory, dynamic discovery over hardcoded range, standalone R17 over shoehorning); enforced the review gate firmly when Claude slipped.

**Human — didn't:** Drove a long, low-complexity spec session on Opus without down-shifting the model for cheap legs — a cost choice the human controls as much as Claude. Also relied on prose gates rather than asking Claude to use plan mode, leaving that guardrail unused.

## Bottom line

A well-reasoned, non-duplicative spec build whose substance is strong — the architecture is resilient and the human's steering was the sharpest thing in the room — but it is, through the economy lens, an *over-powered and under-tooled* session: a frontier model spent on read-and-write-Markdown work, with plan mode (the one feature purpose-built for review-gated multi-phase work) never reached for, and a commit-before-review slip that better sequencing would have eliminated along with its three-turn cleanup. The right capabilities were available; the session left the most relevant leverage on the table.
