## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** Tool selection was right-sized throughout: `Read`/`Grep`/one-shot `python3` for investigation, `Edit` for surgical changes, `make` targets for verification at the correct strictness tier. It proved the core invariant (snapshot ⊂ reference, identical) *before* deleting anything, which is the difference between a confident fold and a hopeful one. The reference-triage discipline (heapdump red herrings, historical-vs-live narrative) avoided a class of churn. Recovery from the user interrupt was clean — it re-grounded on `git status` and a fresh grep rather than assuming its pre-interrupt state.

**Claude — didn't.** Two economy gaps. First, no plan mode on a spec-touching change, which left the T2 work as an unresumable mid-sweep when the session stalled. Second, serialized edits across independent files where batching was available, plus an eleven-turn Read/Edit grind on T2 string-propagation that a single careful multi-edit pass (or a tighter loop) would have compressed.

**Human — worked.** Clean, surgical task framing ("Tackle L10 — fold drift onto cli-reference.json", "close T2 as a no-longer wanted feature") gave Claude unambiguous scope. Per-commit approval was correctly granular. Priming via `/prime-context` and closing via `/capture-session` shows fluent use of the project's own purpose-built slash commands/skills — the leverage was used, not left on the table.

**Human — didn't.** The flagship model ran end-to-end on what is mostly mechanical bookkeeping; routing this to Sonnet (or at least the T2 propagation) would have matched cost to difficulty. And the 8-minute stall suggests the human waited a long time before interrupting — a tighter feedback loop, or asking for plan mode on the spec edits, would have caught the hang sooner.

## Bottom line

A small, correctly-scoped maintenance session executed with sound tool discipline: investigate-before-edit, verify at the right tier, triage references rather than blindly replace. The economy faults are not in *which* tools were used but in *sequencing and fit* — no plan-mode gate on a spec-touching change (which turned into an interrupt-and-resume), serialized edits where batching was free, and the flagship model spent on find-and-replace bookkeeping that a cheaper model handles. Solid practitioner work; not yet maximally economical.
