## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human author.** Strength: the heavy alignment investment lives *upstream*, in the spec, and it paid off here — a two-word kickoff produced a correctly-scoped build because IMPLEMENTATION.md §3, the catalog, and design §7 all pinned the "what." Weakness: two under-specifications that an earlier word would have pre-empted — the venv environment (surfaced only after a failed command and an interrupt) and the standing absence of any pre-build check on the lab's *content* (the human only saw the chosen feature after it was committed). Neither caused damage, but both were avoidable with a sentence up front.

**Claude.** Strength: disciplined resolution of apparent-but-not-real ambiguity by reading the spec rather than asking; faithful adherence to the per-change commit/push gate; and a genuinely well-judged question at the one place intent was actually undetermined (the tag-parity asymmetry). It also elicited "done" from the spec's testable criteria and proved them. Weakness: the single substantive design choice it owned — the U5 feature itself — was made silently. It was a *good* choice, well-fitted to the codebase, so this is a soft mark, not a real failure; but a learner-facing exercise is significant enough that a one-line confirmation before building would have been the more aligned move, especially since the rest of the session was so careful about gates.
