## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The hard tooling was excellent: the U9 behavior-equivalence verifier is a genuinely sophisticated, fit-to-purpose instrument, adversarially validated. Search was lean and batched; the grep-miscount self-correction (line 5072) shows it doesn't trust a single tool's output on a load-bearing claim. Verification discipline (fail-on-start *and* pass-on-solution, `make check` cadence) was consistent. The `AskUserQuestion` at the scope fork was the right light tool at the right moment, and the open-loops audit was thorough — it found three real stale claims about legacy-lab SEEDED tracking and reconciled them rather than papering over.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Everything ran on a single Opus thread with no delegation and no model tiering. The reformatting pass, the ledger audit, and the title-rename sweep are all well-bounded, lower-difficulty jobs that a subagent (to protect parent context) or a cheaper model (to save cost) would have fit better — leverage left on the table, especially given the session's length and the 18%→33% context climb. The one concrete rework — destructively testing an untracked `domain.py` then failing to `git checkout` it back (line 1455) — was a sequencing slip (commit before destructive test) that better discipline avoids.

**Human — strengths.** The readability intervention was the single most economically valuable move in the session: it caught a duplicated-narrative tax Claude had been silently paying every unit, and the author scoped it (via the answered question) to "all three" deliberately. Push/commit approvals were per-change and explicit, never standing — tight control of the expensive, irreversible operations. The "review the open loops and confirm all looks as it should" prompt (line 4842) was a well-placed audit checkpoint before moving on.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault on the economist's axis. The session stretched well past its nominal "through U10" slice into U11 and cleanup; a tighter slice boundary would have been a natural moment to start a fresh context (and is where delegating the cleanup would have helped). The author also accepted the all-Opus, all-in-one-thread mode without ever nudging toward delegation — though that's a shared omission, not a solo one.

## Bottom line

A high-competence build session whose tooling was excellent where the work was hard and merely adequate where the work was easy. Claude built a genuinely sophisticated behavior-equivalence verifier, searched leanly, verified in both directions, and audited the ledger honestly — but it ran the entire mixed-difficulty session on one Opus thread with no subagents, no plan mode, and no model tiering, leaving real context-and-cost leverage unused on the reformat/audit/rename chores. The human supplied the session's sharpest economic move (catching the duplicated-narrative tax) and kept tight, per-change control of pushes. Net: strong fluency, right-sized tools on the heavy lifting, but a panel-worthy miss on delegating the light work.
