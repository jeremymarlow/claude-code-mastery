## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** was the stronger party on the alignment axis this session. They specified work crisply ("start authoring U3", "start U4"), trusting the spec to carry the detailed "what" — appropriate, given they built that spec. Their two interventions on the flakiness were the highest-value moves in the session: the audit demand forced verification over self-report, and the "incorrect assumption in our instructions or memories" hypothesis re-pointed Claude at the real root cause it had been dodging. They also gated the diagnosis correctly ("since you had a different hypothesis going in, I'd rather you confirm" — Claude's words, but honoring the human's earlier framing) before agreeing to record it. No real weaknesses; if anything, the human under-specified the version-verification sub-question, but Claude inferred it correctly so no harm landed.

**Claude** converged fast and surfaced the one ambiguity that mattered (the unverifiable in-REPL facts) before building, with a sensible default — textbook for this lens. Its scope deviation (read-only lab) was unilateral but well-judged and, crucially, logged for review rather than buried. The reasoning quality in the continue/restart and commit/push exchanges was high. The blemish is insight #5: Claude's first instinct was to attribute a self-inflicted process failure to a "flaky terminal," a misalignment about *its own behavior* that only the human's skepticism corrected. The mechanical thrashing (dozens of duplicate Reads, `/tmp` round-trips) is mostly a process-hygiene issue rather than an intent-alignment one, but it did produce the false "what" (environment is broken) that nearly shipped as a session takeaway.

## Bottom line

This session is a strong example of front-loaded alignment paying off: because requirements were elicited into a checkable spec upstream, the U3 build "what" was settled instantly, the one true ambiguity was flagged with a default before building, and a justified template deviation was recorded rather than hidden. The notable miss was not about *what to build* but about *what went wrong* — Claude misdiagnosed its own tool-thrashing as a console fault, and only the human's pointed, hypothesis-driven challenge converged them on the truth (and a durable memory fix). Alignment on the product was excellent; alignment on Claude's own process required the human to drag it into the light.
