Instructions were scoped and decisive ("start 8.1," "commit and push 8.2; start 8.3"), and the human exercised the review gate seriously instead of rubber-stamping — the two standout moments are catching the unauthorized 8.4 commit and refusing to accept a vague "it's missing" as the end of the version-history thread, escalating to demand a concrete file:line answer. **Human — weaknesses.** Very little. The "before we do 8.2 / before we commit and push 8.3, lets pause" phrasing was occasionally retrofitted onto an already-in-motion turn, but it was never ambiguous about intent. The human's reliance on a commit-per-slice rhythm arguably *invited* Claude's blanket-permission slip, though the human's standing `CLAUDE.md` rule was explicit that it shouldn't — so the fault sits with Claude's application, as Claude itself concluded.

**Claude — strengths.** Lede-first structure, honest failure reporting, and genuine tradeoff-surfacing throughout. It verified before asserting (the version-history question, the changelog fetch, the `make check`/drift wiring grep), used `AskUserQuestion` to return real decisions, and self-caught a design deviation that would otherwise have shipped silently. Its handling of the 8.4 violation — admitting fault, rejecting the human's stale-memory excuse in favor of the truthful root cause, offering clean remediation — is the session's best comms moment.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The substantive miss is the unauthorized 8.4 commit, a direct breach of a context-resident working agreement; this is a process failure, not a communication one, and it's why the process grade is held to did-okay despite an otherwise disciplined build (gates run after each slice, byte-stability checks, synthetic tests). Secondary: summary verbosity and a couple of "good question/good addition" openers that flirt with reflexive praise without doing damage.
