# Content review — elite-practitioner
Reviewer: elite-practitioner — content sufficiency against real expert craft
Date: 2026-06-09
Scope read: all 16 rendered units + requirements §1 + workflows.md + learner docs

## Headline

This course would produce a *disciplined, verification-minded daily driver* — which is already better than almost any AI-coding material I have seen — but not an elite practitioner. The verification through-line (CV) is genuinely expert-grade and rings true everywhere; the workflows in `meta/workflows.md` match real practice. What's missing is the *operator* half of the craft: the course never teaches how to **compose a brief** (every lab hands the learner a pre-written prompt in quotes), barely teaches **context-window and session-lifecycle intuition** (one paragraph in U4, never reinforced), and is **silent on reading the model's behavior** — when to interrupt, when to steer, when to throw the session away. Those three skills are what separate someone who runs the loops correctly from someone the tool amplifies. There is also a credibility wound: four shipped units end with leaked AI tool-call tags (`</content>`/`</invoke>`), an unread-AI-output artifact in a course whose thesis is "read the diff."

## Cross-cutting findings

**F1 — Prompt/brief craft is never taught as a skill; learners execute prompts, they never author them.** Severity: high. Genuinely absent (one nugget, no habit). The only brief-craft instruction in 16 units is U2's "*specific and located*: 'Where is request authorization enforced…' beats 'explain the auth'" (`course/units/02-explore-a-codebase/unit.md:54-57`) and U13's "self-contained with a defined deliverable" for subagent briefs (`13-subagents/unit.md:150-153` — good, but scoped to delegation). Everywhere else the labs supply the exact words: U1 step 4 ("ask Claude to *'add a `service` field…as a one-line change'*", `01-…/unit.md:129-130`), U2 step 2-3, U3 step 4, U5's feature spec is fully written for you. Nothing teaches the anatomy of a reliable instruction (goal + constraints + acceptance + non-goals), how to scope down an erratic request, when to iterate vs. rewrite, what makes Claude reliable vs. erratic, or how to critique your own failed prompt. An elite operator's single highest-leverage skill is the brief; this course never has the learner write one cold and examine why it worked or didn't. The capability map (`course/progress-checklist.md`) confirms it: no can-do statement covers instruction-authoring.

**F2 — Context-window intuition and session lifecycle are one paragraph, taught once, never built into a habit.** Severity: high. Too thin. U4's entire treatment is two bullets: "More context is not better context" and "Inspect and reset it. In-REPL slash commands: /context, /compact, /clear … clearing it is often the fastest fix for 'Claude seems confused'" (`04-memory-and-context/unit.md:78-88`). That's a mention, not a skill. Missing: what actually fills the window in practice (tool outputs, fat test logs, long diffs — the things a senior engineer doesn't anticipate); how degradation *manifests* (forgetting an earlier constraint, re-reading files, contradicting its own plan); compact vs. clear vs. fresh-session as a decision, and what `/compact` loses; auto-compaction; `--resume`/`--continue` (never mentioned anywhere); when in a long task to deliberately checkpoint context. No later unit re-exercises the lever — U9's 40-minute legacy refactor is exactly where a session bloats, and the unit says nothing about managing context across the increments. The lab proves `CLAUDE.md` loads; it never makes the learner *feel* a degraded session and recover it.

**F3 — Reading the model's behavior, interrupting, and recovering from a bad turn: absent.** Severity: high. No unit teaches the signals a session has gone off the rails, when to hit Esc mid-tool-loop, steer-vs-restart judgment, or the recovery sequence after a bad accepted edit. `/rewind` appears only as an inline token aside — "In-REPL `/rewind` checkpoints/restores recent changes" (`03-operate-safely/unit.md:107`, repeated at `16-…/unit.md:97`) — and U3 promises "The debugging unit leans on this when a fix attempt makes things worse," but U7 never mentions rewind or any recovery move at all (I checked; the word does not occur in `07-debug-a-failure/unit.md`). The only real recovery teaching in the whole course is a supporting doc: `course/stuck.md:36-37` "if a session has gone sideways… a fresh context often beats untangling a confused one." That's the right instinct, buried in a FAQ instead of drilled in a unit. Elite operators interrupt early and often; a learner of this course will sit and watch a doomed turn finish.

**F4 — Leaked AI tool-call tags shipped in four learner-facing units.** Severity: high (trivially fixable, but it's a credibility wound). `course/units/01-onboarding-first-win/unit.md:173-174` ends with literal `</content>` and `</invoke>` lines; same artifact in `02-explore-a-codebase/unit.md:157`, `03-operate-safely/unit.md:215-216`, `05-ship-a-feature/unit.md:190-191`, and in their `unit.src.md` sources (e.g. `01-…/unit.src.md:172-173`). These are the closing tags of the Write tool call that authored the file — pasted in unread. For a course whose first lesson is "read the diff before accepting," this is the exact failure mode it warns about, shipped. None of the `tools/check-*` suite caught it.

**F5 — Version-data token injection produces visible prose seams, including one ~70-word blob pasted verbatim three times in one unit.** Severity: med. The `{{vd:key}}` discipline is sound, but the rendered grafts read like data dumps mid-sentence: "The currently available modes are acceptEdits, auto, bypassPermissions, default, dontAsk, plan (`--permission-mode <mode>`) — the exact set is version-specific" (`03-…/unit.md:59-61`); "Inspect and reset it. In-REPL slash commands: /context, /compact, /clear (inspect and manage the context window). You can see what's…" (`04-…/unit.md:85`); U2:53 states the same @-mention fact twice in adjacent clauses. Worst case: U12 injects the identical commands-syntax paragraph ("Custom slash commands are user-defined skills… Gotcha: `$1` is the SECOND arg…") verbatim at lines 65, 97, and 217 of `12-commands-and-skills/unit.md`. An expert reader spots the template seams immediately, and repetition at that length reads as filler, not reinforcement.

**F6 — Cost, latency, and model-choice intuition is nearly absent.** Severity: med. The sum total: extended thinking "costs latency and tokens" (`05-…/unit.md:89-91`) and `--max-budget-usd` in U16. Nothing on model selection at all — no `/model`, no Opus-vs-Sonnet-vs-Haiku tradeoff, no "use a cheaper/faster model for the explorer subagent" (U13 mentions agents can set "a model" in passing at line 90 and never returns to why). Nothing on token economics of delegation (a subagent re-reads the repo), nothing on when the tool is the wrong choice *economically* (a 30-second manual edit vs. a 3-minute agent run). Credit where due: the course is unusually good at workflow-level "when NOT to" (TDD `06:94-97`, spec-driven `10:103-106`, hooks-vs-commands `14:99-110`) — but the tool-level and dollars/minutes judgment an elite practitioner runs constantly is missing.

**F7 — Working across multiple sessions on one piece of work is dogfooded brilliantly and never taught.** Severity: med. The repo itself is the best multi-session exemplar I've seen — `IMPLEMENTATION.md` §3 re-read every fresh session, the open-loops ledger, the `prime-context` skill (shown as U12's worked example, `12-…/unit.md:105-114`) — but no unit transfers the skill: how to end a session so the next can resume (handoff notes, state-in-files vs. relying on conversation persistence), when to split work across sessions deliberately, how memory + spec files function as cross-session anchors. The learner sees the machinery as a *specimen* but is never asked to build or run one. The capstone will force exactly this skill, untaught.

**F8 — U16 dodges the question every practitioner actually asks about unattended runs: how does headless Claude get permission to act?** Severity: med. The unit says CI "is **unattended**: there's no one to answer a permission prompt, so what runs must be safe to run without one (see §5)" (`16-automate-and-scale/unit.md:73-74`) — and §5 lists hooks, budget caps, and per-diff review but never says *how a headless run that needs to edit files is authorized* (allowlisted tools in settings? `--dangerously-skip-permissions` inside a throwaway container — the sanctioned use U3 itself names?). The lab's headless task is read-only, so the question never surfaces. A learner finishing U16 cannot actually ship the "draft a fix in CI" pattern the unit advertises.

**F9 — Thin or missing advanced surfaces an elite user touches weekly.** Severity: low-med. Permission *rule authoring* is deferred from U3 to U4 ("Where the gate's allow/deny rules live… is the Memory & context unit's subject," `03:78-79`), but U4 only describes the shipped baseline (`04:90-97`) — the learner never writes or evolves an allow rule. Never mentioned anywhere: `--resume`/`--continue`; CLAUDE.md `@`-imports / nested per-directory memory; thinking-effort beyond a one-line aside. These are awareness-tier at worst, but right now they're zero-tier.

**F10 — Credit: the verification spine and the lab-design honesty are expert-grade.** The CV thread is the real thing, taught with escalating teeth: red-for-the-right-reason (U6), the three-site duplicated bug (U7), "preserve the bug" characterization discipline (U9 — most courses get this wrong), planted false-positive triage with dismiss-with-a-reason (U11's E712 example, `11:91-94`, is exactly right), "least-privilege means the tools the job needs, not always read-only" (`13:170-177`), and the synthetic-payload hook proof (`14:183-191`). The injection lab (U3) with a structural-defense framing ("model judgment is a *second* line of defense") is more honest than most vendor security material. `meta/workflows.md` would survive review by any senior practitioner.

## Top 5 enhancement recommendations

1. **Add a brief-craft thread with a deliberate fade.** In U5, replace the pre-written feature spec walkthrough with: learner writes the brief cold → compares against a provided good-vs-bad brief pair (annotated: goal/constraints/acceptance/non-goals) → re-runs. Add a "anatomy of a brief" box to `meta/workflows.md` that W1/W2/W7 reference, and a can-do for it. By U9+, labs should hand the learner *the situation*, never the prompt.
2. **Expand U4 with a "session lifecycle" section and re-exercise it in U9.** Teach: what fills the window (tool output, logs, diffs), the degradation signals, a compact/clear/new-session decision table, `--resume`/`--continue`, and what compaction loses. Then add one explicit step to U9's lab ("at increment 3, inspect context; decide compact vs. clear vs. fresh session and say why") so the habit is built, not mentioned.
3. **Add a "when it goes sideways" recovery playbook as unit content (likely U7 or a U3 section), not just `stuck.md`.** Interrupt (Esc) signals — repeated file re-reads, plan drift, fix-on-fix; the ladder: interrupt → restate constraints → `/rewind` → `git restore` → fresh session; and "steer vs. start over" heuristics. U7's lab is the natural place to make the learner practice one deliberate interrupt-and-redirect.
4. **Strip the leaked tool-call tags and add a renderer/check guard.** Remove `</content>`/`</invoke>` from units 01/02/03/05 (`unit.src.md` + `unit.md`) and teach `tools/check-*` (or the render step) to fail on stray XMLish tags outside code fences — the course's own defense-in-depth thesis applied to its authoring pipeline.
5. **Smooth the vd-token grafts and dedupe U12.** Render tokens into sentences (one human pass per unit reading aloud), and collapse U12's three identical syntax blobs to one canonical placement plus short references. Same pass should fix U2:53's doubled sentence and U12:28's stray blank bullet line.

## Out-of-scope flags

These are missing *outcomes* (no can-do/coverage promise exists), not thin teaching of promised ones — they feed the taxonomy decision:

- **Instruction/brief authoring** — no C-statement covers composing and iterating effective instructions; the capability map treats prompting as ambient rather than a capability (relates to F1, but the absence of the *promise* is the taxonomy issue).
- **Session/context lifecycle management** — no can-do covers managing long sessions, resuming, or multi-session work on one deliverable (F2/F7); C5 covers memory + selection only.
- **Recovery/interruption competence** — "I can recognize a derailed session and recover it" is promised nowhere.
- **Cost/model-choice judgment** — model selection and spend/latency reasoning appear in no requirement, can-do, or coverage area I can see from the unit surface.
- **Labs/capstone (believed solid, not my subject):** noting only that U8/U10/U12–U16 are checklist-graded, so all the judgment-heavy Autonomy-stage skills rest on self-assessment — and that the capstone will implicitly demand the untaught multi-session skill (F7). Flagging, not solving.
