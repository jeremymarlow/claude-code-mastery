## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-exemplary alignment session. Claude consistently refused to build before scope was agreed, asked the right questions at the right moments (and only those), and elicited requirements as testable ACs with explicit invitations to redline. It grounded every proposal in the actual repository rather than assumptions, which is why "non-duplicative" claims (machine reference is a sibling of `version-data.yaml`, not a clone) held up. It pressure-tested the human's ideas instead of flattering them, and twice upgraded a vague human instruction into something sharper (dynamic requirement discovery; the closed-vs-open-set principle). State capture (decisions.md P8 section, IMPLEMENTATION §3, the open-loops discipline) was kept current at every gate so the convergence is durable across a context reset.

**Claude — weaknesses.** One real slip: attempting to commit the design before the human reviewed it, despite an existing CLAUDE.md agreement covering exactly that case. It was a momentary under-weighting of a known rule, not a misread of intent, and was corrected immediately — but it is the session's one place where Claude moved past a gate that wasn't settled. Minor secondary note: the design draft initially proposed the brittle hardcoded-R16 traceability bump, which the human's resilience steering then had to correct; a more forward-looking first draft might have reached for dynamic discovery unprompted.

**Human — strengths.** The human ran this collaboration extremely well from an alignment standpoint. The "clarify before answering" move unlocked the best architectural idea in the session. The anti-shoehorn steering and the "resilient process" framing were high-leverage abstractions that shaped the design correctly. The decision to push the review-before-commit rule into the repo (not machine memory) shows mature systemic thinking. Approvals were explicit and per-gate ("requirements approved", "design approved", "approved tasks list"), and the human used Claude's redline invitations to actually redline (refusing the shoehorned feature).

**Human — weaknesses.** Few, and all minor. The initial feature ask was under-specified on placement/dogfood/docs — though that is arguably appropriate delegation, and Claude surfaced the gaps, so it converged fast. The R17 ask arrived mid-stream ("I would like to add a requirement to check and add the claude clode cli online version change synopsis…"), expanding scope after the design was already being framed; the human did pause the design correctly to absorb it, so no harm, but a fuller upfront requirement set would have avoided the design having to be re-scoped to cover R16+R17 together.

## Bottom line

A textbook spec-driven alignment session: ambiguity was surfaced before any building, the right questions were asked at the right moments (and over-asking was avoided), requirements were elicited as concrete testable ACs with genuine redline opportunities, and scope was explicitly agreed at each of three gates. The human was an unusually strong collaborator — clarifying, steering toward resilience, and elevating corrections into durable repo invariants — and Claude matched that with grounded, non-flattering proposals and meticulous state capture. The single blemish, an attempt to commit the design before review, was a known-rule slip cleanly owned and corrected, and the human's response turned it into a permanent safeguard. Convergence was fast and the "what" was settled before the "how" every time.
