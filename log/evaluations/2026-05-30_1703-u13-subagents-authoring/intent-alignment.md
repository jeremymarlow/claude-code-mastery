## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Convergence on "what to build" was consistently fast and grounded: it read the spec for each unit's requirements instead of assuming, framed every next step as confirm-or-redirect, and the requirements it worked against were already concrete and testable (can-do IDs, coverage areas, EARS ACs), so misalignment had little room to creep in. Its single best moment was the U15 fork: it recognized a decision the spec didn't resolve, judged it non-inferable, and asked a genuinely well-built question — distinct options, honest tradeoffs, no false binary — at precisely the right time. It also avoided over-asking elsewhere (the redundant `/close-unit`, the empty-arg case) by inferring obvious intent. The branch-naming turn showed it resisting a plausible-but-wrong default in favor of checking what the project actually did.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The elicitation bar was applied unevenly. The U14 hook landed in the *committed* project settings file on a silent project-vs-local assumption, against the loaded skill's own explicit instruction to ask about settings-file scope — the kind of choice that, by the standard Claude itself set in U15, deserved a question. The lab-verification shape for U13/U14 was likewise resolved by inference rather than surfaced. None of these caused a wrong build (the dogfood was pre-agreed, the tree stayed reviewable), but they reveal that "when to ask" was governed by Claude's interest in the fork more than a consistent rule.

**Human — strengths.** Communication was crisp and decisive: short, unambiguous directives ("go ahead with U13", "commit and push, then start U15") that gave Claude exactly enough to proceed, and a willingness to engage substantively when a real "what" was open — the branch-naming question was well-posed, and the "we're still technically in the tasks phase, right?" reframe was the correct insight that resolved it. The human also answered the U15 AskUserQuestion immediately and didn't second-guess it.

**Human — weaknesses.** Mostly process-by-omission. The human never set an explicit standing expectation about *which* per-unit decisions Claude should elevate to a question versus infer (lab shape, settings scope), which left Claude to improvise that boundary — and accepted the U14 committed-settings mutation without comment, implicitly ratifying a silent assumption that touched the operator's live config. A one-line standing instruction ("flag before you mutate committed config or pick a lab-verification shape") would have closed the seam.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning, spec-anchored collaboration where the "what" was settled before building at every unit boundary and convergence was near-instant — because the spec made requirements concrete and Claude consulted it rather than guessing. The session's signature is a single, well-judged AskUserQuestion at U15 that shows Claude knows how to surface a genuine fork at the right moment. The one blemish is the mirror image: U14 wired a real hook into committed settings on a silent assumption that, by Claude's own U15 standard, merited a question — a consistency gap in *when* ambiguity gets elevated rather than a wrong build. Both parties communicated cleanly throughout; the only thing that would have made the alignment airtight is a stable, shared rule for which per-unit decisions are Claude's to infer and which are the human's to call.
