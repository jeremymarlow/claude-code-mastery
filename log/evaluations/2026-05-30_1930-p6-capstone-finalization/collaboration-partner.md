## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude.** This was a strong showing on every axis the partnership lens cares about. Autonomy was calibrated to the work, not to anxiety: it took initiative across the deterministic middle of P6 and reserved its one question for the one place taste mattered, framing the question with a concrete preview so the human could decide in seconds. The correction loop was frictionless — one rejection, instantly absorbed, never repeated. Trust was actively *earned* mid-session through the checklist-generator call: discovering a latent false claim, fixing it durably, wiring in enforcement, and disclosing it prominently is precisely the behavior that lets a human keep delegating tersely. Communication was crisp throughout — each step announced what it was doing and why, the final summary was a scannable table plus the non-blocking remainder (L1) and the explicit commit/push ask. The PR explanation was honest about environment reality rather than reflexively GitHub-shaped. If I'm hunting for weakness: the self-inflicted hook trips (the `{{vd:key}}` literal, the forward link) were predictable from prior phases — a partner with full recall might have pre-empted both rather than discovering them via the hook — but each cost only a quick edit and Claude explicitly recognized them as known patterns, so this is a minor efficiency nit, not a momentum problem.

**Human.** The human delegated like someone who has earned the right to be terse — short, high-leverage instructions, trusting the standing agreements to hold the line. That trust was well-placed and the session moved fast because of it. The one substantive intervention (the venv correction) was precise and immediately actionable. The "explain the PR mechanism rather than just do it" phrasing was a nice piece of pedagogical steering — it kept the outward-facing action in human hands while extracting the knowledge. The communication grade is did-okay rather than did-well only because the delegations are so compressed that they offload nearly all judgment to Claude and the written memory; this is efficient with a trusted, aligned partner, but it provides little in-session steering signal, and "commit this and push" granted both in one breath where the CLAUDE.md model is per-action approval — Claude correctly treated the explicit "push" as sufficient, but the human's phrasing collapses a boundary the agreements otherwise keep distinct.

## Bottom line

Claude ran a multi-step closeout autonomously, stopped at exactly the right two boundaries (one subjective fork, one outward-facing action), absorbed its single correction without friction, and — the standout — turned an off-plan discovery of a false "generated" claim into real, enforced tooling rather than a cosmetic patch.
