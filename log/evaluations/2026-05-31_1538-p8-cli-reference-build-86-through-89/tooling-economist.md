## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** When the environment cooperated, Claude's tool selection was genuinely good: light tools for light jobs (`grep`/`Grep` to locate and audit, surgical `Edit`s, `git diff` to confirm the real change before claiming it), and a self-authored heredoc to independently verify the 8.7 discovery rather than trusting the check it had just modified. The 8.7 engineering — dynamic requirement discovery, the broadened reference regex, the circularity guard excluding the check's own comments, and the audit that correctly spared the W1–W9 range — is the kind of capability-to-potential work the heavy model earns its keep on. State-sync at 8.9 was methodical and file-grounded.

**Claude — didn't.** Economy was the casualty. A duplicate-call loop ate ~40 opening turns; a ~30-call parallel batch with a `git push` welded into it triggered an API error and a cascade of hundreds of cancelled calls; three Edits failed because Claude acted on a fabricated file image instead of re-Reading; and the `echo .` workaround, while inventive, spent turns rather than waiting one out. Several of these are environmental in origin, but the model's batching and read-before-edit discipline repeatedly amplified rather than dampened the flakiness — the opposite of what the project memory explicitly warns about.

**Human — worked.** The human was the session's stabilizing force: catching the failed edits and naming the better tool, flagging the API error, and gating every commit/push per-change exactly as the working agreement demands. Communication was terse and unambiguous ("push only").

**Human — didn't.** Little to fault. One could argue the human tolerated a lot of wasted-turn churn before intervening on the duplicate-call disease (it ran unchecked through the whole opening), but the human had limited visibility into an environmental console bug, and intervened decisively the moment the failures became substantive (failed edits, API error).

## Bottom line

Two sessions in one transcript: an environmental console malfunction (duplicate-batch lag, an API cascade) that wrecked turn economy, and — underneath it — a competent, correctly-tool-sized P8 build that landed five slices with the standout being a genuinely well-reasoned dynamic-requirement-discovery change. The fabrication-then-failed-edits incident is the avoidable part: read-before-edit discipline and smaller batches would have spared the rework and the human's two corrections. Claude was candid and self-diagnosing about all of it, which counts, but candor after the fact is not the same as economy in the moment. The human's crisp tool nudges and clean gating are what kept a flaky session productive.
