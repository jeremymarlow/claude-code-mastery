1. **`AskUserQuestion` was the right tool, reached for at exactly the right moment, and used to its potential** — The spec explicitly gates Design content on two undecided inputs (Q1 domain, Q2 catalog), and Claude honored that gate by surfacing the decision through the structured `AskUserQuestion` tool rather than a wall of prose. The question payload carried `preview` ASCII mockups of each codebase layout (`PRIMARY taskflow-api/ routers/ models/...`), which let the human decide on concrete artifacts instead of abstractions. This is the single best capability call of the session: a genuine decision point, routed to the purpose-built decision tool, with enough fidelity to make the choice cheap for the human. _Evidence:_ turn "**→ AskUserQuestion**" with the three domain options and their `preview` blocks. 2. **The status-marker churn across four files is the session's biggest economy leak** — Claude repeatedly hand-edited the same status lines in `design.md`, `README.md`, `IMPLEMENTATION.md`, `tasks.md`, and `decisions.md` — first to "in progress," then to "authored," then to "approved/planned." That's the same fact ("where is the build") restated in 4–5 places and re-edited at each phase transition, producing a long tail of one-line `Edit` calls (e.g., the §1/§2/§4 header-tagging edits at lines 684–739, then the README/IMPLEMENTATION status flips at 826–896, then again at 1166–1192). The CLAUDE.md working agreement itself warns against exactly this ("Don't let parallel lists drift; point them at the ledger") — but the convention that would have *prevented* the leak (a single open-loops ledger / single status source) didn't exist yet this early. So Claude paid full manual cost to keep five mirrors in sync. A batched multi-file edit, or better, a single canonical status line referenced elsewhere, would have collapsed a dozen turns into two or three. _Evidence:_ the serialized chain of status-line `Edit` calls at transcript lines ~810–896 and ~1146–1192. ## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked:** Capability selection at the one moment that mattered was excellent — `AskUserQuestion` with preview mockups turned an abstract gate into a concrete, cheap decision. Verification was right-sized: one version check, one end-of-phase consistency read, two targeted fixes. The communication was genuinely strong — every gate was named explicitly, every judgment call ("three judgment calls I want you to confirm," the U14-fold-in option) was surfaced with its trade-off, and the closing summaries were accurate and scannable. Claude never guessed a gated input, and it kept the git commit batched per the human's instruction.

**Claude — didn't:** The tool *economy* lagged the tool *selection*. The session is dominated by serialized single-line `Edit` calls maintaining five parallel status mirrors and section-header tags — independent edits that should have been batched, and redundant bookkeeping that better tooling (a single status source, an open-loops ledger) would have eliminated. No plan-mode or todo structure framed a session that clearly warranted it. And the most expensive model in the lineup spent a meaningful share of its turns on clerical Markdown edits.

**Human — worked:** Decisive, low-overhead steering; the high-value curriculum judgments (unblock the unit count, pull security forward) were exactly the inputs to spend human turns on; the "one commit at session end" instruction was a clean economy call.

**Human — didn't:** Picked the "review the design" path then signed off "all fine" without exercising it — the review gate's whole point is to catch a wrong design cheaply before tasks compound on it, and that value was left partly on the table. Communication was terse to the point of giving Claude little to verify the design against.

## Bottom line

A clean, well-gated spec-and-design session whose *capability selection* was sharp — `AskUserQuestion` at the right gate, version verified against the installed CLI, one economical consistency pass — but whose *turn economy* was loose: a long tail of serialized, single-line status-marker and header edits maintaining five parallel mirrors of "where the build is," on the most expensive model, with no plan-mode or todo scaffolding to structure a multi-gate, multi-phase arc. The right tools were reached for at the decision points; the cheaper, batched, single-source-of-truth habits that would have made the bookkeeping economical were not — partly because the conventions that later prevent exactly this churn didn't exist yet this early in the build.
