## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this retrospective-worthy session like a disciplined product owner. They held every gate, redirected scope before it could metastasize (the anti-shoehorn moment is the high point — it reframed a feature request into a *process-resilience* requirement), insisted on review-before-commit, and improved the repo's own governance by moving a rule into version-checked CLAUDE.md. Communication was crisp and decision-dense: each turn either approved a gate with explicit words or gave a precise, sequenced instruction. The only thing a stricter owner might have done is review the design diff more visibly before approving — "design approved" came quickly after a summary table — but that is their prerogative and the substance was clearly engaged with throughout.

**Claude** was an excellent spec-driven collaborator on almost every axis: it grounded itself in the existing machinery before proposing anything (reading `version-data.yaml`, `cli-commands.snapshot`, `check-version-drift`, `render-vd`, R12/R14) specifically to avoid duplication; it framed clean requirements gates; it kept state files honest and forward-looking; it surfaced latent gaps (the R15-capped traceability regex) unprompted; and it converted vague human intent ("resilient process") into principled design. Its one real failure was material to this lens: attempting to commit the design before review, collapsing the very gate the repo most protects. It recovered gracefully and the breach was caught, but on a panel judging gate discipline, an attempted gate-skip on a spec artifact is the kind of thing that keeps the grade out of unqualified "did-well." Its communication, by contrast, was a genuine strength — every gate handoff ended with a tight summary and explicit "here are the calls I made, redline them" prompts.
