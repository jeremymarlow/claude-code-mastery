## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The dominant story is excellent ambiguity management. Claude reliably distinguished what it could infer from what it could not, and reserved questions for genuine forks rather than over-asking. The opening four-question gate, the three cross-cutting forks, and the per-requirement why/what-breaks/alternatives format together turned a vague aspiration into a traceable, testable spec with gap-free IDs. Its communication was consistently decision-ready: every option carried a consequence and a stated lean, and it flagged the one judgment call per requirement it most wanted the human to weigh in on. The handoff reasoning ("zero memory of this conversation") shows real alignment maturity — it recognized that intent captured only in chat is intent lost.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two process lapses. First, the opening batch that wrote a full `requirements.md` *simultaneously* with asking the questions that should have shaped it — building before the "what" was settled, narrowly saved only because the batch errored. Second, the ladder/elite framing was a silent assumption carried deep into the requirements before the human caught it; a single "how do we frame progress to the learner?" question up front would have pre-empted a multi-file rework. Both are the same underlying tendency: a bias toward producing artifacts slightly ahead of confirmed agreement. (Also minor: repeated wasted/duplicate Reads and a couple of glob-induced batch cancellations — execution noise, not alignment.)

**Human — strengths.** The user was a model collaborator for convergence: high-level vision stated once, then crisp, itemized keep/change/cut decisions; genuine independent steering on the decisions that mattered (outcome-driven, version-resilience-not-pinning, capstone-only); and the alienation insight on ranking labels was a sharp, values-driven catch Claude had missed. Critically, the user calibrated process depth deliberately (one-by-one over batching) and reframed the end goal toward durability rather than letting the session just stop.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very few. The original prompt embedded "elite practitioner" framing without signalling sensitivity to learner-facing ranking, which seeded the ladder assumption the user later had to unwind — a small under-specification of the persona's emotional/tone requirements that an opening note could have prevented. The pinned version "2.1.157" in the original ask also briefly misdirected Claude toward version-pinning until the user clarified intent ("the reason I included the version was to highlight... the most recent version") — a case where the human's phrasing carried an unintended requirement.

## Bottom line

This was a high-functioning alignment session: a deliberately under-specified vision converged, over many rounds, into a rigorous, testable, internally consistent spec, with the human and Claude trading the "what" cleanly and the human steering the few decisions that genuinely needed steering. Claude's ambiguity instincts were strong — it asked at the right moments and answered with decision-ready structure — and its handoff reasoning was a standout. The two real blemishes both point the same way: a tendency to author permanent artifacts a half-step ahead of confirmed agreement (the build-while-asking opening batch; the unconfirmed ladder framing that triggered a rework). Neither derailed the outcome, and the human's sharp catch on ranking labels plus Claude's fast, clean propagation kept convergence on track. Net: alignment was caught early far more often than it was missed.
