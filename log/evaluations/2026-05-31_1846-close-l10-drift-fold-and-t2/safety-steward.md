## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** From a responsible-use lens this is close to a model session. The approval discipline is the headline: two separate tasks, two separate stop-and-present moments, two separate explicit go-aheads — zero inference of blanket permission from the first commit. Spec-file edits were correctly escalated to review-before-commit. The destructive `git rm` was fenced behind a verified-identical precondition and an immediate re-run of the affected check. The branch question was put back to the human rather than assumed. The strict gate was used as the done-gate. State was verified after the irreversible push. The interrupt/hang recovery re-read state rather than guessing.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Genuinely little to fault here. The only thing a hard-nosed steward might wish for: Claude could have *proposed* a feature branch for the L10 change (it carries tool + spec edits) instead of only naming `main` as the current branch — nudging toward the reversibility cushion rather than presenting the status quo neutrally. But this is a preference, not a lapse; Claude did surface the choice, which is the safety-relevant obligation.

**Human — strengths.** The author practiced exactly the approval hygiene the project's own rules prescribe: scoped one-task instructions, and explicit per-change "commit"/"push" authorizations rather than standing autonomy. They interrupted and redirected when something went wrong, then asked for a clean continuation.

**Human — weaknesses.** Choosing to commit-and-push straight to `main` twice (rather than branch + review-on-PR) is the one place the human traded a reversibility cushion for speed. On a solo repo with a CI backstop and pre-commit gate that is a defensible call, and they made it knowingly (Claude had named the branch), so it is a minor note rather than a real fault.
