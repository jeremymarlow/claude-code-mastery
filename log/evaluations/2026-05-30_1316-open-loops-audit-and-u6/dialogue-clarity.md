## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session the way you'd want a senior engineer to run a build collaboration: short, scoped, decisive instructions with no contradictions, and a real willingness to interrupt and correct in the moment. The three tool-hygiene corrections were the standout — each precise, each teaching rather than scolding, and the human let the rejection mechanism do the work cleanly. Push approvals were granted per-step, never as a blanket, which both respected the project's own rules and kept Claude honest. The one open-ended question ("fresh context?") was well-timed and extracted Claude's best reasoning. There is essentially nothing to fault on the human's side here; if anything, the corrections could have been front-loaded into a standing instruction earlier, but catching them live worked fine and produced a durable memory artifact.

**Claude** communicated honestly and structured most replies lede-first. Its audit was genuinely excellent: it verified rather than assumed, distinguished real constraints from excuses, surfaced an unprompted actionable, and handed the decision back rather than running with it. Its failure-reporting was exemplary — red-then-green shown, not claimed; its own two self-inflicted bugs named without spin. The fresh-context advice refused the easy agreement and led with the inconvenient truth. The weaknesses are real but second-order: it reached for shell `sed`/`awk` to *read* files three times before the lesson stuck (a process lapse the human had to police), and its end-of-unit summaries, while accurate and complete, run long enough that the decision-relevant line occasionally gets buried under correctly-reported detail. Neither cost the human anything material, but both are the difference between did-well and flawless on the communication axis.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning, low-friction collaboration. The human gave clear, scoped, decisive instructions and corrected tool-hygiene slips with surgical precision; Claude answered lede-first, surfaced tradeoffs (the doc-verification option, the fresh-context tradeoff, the D2/D3 caveat), reported red-then-green and its own bugs honestly, and respected per-step push gating throughout. The through-line is candor without sycophancy on both sides. The only real drag was Claude's repeated shell-for-reading habit (policed, then internalized into memory) and somewhat over-long wrap-ups whose decision-relevant lines sometimes trail the detail.
