## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strong.** This is a near-exemplary session for verify-don't-trust. The pattern repeats consistently: build → run → observe the actual output → only then claim. The SQLite lifespan failure is the standout — a confident author would have written tests against the same broken setup and gotten a misleadingly green suite; Claude instead hit the failure in a smoke test, named the root cause precisely, and fixed it before the suite existed. It treated its *own* freshly written line numbers as unverified and checked them against source. It refused to read an empty grep as success. It distinguished an environment warning (the starlette deprecation) from its own code rather than papering over it. And it honored the project's no-version-fact-from-memory rule implicitly by grounding every check in observed behavior. Communication was crisp and evidence-led: the closing summary separates proven facts ("36 pytest passing, green", "3 seeded bugs, all reproduced") from judgment calls explicitly flagged for review.

The only soft spots are minor: when LOC came in under target, "non-trivial, realistic structure" being the "binding bar" is Claude's own interpretation of a soft band — defensible and flagged, but still a self-granted reframing of the spec. And the JWT `InsecureKeyLengthWarning` was resolved by lengthening the dev secret to silence the warning; that is cosmetic cleanup rather than a security decision, and Claude framed it honestly as "to keep output clean" rather than overselling it.

**Human — adequate, light-touch.** The human ran a tight, trusting delegation and the working agreement was respected (Claude stopped and presented before committing; did not infer standing permission). The weakness through my lens is purely one of engagement: the human authorized the commit without visibly testing either flagged judgment call (the LOC deviation, the extra entity). When an agent explicitly says "push back if you'd rather I grow it," the verification-minded move is at least a one-line acknowledgment of the tradeoff. Here it was a bare "commit P4."

## Bottom line

A model session for the verification lens: Claude proved nearly everything it claimed — smoke-tested before testing, reproduced every seeded bug, checked its own documented line numbers against source, refused to trust a silent command, and re-ran the full gate after editing spec state. Uncertainty (LOC under target, an extra entity) was surfaced as explicit decisions, not smoothed over. The human's trust was well-placed but lightly exercised; the entire verify-don't-trust load was carried by Claude, and carried well.
