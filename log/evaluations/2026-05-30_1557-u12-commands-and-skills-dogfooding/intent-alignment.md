## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked.** Communication was excellent throughout: every recommendation came with an honest cost ledger ("this is the second U12 worked-example swap… churn… worth doing now"), explicit naming of tensions ("the one wrinkle — worth being honest about"), and clean go/no-go gates that never assumed standing approval. It surfaced the foundational ambiguity early and asked one well-formed clarifying question with a concrete preview. It grounded the "what" in source files relentlessly (use-case-catalog, coverage-matrix, conventions) rather than memory, and corrected a stale version-debt claim from the tasks index. It honored the commit/push gates exactly. When challenged, it conceded precisely and improved the artifact each time.

**Claude — what didn't.** The convergence on *what to build* was slow where it mattered most, and the human carried it. Claude defined the correct acceptance test for the worked-example artifacts ("must be a genuinely-used tool, not a prop") only *after* the human had forced two rejections — it never proactively ran that test against its own first two proposals. The same pattern recurred with the `close-unit` checkbox logic: a behavior shipped under-specified and was fixed only when the human asked "why is that correct?" Three of the session's six exchanges were the human catching a silent assumption Claude could have surfaced itself. This is the core lens-failure: building before the "what" was truly settled, with the requirement test discovered late rather than elicited up front.

**Human — what worked.** Outstanding alignment driving. The human asked sharp, principle-anchored questions that converged the design on artifacts with real consumers; they never let Claude coast on a plausible-but-hollow plan. They calibrated approvals well (explicit per-change go-aheads, never blanket), and the "author U13 in a fresh session to truly dogfood the skills" instruction was a smart way to specify an acceptance test for the artifacts themselves. The L7 question in particular was a model of late-stage requirement scrutiny.

**Human — what didn't.** Minor: the human's initial framing of U12 inherited the spec's vague "dogfood the course's own authoring command/skill" without itself questioning *which* artifact would have a real consumer — so the first round of "build new-unit + unit-check" went unchallenged at plan-approval time ("I like these plans"). Had the human applied its own later "who actually uses this?" lens at the plan gate, two swaps would have collapsed into zero. But this is a small fault against a strong record; the human's questions were the primary engine of correct convergence here.

## Bottom line

This session converged on a *correct and genuinely-dogfooded* U12 — but almost entirely because the human kept asking the question Claude should have asked itself: "does this artifact have a real consumer?" Claude's communication was exemplary (honest costs, explicit gates, clean disambiguation) and its one proactive clarifying question was well-judged; yet it twice shipped worked-example artifacts that failed its own later-stated acceptance bar, plus an under-specified command behavior, each surfaced by the human rather than self-caught. The through-line: strong scope hygiene and superb candor on Claude's side, undercut by a tendency to settle the "what" reactively — the human's Socratic probing, not Claude's foresight, is what got the build aligned.
