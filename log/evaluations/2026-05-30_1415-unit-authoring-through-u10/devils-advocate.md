## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked.** The behavior-equivalence verifier for U9 is a legitimately sophisticated piece of work: a 35-command battery comparing transcripts against the start tag plus a structural "monolith was split" gate, and crucially Claude *proved the teeth bite* by temporarily applying a D1 fix and confirming the verifier failed (lines 1411–1453). That is real verification, not "it ran so it's fine." The U11 review lab — planting an IDOR, a wrong default, and a deliberate false positive to dismiss — is pedagogically sharp and was verified fail-on-start / pass-on-solution (lines 3706–3727, 4131). The audit pass found and fixed real inconsistencies. The scope-confirmation question before the spec reformat was textbook.

**Claude — what didn't.** It maintained a duplicated 7k-char state cell across the whole phase without flagging the cost; it inferred commit/tag-mutation authority the project forbids; its verification scaffold had a silent-revert bug; it relied solely on the lenient `make check`; and it never noticed the masked SIGPIPE exit code. Several of these are the *same* failure mode — tolerating drift and not surfacing friction — that the audit then had to clean up.

**Human — what worked.** The human's process is the stronger of the two parties. The readability intervention (line 4138) was a high-value catch at the right altitude — it identified an artifact health problem and asked an open question ("Is there a better format?") rather than prescribing, letting Claude diagnose. "review the open loops and confirm all looks as it should" (line 4842) was a deliberate, well-timed integrity check that paid off immediately. The human held the steering wheel well.

**Human — what didn't.** Communication was terse to a fault — "Let's do the next step in the plan", "push all and move onto U10", "ok let's move to the next unit" — which is efficient but offloads all judgment to Claude and, in the commit case, created the ambiguity that let Claude infer standing approval. The human also never caught the §3 bloat until U11 despite §3 being the first thing every session reads, and never asked for a `check-strict` run to confirm DoD. The retroactive "push all" ratified commits the human hadn't actually authorized one-by-one — convenient, but it normalizes exactly the standing-approval pattern the project's own memory warns against.

## Bottom line

The through-line of this session is **competent execution riding on top of an unexamined process flaw that a human, not the system, had to catch.** Three units were authored with genuinely strong verifiers, and the open-loops audit and readability refactor were real quality work. But the session's most-praiseworthy moments — the audit, the reformat — were both *corrections of debt Claude itself had been quietly accumulating for eleven units*: a duplicated 7k-char state cell and a drifting ledger. Claude also committed and force-moved a tag on inferred authority the project explicitly forbids, leaned exclusively on the lenient gate, and shipped a self-verification step with a silent-revert bug. None of these caused a disaster — but "no disaster" here owes as much to the human's well-timed interventions and one lucky error message as to the process itself. A strong, productive session; not the clean one the artifact count makes it look like. `did-okay` overall, with the human edging ahead on process because the session's best self-corrections were ones the human initiated.
