## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude.** This is a strong showing on my axis. The defining move — refusing to guess Q1/Q2 and converting them into concrete, previewable options — is textbook alignment: ambiguity caught at the exact right moment, with questions phrased so the human could decide in seconds. Its scope discipline was equally good: it honored both phase gates (catalog/map before dependent design; full design before tasks), it labeled its own discretionary calls ("my call") so nothing crept in silently, and it neither over-asked during mechanical authoring nor under-asked on consequential forks. The post-Design review guide deserves specific praise — distilling a large document into six confirmable judgment calls is how you make a review gate real instead of theatrical. I find no instance in this session where an earlier question would have caught a misalignment Claude missed.

**Human.** The human's strength is structural: the prior-session investment in explicit gates and a decisions ledger let a one-line "continue" run safely, and the human *used* the gates offered (choosing "review" over "approve as-is"). Direction was decisive and well-judged — loosening the unit cap and pulling security forward were substantive, content-improving calls, and delegating the onboarding split was an efficient use of Claude's judgment. The weaknesses are minor and both about *timing of communication*: the full session scope surfaced mid-stream rather than up front, and "all fine" / "Please" were thin ratifications that lean heavily on trusting Claude's framing. None of this caused rework, but a reviewer on a *busier* session would want the human's approvals to carry a bit more evidence of actual inspection.

## Bottom line

A clean, gate-disciplined convergence on *what* to build. The session's through-line is that the hard alignment work — refusing to guess the domain and catalog, turning those into concrete options, and honoring two review gates before building on them — was done deliberately and at the right moments. Claude drove that with notably well-calibrated asking-vs-assuming; the human supplied decisive direction and (crucially) an earlier scaffold of explicit gates that made a terse "continue" safe. The only soft spots are the human's late scope declaration and thin sign-offs, neither of which produced a misalignment. Both parties earn high marks for keeping the "what" settled before the "how."
