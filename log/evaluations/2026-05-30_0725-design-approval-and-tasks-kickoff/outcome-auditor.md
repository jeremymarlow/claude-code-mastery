## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The defining quality this session was that *nothing was declared done without being run*. Every artifact that could be executed was: the cross-reference script, `make check`, `make check-strict`, `doctor --no-probe`, `check-version-drift`, the pre-commit hook path, and `render-vd`. Three bugs and one prose false-token (`{{vd:key}}` literally appearing in `stuck.md`, line 2601) were caught *because* of that discipline, and all were fixed and re-verified before commit. The version-verification rule was honored substantively — a real correction to permission-modes, honest `unverified` flags on the 7 keys that couldn't be checked. Commit messages were accurate to the delivered work (I cross-checked the permission-modes and PENDING claims against the files). And the self-assessment when the human asked about continuity was candid rather than reassuring.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Genuinely little to fault on the outcome axis this session. The closest thing to a weakness is that the three tooling bugs existed at all — a more careful first draft of `_common.py` would have avoided the attribute/method name collision, which is a basic Python smell. But since they were all caught pre-commit by Claude's own verification, the *shipped* product was sound; the bugs are a process-quality footnote, not a delivered defect. The opportunistic `.gitignore` fix is mild scope-creep, disclosed and harmless.

**Human — strengths.** The author drove the session well through small, explicit gates (approve design → merge → branch → P2 → P3 → continuity → close out), and the two best moments were both probes, not commands: the continuity-tracking question that surfaced the stale `IMPLEMENTATION.md`, and the closing "any knowledge pertinent for subsequent sessions?" that prompted the memory writes. The author also corrected Claude's tooling assumption cleanly — interrupting the `python -m venv` attempt to say a venv was already active and to use `uv pip install` (line 931) — preventing a wrong-environment artifact.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very little. The author largely trusted Claude's "all green" reports without independently spot-checking a file or running a check themselves — which worked out because Claude's verification was sound, but it means the human's own outcome-auditing was light. A skeptical glance at, say, the version-data unverified flags would have been the auditor-ideal. Not a fault given the result, just the thinnest part of the human's contribution.
