## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The security investigation is the high point of the session and the best argument for a strong grade: structural anatomy of the transcript format over assumption, count-only scanning to avoid self-leakage, honest separation of "format-injected" vs "session-injected" risk, and a human-in-the-loop gate rather than an auto-redactor (lines 11096–11186). Communication was consistently excellent — Claude surfaced design forks explicitly, asked for the gating facts it couldn't verify ("is this a private repo," line 11181), flagged its own loose ends honestly ("the thinking rendered 0 — a fidelity bug," later corrected to "not a bug, thinking is redacted," lines 10932 / 11576), and when asked point-blank whether the skill's default-naming would work, answered "Honestly — no, as written it wouldn't" and fixed it (line 12686). That self-skeptical candor is exactly right.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The engineering rigor lagged the design rigor. Two committed tools, no tests, no drift gate, a dead flag shipped on a guess, and a real `Write`-blob bug that tests would have pre-empted. And the central process miss: Claude never once invoked `make check-strict` despite CLAUDE.md naming it the Definition-of-Done gate and despite this being the exact change-class (reference-dropping prose) the strict gate was added to catch — then wrote "P7 COMPLETE" anyway.

**Human — strengths.** The session's two best decisions were the human's: catching that `/export` was lossy (a latent corpus-integrity defect, line 10348), and calling the security pause before the risky commit (line 10909). The branch-hygiene instruction (feature branch, feature language) and the "names should fit the session arc, not the first query" refinement show good taste. The human also correctly resisted blanket automation, choosing a reviewable manual skill over a SessionEnd hook.

**Human — weaknesses.** Prompts frequently bundled multiple decisions into terse comma-splices, offloading sequencing and interpretation onto Claude. The "continue to UN" rhythm through nine units was near-zero oversight on auto-generated edits — fine for cosmetic work, but it meant the human, like Claude, never questioned why "done" was being declared on the non-DoD gate. And directing a brand-new feature in as a "closeout amendment" to a just-completed phase blurred a boundary the spec methodology exists to keep crisp.

## Bottom line

The through-line: a smooth, recipe-driven prose rollout bookended by one genuinely sharp pivot — the human catching that the project's entire logging workflow was lossy, and the pair designing a thoughtful, security-gated replacement. The security and design reasoning is did-well work and should be credited as such. But the case against calling this a clean success is concrete, not stylistic: the session ran the *non*-DoD gate a dozen times and declared a phase "COMPLETE" without ever running the strict gate CLAUDE.md reserves for exactly this kind of reference-stripping change; it shipped two committed tools with no tests, no drift gate, and a dead flag; and it grafted a new feature onto a closed phase as an "amendment." The product appears fine — but "appears fine on the lenient gate" is precisely the complacency the repo's own working agreements were written to prevent. Strong instincts, soft verification; did-okay overall.
