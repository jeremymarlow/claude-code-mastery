## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** The core deliverable is sound. The capability map, 16-unit catalog, nine-workflow table, and 29-area coverage matrix are mutually consistent and densely traced back to numbered acceptance criteria (R1.AC5, R3.AC2/AC4/AC6, R4.AC1/AC4, R7.AC1, R10.AC5). The "security at U3" decision propagates correctly through §2, §3, and §4 — a common place for drift, and it held. Claude re-grounded in the spec before acting, verified the live CLI version, gated on user approval twice, and ran a real consistency pass that caught its own stale prose. Status markers across `README.md`/`IMPLEMENTATION.md`/`decisions.md`/`tasks.md` were kept in sync, and the commit was correctly deferred and branched.

**Claude — didn't.** Communication is where I dock a notch, mostly for reasons partly outside Claude's control: the way the work was emitted (header-flip Edits that the renderer collapses to look like no-ops) makes the session nearly un-auditable from the render alone — a reviewer or a future maintainer skimming the transcript would not see that any design was authored. More structuring of the Edits (or a one-line "expanding §3 stub into the full workflow table" note) would have made the work product legible without diving into raw JSON. The "staged-and-ready" phrasing was also imprecise. These are presentation nits on top of strong substance.

**Human — worked.** Clean gate-keeping and decisive, rationale-bearing direction. The human ran a genuine spec-driven loop: opened the floor for Claude's draft, made targeted changes (security forward, unpin count), delegated one call explicitly, and chose to review the design (path 1) rather than auto-approving everything. The commit was authorized only when explicitly intended.

**Human — didn't.** The "review" of the design and the Phase-3 task files was light — "all fine" arrived without visible spot-reading of the artifact. Given the volume authored in one pass (design §3–§10 plus six task files), a quick independent read of even one high-stakes section (the §6 dependency graph, or the §7 codebase-size commitment Claude flagged as "the biggest build commitment") would have hardened the approval. The output happens to be sound — but the session didn't demonstrate the human confirming that.

## Bottom line

A genuinely strong outcome session: the spec/design artifacts that got produced are correct, internally consistent, and match every substantive claim made about them — I verified the workflow table, the coverage matrix, and the security-at-U3 propagation against the committed files and they hold. Claude gated properly, verified the live CLI version instead of trusting memory, and caught its own stale prose before calling it done. The one real caveat for my lens is legibility, not correctness: the rendered transcript makes the design authoring look like header-flipping, and only the raw record reveals the thousands of tokens of sound content behind those Edits. The human steered crisply and gate-kept well, but leaned on Claude's self-consistency rather than independently checking the artifact — which paid off here, but is the thin spot in an otherwise solid build.
