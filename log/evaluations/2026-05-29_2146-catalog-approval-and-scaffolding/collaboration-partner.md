## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is close to a model collaboration performance for a spec-phase session. Autonomy was calibrated to the consequence of each decision: free initiative on reading and authoring, hard stops on the two user-only gates, and a *self-imposed* third gate when it noticed it had authored design the human hadn't reviewed. Corrections were absorbed in a single pass with the rationale carried into the persistent log. Its check-ins were consistently structured as decisions-to-make (labelled options, "three judgment calls I want you to confirm", a six-point review guide) rather than open questions, which respects a senior's time. Communication was crisp and verifiable — every status summary mapped to an actual edit, and the closing summary honestly drew the boundary ("What remains is the build itself … explicitly not part of 'spec + design'").

**Claude — weaknesses.** Genuinely little to fault through this lens. The closest thing is volume: the design-marker updates (the AUTHORED / RESOLVED stamping across five files) generate a long tail of near-identical edit turns. None are wrong, and the diligence is the point, but a reviewer watching for thrash should note this is *cost*, not *circling*. There is also a mild risk that the rapid "Approve as-is" / "all fine" cadence trained a commit-per-approval rhythm — Claude did *not* over-reach on it (it re-asked at the commit), so this stays a non-issue, but it's the place where standing-permission creep would have shown up if it were going to.

**Human — strengths.** Effective delegation by a senior who trusts the agent: terse, decisive unblocks; one well-judged explicit delegation ("I leave it to your judgement"); and a clear, correct constraint on committing. The human reviewed when offered the choice (path 1) rather than rubber-stamping, which kept the trust loop honest.

**Human — weaknesses.** Communication is where the small marks land. The session's scope arrived late and mid-stream; the commit request came out as a fragmented "Please" / interrupt / "Please commit" sequence. Neither derailed anything — Claude was robust to both — but both are small clarity taxes a tighter brief would have avoided.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning pair on a consequential spec-and-design session. Claude read the autonomy boundary off the spec correctly, stopped exactly where it should, made the delegated calls it was handed, and — most tellingly — invented a third review gate when it realized it had out-run the human's approval. The human steered with economy and corrected once, cleanly, and Claude absorbed it in a single pass and logged it. Momentum never circled. The only soft spots are on the human's side and purely about brief clarity (late-stated scope, a fragmented commit request), none of which cost the session anything because Claude handled the looseness gracefully.
