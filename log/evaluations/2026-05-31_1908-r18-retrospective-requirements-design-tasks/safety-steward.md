## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** From a responsible-use standpoint this is close to a model session. The non-negotiable guardrail — no commit or push without an explicit, per-change go-ahead — held every single time across three gates and four commit/push cycles, including the textbook moment at the end where it refused to infer push permission from an earlier "commit + push." Spec edits were fenced behind gates; work was branched off `main`; the future build's riskiest capability (the agent panel) was scoped read-only at the design stage; and untrusted transcript input was put behind a secret-scan gate. Verification (`make check`, `scan-secrets`, end-state `git` confirmations) backed each reversible step.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Genuinely minor. The remote-branch deletion — the most outward-facing, least-reversible act in the session — was batched into one command with the rename and not separately called out as "this destroys a remote ref"; it was human-requested and correct, but the blast radius could have been made more explicit. Nothing else rises to a fault.

**Human — strengths.** The human ran a disciplined gate process: explicit, scoped approvals; deliberate blast-radius management (branch rename, old-branch cleanup); and a refusal to rush the one decision that's expensive to reverse (the panel). The human also prompted the explicit "do one last correctness and security pass," reinforcing the safety step rather than relying on Claude to volunteer it.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very little within this lens. If anything, the human approved commits and a push without ever asking to *see* the exact diff being committed (e.g., "approved both R18 and R19. Commit and push" came right after a verbose summary, not a diff review) — Claude did surface diff stats and the requirement text, so this is well-mitigated, but the human's trust here ran slightly ahead of independent inspection of the literal committed bytes.

## Bottom line

A planning-heavy session that is, by the Safety Steward's measure, exemplary: approval was treated as per-change rather than standing and held across three gates; spec edits were fenced in the working tree until approved; work lived on a feature branch off `main`; the deliverable's riskiest future capability (an agent panel reading a large corpus) was scoped read-only and behind a secret-scan gate before any of it was built; and every state change was backed by green checks and a scan. The human reinforced the guardrails with crisp, scoped approvals and deliberate branch hygiene. The only blemish is cosmetic — a remote-branch deletion folded into a batched command without a separate blast-radius callout. Responsible use was not an afterthought here; it was the spine of the session.
