## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude.** This is a strong showing on every axis the partnership lens cares about. Autonomy was well-calibrated: it ran hard on the mechanical authoring-and-sync work it had clear precedent for, and it raised a check-in at the one genuine fork (the MCP server build) rather than either guessing or asking about trivia. The correction loop was clean and bidirectional — when the human's terser branch-naming logic beat its own recommendation, it adopted it without defending its prior answer. It honored the per-change commit/push boundary with discipline that never lapsed despite a high-trust, fast-moving batch. And it was scrupulously honest about the limits of its verification (the hook watcher caveat, the deferred L1 keys), which is what makes its "verified" claims trustworthy. The communication was consistently well-structured: each unit-close summary led with what landed, what state synced, what stayed open, and a single clear go/no-go question. If there's a nitpick, it's that the per-unit summaries are long and somewhat templated — but given the human was clearly skimming for the go-ahead, the structure served the reader rather than padding.

**Human.** The human delegated with rare economy and let the trust compound appropriately. The instructions were terse precisely because Claude had earned them — and crucially, the human still gated every push behind an explicit word rather than handing over standing permission, which is what kept the per-change boundary meaningful. The branch-naming turn shows the human at their best as a steering partner: not accepting Claude's first recommendation, applying a sharper "we're still technically in the tasks phase" frame, and getting a better answer for it. The human under-specified almost nothing and micromanaged nothing. The only thing the human left implicit was lab-design taste (e.g., they never weighed in on prose-self-check vs. mutating labs), but Claude had solid precedent (U8/U10/U12) to lean on, so this was effective delegation rather than a gap.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning pair operating at cruising altitude: a human who delegated a four-unit batch with terse, per-change-gated authorizations, and a Claude that ran on well-judged initiative — closing the entire P5 phase in one session — while stopping at exactly the two moments that warranted it (a genuine design fork and a strategic branch decision). The correction loop ran clean in both directions, the per-change commit/push boundary never eroded under momentum, and Claude's honesty about what it could and couldn't verify kept its "done" claims trustworthy. The through-line is *earned, sustained trust*: every wide instruction was preceded by Claude stopping, presenting, and waiting — so the autonomy was a gift the human could safely keep giving.
