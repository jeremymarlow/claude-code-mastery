## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude.** This is a strong session by the verification lens — arguably exemplary. The through-line is that Claude treated "it's tracked / it's fixed / it's done" as hypotheses to be tested. The audit confirmed the baseline before trusting any recorded value, and it stress-tested the *reasons* in the ledger rather than the values alone. The lab work proved both halves of the loop (red-for-the-right-reason and green-on-solution), then went further to prove the revert and reset paths — closing the gap between "the verifier passes" and "the learner's whole loop works." Failures were diagnosed from source (the `check-version-refs` matcher) rather than guessed at, and the one false-positive signal was named as self-inflicted. Honesty about uncertainty was genuine and proactive (the WebFetch offer; the `${PIPESTATUS}` concession). The thinnest spot is that unit *prose* is content Claude authored confidently and the transcript shows no independent check of its pedagogical claims — but that is outside the mechanical-verification remit, and everything that *could* be objectively confirmed was.

**Human.** The author was a high-quality verification partner. The three interventions (two on read-tool discipline, one on `PIPESTATUS`) all sharpened either the evidence channel or tool hygiene, and the author let Claude prove each slice end-to-end before authorizing commits and pushes — honoring the per-change approval rule (separate explicit "push" for refs, then branch). The only thing I'd note is that approval to push was given without the author independently re-running `make check-strict` or the verifiers themselves; the author trusted Claude's read of the evidence. Given how thoroughly Claude surfaced that evidence (exit codes, reset confirmation, dropped pending entries), that trust was well-placed here — but it was trust extended on Claude's reporting, not the author's own re-verification.

## Bottom line

A near-textbook session for verify-don't-trust. The open-loops audit checked the ledger against live CLI and git state — and audited the *excuses* in the ledger, not just the values — while the U6/U7 lab builds proved red-for-the-right-reason, green-on-solution, *and* the revert/reset paths, reading failures from source rather than guessing. Version facts were never authored from memory; the one consumed `{{vd:}}` key was honestly left unverified with the blocker surfaced. The human author materially strengthened the verification discipline, most pointedly by catching a `PIPESTATUS` footgun before Claude could trust a misleading exit code. "Done" was, repeatedly, proven.
