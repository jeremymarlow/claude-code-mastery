## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human — worked.** This is a strong showing. The human gated work cleanly (step-by-step go-aheads, explicit "commit" then explicit "push," consistent with the repo's per-change approval rule), and — more importantly — supplied the critical thinking that the artifact's quality actually depended on. The four challenge-questions are the spine of the session; without them U12 ships a `unit-check` prop and a `new-unit` prop. The human also correctly insisted on a fresh-session dogfood run rather than accepting Claude's in-session "ready" assertion at face value. **Human — didn't.** Little to fault. One could argue the human authorized the push while a brand-new, never-executed command sat in the tree (and it turned out to be buggy) — but the human had asked Claude to "double check," reasonably relied on that QA, and then caught the bug anyway one turn later. The residual risk is real but the human's instinct ultimately covered it.

**Claude — worked.** Disciplined inputs-first sequencing (read spec, templates, conventions, schema before authoring); honest cost/caveat disclosures throughout; correct, conservative git hygiene (staged exactly the intended files, never pushed without leave); good catch on the already-verified version keys ("no L1 debt, contrary to what the tasks index implied"); and genuine continuity hygiene across many state files. When prompted, its reasoning about *why* each artifact was a prop was sharp. **Claude — didn't.** Its independent design judgment on the unit's central deliverable was weak: two consecutive dogfood artifacts failed R14's "genuinely used" test, and the human caught both. Its self-QA passed a logically self-contradictory command and pushed it. And it never actually exercised either shipped artifact, so the session's central claim — these are real, non-prop tools — remains untested at session end.

## Bottom line

The through-line: **a green, pushed, tidy-looking U12 whose central achievement — authentic dogfooded artifacts — was reached only because the human caught two props and one logic bug that Claude shipped with confidence.** Credit the human's reviewing instinct and Claude's honesty-under-questioning and state hygiene. But resist the over-generous reading that this was a smooth collaboration of equals: it was a competent author (Claude) repeatedly rescued from prop-shaped, unexamined dogfood choices by a sharp reviewer (the human), with the actual proof of the work — running the new tools — deferred to a future session. The strongest case against calling this a success is that the unit's defining requirement was satisfied by trial-and-error correction, not by skill, and the final artifacts were never run. `did-okay` overall: the outcome is probably fine, but it was earned by the human's questions, not by Claude's autonomous judgment.
