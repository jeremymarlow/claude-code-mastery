## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** Orientation-before-action was textbook: it honored the project's read-order (IMPLEMENTATION §3 → ledger → per-unit row) and reconstructed precise state from files rather than memory. It respected the "source of truth is the files, not memory" rule under pressure — leaving `search-refs`, `checkpoint-rewind`, and `managed-settings` honestly `unverified` rather than authoring version facts from recollection (R12.AC3), and catching its own fabricated `{{vd:permissions-config}}` key that would have failed `check-version-refs`. Continuity externalization was thorough and correctly prioritized (knowing which failed edits mattered and which didn't). And the post-mortem diagnosis was a model of turning a context failure into a written, indexed lesson.

**Claude — didn't.** The window filled with noise it generated itself: duplicate reads, repeated probing of mis-guessed filenames, and a `/tmp`-round-trip ritual that treated a non-existent environment problem. It then misattributed its own batching disorder to a "flaky console" and reported that to the user as fact — a context-management failure dressed up as an environment bug. Even after diagnosing it, the next unit showed the same duplicate-read reflex, so the in-session correction was partial.

**Human — worked.** The human was the decisive context engineer of this session. The opening prompt invited proper priming. The pivotal intervention — refusing the "flaky console" hand-wave and pointing at "an incorrect assumption in our instructions or memories" — is exactly the skeptical, files-over-vibes pressure that forced the real diagnosis. The restart-vs-continue question was timed at a natural boundary and surfaced the right tradeoff. And the human insisted the lesson be recorded for future sessions, closing the loop on externalized state.

**Human — could improve (minor).** The human authorized commit-and-push in one breath ("commit the new work and push it to the remote") *before* the flakiness was diagnosed — pushing work produced amid a state-corruption scare. Given the project's own caution about per-change approval and the fact that two intended edits had silently not applied, a beat to confirm disk state before pushing would have been the more conservative call; Claude did verify first, so no harm landed.

## Bottom line

A session whose context engineering was a tale of two halves: faithful priming, disciplined files-over-memory authoring, and genuinely strong continuity externalization, wrapped around a long stretch of self-inflicted window noise — duplicate reads, phantom-file thrashing, and a `/tmp` ritual — that Claude wrongly blamed on the environment. What rescues it is the ending: the human refused the misdiagnosis, Claude ran a clean experiment, found the real cause (oversized duplicate-laden batches), and wrote the fix into a persistent memory file. The work shipped correct and green, and the next session inherits accurate state and a sharper operating rule — but the path there spent far more of the window than the one-unit slice warranted.
