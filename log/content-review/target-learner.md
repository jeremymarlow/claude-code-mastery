# Content review — target-learner
Reviewer: target-learner — reads the course as the audience persona
Date: 2026-06-09
Scope read: all 16 rendered units + requirements §1 + workflows.md + learner docs

## Headline

The course's *judgment* content is genuinely good — the planted-defect labs (injection in U3, the three-copy bug in U7, the must-not-fix discipline in U9, the IDOR-plus-false-positive in U11) are the best teaching I've seen on verify-don't-trust, and the skip-checks and fast paths respect my time. But the prose reading experience has two serious problems. First, it never once shows me the tool: sixteen units in, I have read thousands of words about reviewing diffs and approving plans and I have never seen what a permission prompt, a proposed diff, or a plan-approval moment actually looks like on screen — I know the philosophy cold and the interface not at all. Second, the version-data substitution machinery visibly breaks the prose: pre-canned definition blobs are jammed mid-sentence where they don't parse, one ~70-word blob repeats verbatim three times in a single unit, and four units end with raw `</content>`/`</invoke>` tool-call residue. For a skeptical engineer told this course models elite AI-assisted authorship, those glitches read as exactly the unread AI output the course warns me about.

## Cross-cutting findings

**F1 — The course never shows the tool's actual interface; I'm told to "accept," "approve," and "inspect" things I've never seen. Severity: high.**
Evidence: U1 lab step 5 — "**Read the diff before you accept it** … Then accept and commit it" (`course/units/01-onboarding-first-win/unit.md:131`) — nothing in the unit shows how a proposed diff is presented or what accepting/rejecting looks like. U5 step 3 says "**Plan (in plan mode)**" (`05-ship-a-feature/unit.md:136`) after step 1 started a plain `claude` session — the unit never says how to enter plan mode from inside a session (U3 only ever shows the launch flag `--permission-mode plan`). U4 lab step 3 says "Inspect the loaded context" (`04-memory-and-context/unit.md:148`) without naming `/context` at the point of use (it appears only inside a definition blob at line 85). There is not one session transcript, screenshot, or stylized REPL excerpt in 16 units. Why it matters: I have *never used an agentic tool* — that is the premise of the audience (requirements.md §1.4). The single thing genuinely foreign to me is what driving this thing moment-to-moment looks like, and the course teaches everything except that. This is where I'd stall in U1 and where "what do I actually DO next?" hit hardest.

**F2 — Version-data token substitution produces broken, duplicated, glitch-reading prose throughout. Severity: high.**
Evidence: U12 inlines the same ~70-word blob verbatim **three times** — "Custom slash commands are user-defined skills, invoked as /name … Gotcha: `$1` is the SECOND arg, not the first" (`12-commands-and-skills/unit.md:65, 97, 217`). U3: "The currently available modes are acceptEdits, auto, bypassPermissions, default, dontAsk, plan (`--permission-mode <mode>`) — the exact set is version-specific" (`03-operate-safely/unit.md:60`) — six modes listed, two (`auto`, `dontAsk`) never explained anywhere. U3:65 nests a pasted definition inside parens inside a sentence: "(Plan mode (`--permission-mode plan`): Claude plans without writing changes until you approve.)". U4:56 is a fragment: "Project memory via CLAUDE.md, auto-discovered at startup (auto-memory)." U2:53 duplicates its own sentence: "**Ask in natural language about the code.** In-REPL: ask codebase questions in natural language; reference files/dirs with @-mentions…". U15:229 and U16:224 dump multi-clause blobs into "Going deeper" bullets. Why it matters: every one of these reads as template output nobody proofread; a time-pressed skeptic starts discounting the prose and skimming for the lab. The single-sourcing *policy* is right; the rendering is not.

**F3 — Literal AI tool-call residue is committed in four learner-facing units, including Unit 1. Severity: high (trust), trivial to fix.**
Evidence: `</content>` and `</invoke>` as the final lines of `01-onboarding-first-win/unit.md:173-174`, `02-explore-a-codebase/unit.md:157`, `03-operate-safely/unit.md:215-216`, `05-ship-a-feature/unit.md:190-191` (present in the `unit.src.md` sources too, so this is authoring residue, not a render bug). Related polish: stray whitespace-only bullet lines mid-list at `12:28`, `14:26`, `16:30`. Why it matters: the very first unit — the one teaching "read the AI's output before accepting it" — ends with unread AI output. As the skeptical learner this course is for, that's the moment the course's authority took real damage.

**F4 — The verification sermon repeats at a dose calibrated for a much slower learner. Severity: medium.**
Evidence: "green tests are necessary, not sufficient" appears in U1:62, U3:110-111, U5:84, U6:86, U11:99 (plus learning objectives and pitfalls restating it); nearly every unit re-derives the CV lineage ("the same reflex from your first unit…", e.g. `02:66`, `05:86`, `06:199`, `08:187`). Why it matters: §1.4 says I "resent hand-holding." By U6 I was skimming any paragraph containing "(CV)", which means the repetition trains the exact skipping behavior it's trying to prevent. One crisp statement per unit, in a consistent place, would land harder than four.

**F5 — The course's claims about Claude's failure modes are asserted, never exhibited. Severity: medium.**
Evidence: "Claude can describe a layering that's 90% right and confidently wrong about the one boundary you care about" (`02:62-63`); "Claude is extremely good at producing a confident, plausible change in response to a symptom" (`07:53-54`); "automated review loves to flag `Project.archived == False` … as the E712 lint" (`11:92-93`). All asserted in prose; no unit shows a captured example of Claude actually being wrong and the verification habit catching it. The planted-defect labs partially redeem this (the *lab* demonstrates it), but the prose asks a skeptic to take the skeptic's case on faith. One authentic before/after exhibit — a real wrong architecture claim, a real symptom-patch attempt — would be the most persuasive page in the course.

**F6 — Worked examples are dominated by the course describing its own build, and the weakest ones teach little. Severity: medium.**
Evidence: U1 (doctor), U5 (the spec), U6 (the check suite), U7 (a vd-token bug), U8 (the repo's git history), U9 (`tools/_common.py`), U10 (the spec again), U11 (`make check`), U14 (`check-on-edit`). Some are excellent (U14's defense-in-depth, U10 where the spec *is* the subject). But U8's "Run `git log --oneline` in this repo and you'll see the pattern" (`08:106`) shows an outsider commit messages like "P10 10.4 — wire check-breadcrumbs…" — opaque spec-tracker prose, not a model of messages "you can read a year later." U6's worked example is about check-script fixtures, abstract and skimmable. Cumulatively the course's favorite example is itself, and by U10 the dogfooding stops feeling like proof and starts feeling like navel-gazing. The lab codebases (`taskflow-api`/`-cli`) — code shaped like my day job — are barely used in worked examples.

**F7 — Recurring "confirm against the docs / don't author from memory" punts read, after the fifth time, like the course hedging its own content. Severity: low-med.**
Evidence: it is a checklist item in U12:181-183, U13:230-231, U14:203-204, U15:196-197, U16:189; U13's lab even says "an agent file written directly to disk may not be dispatchable until the session *loads* it … Confirm the current behavior against the docs" (`13:193-195`) — the course flags an operational gotcha and then declines to resolve it. The version-resilience discipline is legitimate and well-explained once (U1, U3); as a per-unit refrain it shifts from "discipline" to "we're not sure either," which is corrosive for a learner with no instructor to ask.

**F8 — Named-but-never-explained surface accumulates question debt. Severity: low.**
Evidence: `auto` and `dontAsk` modes named, never explained (`03:60`); `--bare` and `--include-hook-events` appear only inside a blob (`14:115`); `--tmux` likewise (`16:79`); the circular TL;DR openers "Test-driven development with Claude is [test-driven development]" (`06:35`), "Debugging with Claude is [the debugging workflow]" (`07:34`), "The Git/PR workflow is [the Git/PR workflow]" (`08:35`) say nothing and read as template fill. Each is small; together they add to the "generated, unread" impression of F2.

- **U4** — The one-line-changes-behavior lab is a great skeptic-converter. Lab step 3 should name `/context` where it's needed; the "exhaustive CLI reference" reveal is buried in Going deeper (`:197-202`) — I wanted that pointer in U1.
- **U5** — The pivotal unit and mostly solid, but it never says how to enter plan mode mid-session (F1) — the unit's central tool, mechanically unexplained.
- **U6** — Excellent "red for the right reason" teaching; the worked example (check-suite fixtures) is the unit's weak spot. Circular TL;DR opener.
- **U7** — The best unit in the course. The three-copies trap is superb design, and the vd-token worked example is the one authentic "AI-era debugging" story in the prose.
- **U8** — Sound advice; weakest worked example (this repo's history is not a legible model for outsiders, F6). Lab-without-verifier is honestly flagged.
- **U9** — Excellent; the don't-fix-the-bug discipline and the verifier that fails if you "improve" things is the course's thesis made concrete. Long but earns it.

## Top 5 enhancement recommendations

1. **Add one annotated session excerpt to U1 (and ideally U3/U5):** a stylized 15–25-line REPL transcript showing the permission prompt, the proposed-diff presentation, the accept/reject interaction, and (U5) entering/exiting plan mode in-session. This single artifact closes F1 and is the course's most-missing page.
2. **Fix the vd-token rendering pass:** rewrite host sentences so substituted values read grammatically (quote the value; don't paste definition blobs mid-sentence), dedupe any token used more than once per unit (U12 is the proof case), and strip the `</content>`/`</invoke>` residue and whitespace-only bullets from U1/U2/U3/U5/U12/U14/U16. Add a render check that fails on tool-call tags in `unit.md`.
3. **Add one captured "Claude confidently wrong" exhibit** (U2 or U7): a real, short transcript of a wrong architecture claim or symptom-patch proposal, with the verification step that caught it annotated. This converts the course's central skeptic-facing claims from assertion to demonstration (F5).
4. **State in-REPL mechanics at the point of use:** name `/context` in U4 lab step 3; document plan-mode entry/exit in U5's steps; tell U16 step 2 how two agents actually run concurrently. A one-line "(how: …)" per step suffices.
5. **Replace the two weakest worked examples (U6, U8) with taskflow-shaped ones:** e.g., U8 shows a worked 3-commit series for the `archived` feature (prep commit → feature → message diffs before/after editing for "why"); U6 shows red-then-green on an actual API behavior. Keep the dogfooding where it's load-bearing (U10, U14).

## Out-of-scope flags

- **`course/README.md` does not exist** — my reading brief named it; orientation lives in the repo-root `README.md` and `course/units/README.md`. Works fine, but any doc pointing learners at `course/README.md` would dangle.
- **Possibly missing promised-outcome vs. never-promised skills** (flagging, not solving): session lifecycle (`--resume`/`--continue`, when to start fresh vs. continue) is never taught despite being daily-driver reality; cost/token economics get one flag (`--max-budget-usd`, U16) and no mental model; prompt-writing craft beyond @-mentions and "specific and located" is never treated as a skill of its own; model selection (`/model`, when a smaller/larger model) never appears. If the capability map doesn't promise these, they're outcome gaps for the spec, not unit-content defects.
- **`claude ultrareview` (U11) cost/availability**: a cloud multi-agent review may have plan/entitlement implications the unit doesn't mention — that's a lab/provisioning concern (the README promises no second paid service), not prose.
- Labs, verify scripts, capstone, and overall course structure were out of my scope; nothing here grades them beyond what the unit prose claims about them.
