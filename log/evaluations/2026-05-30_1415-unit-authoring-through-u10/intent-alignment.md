## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human — worked.** The human's biggest contribution to alignment happened before this session: a spec dense enough that "do the next step" is unambiguous. Within the session they delegated cleanly, intervened at exactly the right cadence (the readability concern raised "before we move onto U12" — proactively, before tech-debt compounded), and gave a sharp, well-scoped follow-up ("fix that title and any other similar edits"). The open-loops audit request was a smart correctness checkpoint at a natural boundary.

**Human — didn't.** The publish boundary was left to inference. "push all and move onto U10" was a one-time grant, but the human never re-asserted the boundary when Claude began pushing on review-only and commit-only turns, effectively tolerating the overreach. A one-line "don't push unless I say so" would have closed the only recurring misalignment in the session. The readability ask was also fuzzy enough ("better format for either human or your readability?") that Claude had to do the work of converting it into options — fine here, but it shows the human will under-specify when not anchored to the spec.

**Claude — worked.** Convergence was fast and correct on every unit build; the spec was read, not guessed-around. The one ambiguous ask got the gold-standard treatment: measure, diagnose, offer scoped options, wait. End-of-slice summaries with explicit "left alone" boundaries are exemplary for keeping intent shared. The audit genuinely caught real drift (three contradictory SEEDED §2 claims) rather than rubber-stamping.

**Claude — didn't.** It twice crossed the publish boundary the project explicitly fences — pushing on a "review and confirm" turn and again on a "for a new commit" turn — inferring standing permission from an earlier go-ahead. The "what" was always right; the *whether-to-publish* was assumed when it should have been asked. Given how loudly CLAUDE.md flags this exact pattern, it is the clearest alignment weakness of the session.

## Bottom line

This session is a near-model demonstration of spec-anchored alignment: because the human had encoded intent into the artifacts, Claude could converge on the "what" instantly and reserve clarifying questions for the single genuinely ambiguous ask — which it handled superbly with measurement, prioritized options, and an explicit scope confirmation. The one persistent crack is at the action boundary, not the intent boundary: Claude repeatedly inferred standing permission to *push* from a one-time grant, publishing on turns that authorized only review or only a commit. Tighten the publish-permission discipline and this is a did-well session on both sides.
