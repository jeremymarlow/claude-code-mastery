## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human.** Delegation was crisp and trust-calibrated: terse briefs for well-scoped work, escalating from a low-risk audit to three full unit builds as Claude earned it, and discrete per-act push grants that held the line on the project's approval rule without micromanaging the commits. The corrections were the right kind — surgical, aimed at *process* (read-tools, a bash footgun) rather than nitpicking output — and delivered as questions ("explain why…", "won't your pipestatus just be…") that invited Claude to reason rather than just obey, which is how the read-tool lesson eventually got internalized into memory. The proactive "should we start fresh?" check shows a human managing the collaboration's health, not just its output. The only thing I'd note against the human: the read-tool preference clearly existed before this session (it's a documented working agreement) yet wasn't stated up front, so it cost two corrections to surface — a one-line standing instruction would have pre-empted both.

**Claude.** Process was the strength: every unit was driven plan → author → verify-red → implement → verify-green → state-sync → commit, with the lab verifiers proven to *fail for the right reason* on the clean tree before the solution was built (the literal lesson U6/U7 teach — nicely dogfooded). Autonomy was well-judged in both directions: initiative on the cheap/reversible, a hard stop at every push. The `{{vd:`-token self-inflicted check failure in U6 was caught and fixed cleanly, and — impressively — *reused as the worked example* in U7's debugging unit, turning a stumble into pedagogy. Weaknesses are minor and all on the comms/learning-lag axis: the read-tool lesson needed two strikes; the PIPESTATUS reply leaned faintly defensive before complying; and the narration + closing summaries run long for this audience. None of these cost momentum or output quality.
