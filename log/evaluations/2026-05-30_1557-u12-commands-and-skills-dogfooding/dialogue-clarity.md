## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

The human ran this session like a skilled reviewer: instructions were short, scoped, and decisive ("proceed with step 2", "go ahead with /close-unit"), and the substantive interventions were probing questions that each forced a real design correction rather than vague gestures. The human never over-authorized — every commit and push waited for an explicit, narrow go-ahead — and the conditional push instruction ("push everything if no changes are needed") was precise enough that Claude could honor it without re-asking. The one thing the human could not do from inside the dialogue was pre-empt the prop problem; it surfaced through the human's own questioning, which is itself a strength.

Claude's communication was the standout. Replies led with the verdict, structured the reasoning beneath it, and used tables and bolded ledes to stay scannable despite real length. It dissented when warranted (the command/skill-line wrinkle), surfaced tradeoffs in its `AskUserQuestion` options including the cost of its own preferred path, and — most impressively — repeatedly conceded that work it had just committed was a prop, naming its own output as the failure by the same standard it had just applied. It reported every `make check` failure plainly and drew an honest boundary around what it could not verify (fresh-session artifact discovery). The only soft spot is volume: a few replies are long, and the build-then-relitigate rhythm produced three commits for one unit's worth of settled design.

## Bottom line

This is a high-clarity collaboration in both directions. The human asked sharp, scope-tight, decision-forcing questions and authorized incrementally; Claude answered lede-first, surfaced tradeoffs, dissented honestly, and — repeatedly and without defensiveness — agreed that artifacts it had just built and committed were props, then fixed them. There is no sycophancy that costs candor and no dressed-up success. The only blemish is that the "is this genuinely used?" standard got applied after the build rather than before it, producing avoidable churn — but the dialogue caught and corrected it cleanly each time.
