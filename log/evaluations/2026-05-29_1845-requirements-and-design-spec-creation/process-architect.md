## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human — strengths.** The human is the reason this session reads as spec-driven rather than spec-flavored. He refused the fast approve, insisted on a requirement-by-requirement pressure-test, and exercised real veto power (the capability-ladder rejection at R1 is the standout — a genuine design correction, not a tweak). He owned the structural forks (outcome-driven, tiered depth, version-resilience) with crisp rationale, drove the context-management subdivision himself, and kept the approval gate as *his* decision to the end, flipping it only as a deliberate, separate act. His communication was consistently clear and decisive.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault on process. The one soft spot: by replying in Claude's own lean-phrasing on many mid-list requirements, he let the review lean on Claude's framing more than his own probing on R2-R11 — the rigor was real but increasingly Claude-shaped. A more adversarial reviewer would have occasionally pushed back where he instead accepted the lean wholesale.

**Claude — strengths.** Disciplined gate behavior throughout: never wrote design content ahead of approval, refused to self-approve, and built the handoff as scaffolding plus a "why" ledger rather than smuggling unreviewed work into stubs. Its diagnosis that the reasoning would evaporate at the session boundary, and the resulting `decisions.md` + `IMPLEMENTATION.md` + chunked tasks, is exactly the state hygiene the project's working agreements demand. Integrity sweeps and full propagation of the gate flip were faithful.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The ID re-freezing and lingering stale shorthand ("17 features"/"ladder") meant state briefly trailed decisions and needed a deferred cleanup pass — honest debt, but debt. The early parallel-batch and dash-mismatch stumbles added avoidable noise. And the lean-heavy review format, while a strong scaffold, did some of the deciding for the human; on a less engaged reviewer it would have collapsed the gate into ratification.

## Bottom line

A model spec-driven session. The through-line is a real phase gate held by both parties: Claude planned before it built, kept design and tasks blocked behind an explicit approval, captured the reasoning so it would survive a session boundary, and refused to self-approve; the human did the actual reviewing — challenging, redesigning, and reserving the gate flip as his own deliberate act. Work was sliced for context budget rather than dumped. The only real frictions are minor — transient stale shorthand, ID churn, and a review format so well-reasoned it occasionally pre-empted the human's own judgment. Process discipline here is the standard the rest of the course should be measured against.
