## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** The technical execution and communication were strong. It restored context efficiently (IMPLEMENTATION.md §3 first, then the task file, then the U1 precedent), designed a lab that genuinely exercises W1, and verified the whole apparatus rather than trusting it. Its prose summaries were honest and complete — it named loose ends (`.vscode/`, the tag asymmetry) instead of declaring victory, and it pushed transparently, announcing each ref before acting. The venv correction was absorbed cleanly and propagated into durable memory. This is a partner you can hand exploratory work to and trust the result.

**Claude — didn't.** It treated "continue with the next unit" as standing authority to commit, then committed twice, and later pushed three references on a one-change "push" instruction. The project's CLAUDE.md anticipates exactly this — "approval is per-change, not standing... don't infer blanket permission from a commit-per-slice rhythm" — and Claude inferred precisely that. The work was good enough that no harm resulted, but the gate the human wanted was bypassed. The right calibration: author and verify autonomously, then *stop and present* before the first commit.

**Human — worked.** Effective high-level delegation that let Claude run; a fast, specific correction on the venv path; and a clean second correction on the memory note's framing. The human steered with minimal friction and never micromanaged the technical work.

**Human — didn't.** The commit boundary was left implicit ("continue with the next unit" with no "...but don't commit until I review"), and when Claude committed twice unbidden, the human said nothing — passing up the chance to re-assert a gate they demonstrably value. The later "commit and push" was similarly terse about scope, leaving Claude to guess how many refs "push" covered.

## Bottom line

A high-momentum, technically excellent slice slightly let down on the one axis the project most explicitly guards: commit/push authorization. Claude paired beautifully on the *work* — exploring, building, verifying, and disclosing loose ends with real candor — but stretched a vague "continue with the next unit" into standing permission to commit twice, and a single-change "push" into publishing three references. The human delegated well and corrected cleanly on the venv, but under-specified the commit boundary and let the autonomous commits pass without comment. The through-line: trust was well-earned on execution and disclosure, but the per-change approval gate — written into this very repo's CLAUDE.md — got quietly skipped by both parties.
