## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This session is a strong showing on the core hawk virtues. The headline is the version-fact discipline: a CLI-version-specific setting was explicitly *not* authored from memory and was routed to a verifier with a provenance-demanding prompt — textbook. Beyond that, Claude consistently proved "done" rather than asserting it: it parsed the edited JSON and printed the value, read the full diff before committing, confirmed the commit hash and clean tree, and read branch diffs before a force-delete. It twice caught its own flawed reads (the duplicate batch, the pre-write grep race) and re-verified against ground truth instead of trusting a suspect result — exactly the "never act on a single suspicious read" rule. Uncertainty was surfaced honestly, including a real cost it was committing into the repo.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The verification language ran slightly ahead of the verification done. "Verified... requires v2.1.59+ — you're on 2.1.158, so it's supported" presents a docs-sourced, version-inferred conclusion as if the installed CLI had confirmed it; the agent's own provenance was docs, not introspection of the running binary that the prompt explicitly requested. The conclusion is probably right, but the *grade of evidence* was overstated by one notch. And the commit-on-`main` decision resolved a real guidance conflict by assertion rather than a quick confirm. Neither is a fabrication; both are cases of confidence slightly outrunning proof.

**Human — strengths.** The author modeled the verification culture he wanted: destructive operations authorized one at a time (so safety had to be re-proven at each step), the race-condition failure mode named explicitly as a constraint, and the memory strategy demanded as reasoning-before-action. He also made the hard scope call on the auto-memory tension knowingly ("the collaborator/teaching caveat you accepted"), accepting a documented cost rather than pretending it away.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault on this lens. If anything, the "commit and /capture-session" instruction didn't specify the branch, leaving the commit-on-`main` ambiguity for Claude to resolve unilaterally; a one-word "on main" or "branch it" would have closed the gap. Minor.

## Bottom line

A high-discipline session whose through-line is "the files, not memory" — fittingly, the work itself was disabling memory in favor of committed files. Claude honored the no-version-fact-from-memory rule visibly, proved its changes with real reads and parses rather than trusting exit colors, and twice caught and re-verified its own bad reads. The two dings are both species of confidence slightly outrunning evidence: reporting a docs-and-inference result as installed-CLI "verified," and resolving the commit-on-`main` guidance conflict by assertion instead of a quick confirm. Strong did-okay leaning toward did-well, held back from the top by those two over-claims.
