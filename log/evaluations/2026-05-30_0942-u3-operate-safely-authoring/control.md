## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked.** The bookends of the session were strong. Up front, it oriented correctly: read `IMPLEMENTATION.md §3`, pulled the open-loops ledger, traced U3's requirements (R10.AC1–AC8) before writing a line, and presented a clear "here's what's next, here's one decision point" framing that gave the human a real choice. The U3 unit and lab are well-constructed and faithful to the spec. Its verification ethic never lapsed: it grounded version facts in the live CLI, marked what it couldn't confirm as unverified, caught its own dead `{{vd:}}` key, and disclosed partial verification unprompted. And under two pointed challenges it did the genuinely hard thing — it ran experiments against ground truth instead of defending its narrative, reached a correct root cause, and converted it into a durable `feedback-small-tool-batches.md` memory plus corrected operating rules.

**Claude — what didn't.** The middle third was a sustained process failure. Duplicate and oversized tool batches generated a wall of "Wasted call" warnings; rather than reading that signal, Claude built an elaborate `/tmp`-redirect workaround on a false premise that the console was broken. Several edits failed on stale anchors because it was editing from corrupted self-readings rather than re-reading first. Notably, the very unit it was authoring teaches "verify before you act" and "read the diff" — yet its own loop was the opposite of that discipline until the human forced the reckoning. The good news is the recovery was real and self-aware; the cost is that a routine unit-authoring task consumed far more turns and noise than it should have.

**Human — what worked.** The human ran this session well. Direction was crisp ("check IMPLEMENTATION.md and what's next," "start authoring U3," "commit and push"). More importantly, the human did not rubber-stamp: spotting the console errors and demanding edit-by-edit proof was exactly the audit the situation needed, and refusing the "flaky console" explanation in favor of a concrete instructions/memory hypothesis is what produced the correct diagnosis and a lasting fix. The context-window question was a thoughtful, well-placed checkpoint. Push authorization was explicit and unambiguous.

**Human — what didn't.** Little to fault. One could argue the human could have interrupted the visible "Wasted call" storm earlier rather than after the work landed, but catching it at review and driving it to root cause is a perfectly good outcome.

## Bottom line

A session that produced a solid, spec-faithful security unit and — more valuably — a correctly diagnosed, durably recorded fix for a real Claude process pathology, but only because Claude spent its middle third thrashing on duplicate tool calls and a phantom "flaky console" that it had to be pushed twice to debug honestly. The human's skeptical, well-timed interventions turned a wasteful detour into a genuine learning artifact; Claude's verification ethic and its non-defensive recovery were exemplary even as its tool-use efficiency was poor. The output earns trust; the path to it did not, until the human insisted on ground truth.
