## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-model session for the verification lens. The defining behaviors: the lab verifier was confirmed by observed fail-then-pass behavior with a caught measurement artifact (the `tail` exit-code trap); an unconfirmable version fact was left unconfirmed and flagged honestly rather than flipped to look complete; and a stale memory fact was cross-checked against the artifacts and corrected unprompted. Claude also read the *tools* before relying on them — it read `check-frontmatter`, `check-links`, `check-traceability` to learn what each actually enforces (e.g. confirming `check-links` would break on forward-references to unwritten units, so it used plain text instead of links), rather than assuming. Claims to the user were routinely evidence-backed ("C1/C2 dropped off the pending list"), and the end-of-session summary distinguished what was proven from what was the user's to do (the raw terminal log it correctly refused to fabricate).

**Claude — weaknesses.** Thin. The honest edges: (a) prose/worked-example correctness is asserted on the strength of Claude's own reading, not an independent check — acceptable and disclosed, but it is the soft spot in an otherwise hard-verified session; (b) the push scope was inferred slightly beyond the literal instruction, though guarded by a pre-flight `ls-remote` and a post-push confirmation. Neither rises to a confident-claim-without-evidence fault.

**Human — strengths.** The human ran the collaboration in a way that *forces* verification: explicit per-step go-aheads ("commit U1 and create the lab refs"), a standing-rule correction captured for the future, and a closing audit request that demanded state-vs-reality reconciliation. That last request is precisely what good verification governance looks like — it's how the `2.1.157` drift got caught.

**Human — weaknesses.** The single "push our changes" was looser than the repo's own per-change approval norm and left Claude to decide which refs counted. Low-stakes here, and Claude verified around it, but a more precise instruction would have removed the inference.

## Bottom line

A genuinely strong verification session. The through-line is *prove it, and say so honestly*: the lab verifier was confirmed by observed behavior (with a self-caught exit-code artifact), an unconfirmable in-REPL fact was deliberately left `unverified` and disclosed, and a stale `2.1.157` memory fact was cross-checked against the repo and corrected without prompting. "Done" was tied to read evidence — which pending items dropped, which refs round-tripped — not to a green color alone. The only soft spots are that authored prose rests on Claude's reading rather than an executable check (disclosed, not hidden) and one mildly inferred push scope (guarded by a pre-flight check). Both parties earned high marks on the verify-don't-trust axis.
