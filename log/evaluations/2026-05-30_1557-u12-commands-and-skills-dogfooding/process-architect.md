## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude.** Strong end-to-end. Planning preceded building; values came from source files, not memory; state surfaces were kept honest and even over-swept for stale counters; gates were never collapsed. Communication was a standout — every turn ended with a crisp summary and an explicit ask for the next go-ahead, and when challenged it conceded and reworked rather than rationalizing. The honest caveat about fresh-session artifact discovery ("one thing I couldn't verify from here") was exactly the right epistemic humility before a push. The only weakness is the rework churn: U12 was committed before its dogfood artifacts were genuinely settled, producing two follow-up swap commits. Claude managed this transparently, but a sharper instinct would have resolved "are these two artifacts genuinely used?" during planning, before the first commit.

**Human.** Exemplary lightweight gatekeeping. The human never rubber-stamped: each phase got a discrete approval, and the most valuable contributions were the three substance probes that turned hypothetical props into genuinely-used artifacts, plus the "why is that correct?" question that exposed a latent bug in the dogfooded command. The human also set up the right final test (author U13 in a fresh session to truly dogfood the skills) and correctly gated the push on "if no changes are needed." No real weakness — if anything, the human could have caught the prop problem at the plan stage (the AskUserQuestion preview already proposed `new-unit`/`unit-check`), which would have avoided the rework, but catching it via review is the gate doing its job.
