**Claude — strengths.** This is a strong outcome session. The work product is sound across the board: the version refresh produced byte-clean diffs that exactly match every claim made about them; the R8 fix genuinely closes the gate (independently verified — `rubric.md` carries the anchor, strict reports all 17 requirements referenced); and provenance honesty held (per-key dates left untouched to avoid fabrication). Communication was tight and evidence-backed — judgment calls (bump-vs-leave, strict asymmetry) were surfaced for the human rather than silently made.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little to fault. The one self-introduced defect — the `ensure_ascii=False` em-dash churn in the JSON twin — was caught and corrected within the same session before any commit, so it never reached a delivered artifact; still, a moment's thought about matching the original encoding would have avoided the round-trip. The §3 edit string-not-found was a minor retry. Neither affected the final product.

**Human — strengths.** The human ran a disciplined, incremental session: each unit of work (§3 fix, open-loops review, L6 strike, refresh, R8 investigation) was reviewed before commit, with explicit per-change "commit and push" approvals honoring the repo's ask-first rule. The critical contribution was the mid-flight context injection about the held-back version anchor — information only they had — delivered exactly when it unblocked the refresh. And the human pushed past the symptom: directing the strict-gate guard into `close-unit`/CLAUDE.md turned a one-off fix into a class-of-bug fix.

**Human — weaknesses.** Almost none for the auditor's lens. One could quibble that the human approved committing and pushing the version refresh while the R8 strict-gate failure was still open and known — but Claude had clearly flagged it as pre-existing and separate, and the human immediately directed the investigation next, so the work was sequenced sensibly rather than left dangling.
