## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is among the cleaner process performances you will see. Investigate-then-plan-then-verify was followed on both slices; the L10 fold was gated on an empirically-proven precondition (byte-identical command lists) rather than an assumption; every state file that mentioned a closed loop was reconciled, and the ledger/decisions/§3/`tasks.md` quartet was kept mutually consistent. The judgment about *what not to edit* (historical narrative, the findings table, the superseded-but-marked decision text) is the mark of someone who understands that a decision log is an append-mostly history, not a mutable status board. Commit gates were held without prompting, and approval was correctly re-sought per slice. Verification discipline was strong — strict gate, not just plain check, before presenting.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Thin. The one real soft spot is stamping the T2 rationale "decided with user" when the human supplied only the *decision* and Claude supplied the *reasons* — a small overclaim of joint authorship. Nothing functional turns on it, but in a spec-driven shop the provenance of a rationale matters.

**Human — strengths.** Drove from the ledger: picked the next task off the surfaced open-loops menu, which is exactly how a spec-driven backlog is meant to be consumed. Issued discrete, explicit per-change go-aheads ("commit this main and push", "commit and push to main") and never granted blanket permission — the approval cadence honored the per-change rule cleanly. The interruption-and-resume was handled crisply.

**Human — weaknesses.** The reviews were trusting to the point of being near-instant; there's no visible scrutiny of the diffs or the rationales before "ship it." And the T2 close request was under-specified — handing Claude both the decision *and* the job of justifying it. For small, well-argued changes this is fine, but it means the gates here functioned more as trusted-operator sign-offs than as genuine adversarial review.

## Bottom line

A short, disciplined maintenance session that closes two open loops the right way: ledger-driven selection, plan-before-build, a precondition proven before a fold, exhaustive cross-location state sync, careful preservation of historical record, and commit gates held per-change. The only process nicks are cosmetic — a rationale Claude authored but tagged "decided with user," and human reviews fast enough to read as trust rather than inspection. As a worked example of keeping project state honest, this is close to model behavior.
