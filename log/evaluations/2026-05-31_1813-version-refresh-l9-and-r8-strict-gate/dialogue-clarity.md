## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session well from a communication standpoint. Instructions were short, scoped, and decisive — each one a clear next move, never contradictory, never a vague gesture. Approval discipline was clean and per-change, consistent with the repo's own working agreements. Two human moves stand out. First, the mid-session interrupt to supply the deliberately-held-anchor history was exactly the right intervention at the right time — it caught Claude before it spent effort scoping a refresh on a wrong premise, and it was delivered as context, not blame. Second, the "apply Option A *and also* harden the skill/CLAUDE.md to use strict" instruction elevated a bug fix into a systemic guard, showing the human reading past the immediate symptom. The only thing one could ask for is that the human occasionally let Claude run a little longer before each checkpoint — but that is a preference, not a fault; the tight loop kept the work honest and reviewable.

**Claude** was strong on every axis this lens cares about. Ledes were first, structure was clear (headers, a summary table, bolded verdicts), tradeoffs were surfaced unbidden with rejected alternatives named, and — most importantly — failure and uncertainty were reported with proof, not prose. The R8 pre-existing-failure handling (stash, re-run, isolate, name the stale claim, ask before fixing) is the high-water mark. The provenance judgment call on the version bump (refusing to re-date per-key verifications it hadn't actually re-run) shows honesty extending into places no checker would have caught a lie. Weaknesses are minor: occasional verbosity in the wrap-up summaries, and a handful of read-before-edit / encoding mechanical retries. Neither cost candor or clarity; both were self-corrected transparently.
