## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The verification discipline here is genuinely high. Every claim of "this works" for a lab was backed by running the verifier in *both* directions, and the negative-case probing (temporarily fixing a bug to confirm the verifier rejects it) goes beyond what most sessions bother with. It read tool output rather than assuming success — it caught a failed `git checkout`, a `tools/verify-lab: No such file` from a stale cwd (line 1525, re-run with absolute path at 1535), and a `{{vd:key}}` false-positive token its own check flagged (line 1052-1061). The open-loops audit is the high-water mark: it refused to trust a grep count, re-derived the truth with awk, and reconciled the ledger to reality with evidence cited per loop.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two assertions outran their evidence. The "Legacy ⇒ not in §2" claim was authored from a mental model and contradicted by an existing `u07` row — caught only by a later audit. The "verified review-cmds / no L1 debt" headline understates that the slash-command names are the source's own flagged soft spot, even though the deeper decision row is honest about it. And "content preserved" after deleting a 7k narrative leaned on the duplication premise instead of a diff. None of these are fabrications, and all are minor, but they're the exact category — confident structural claims without a confirming check — that my lens exists to flag.

**Human — strengths.** The author steered with good verification instincts. The "review the open loops and confirm all looks as it should" instruction (line 4842) directly triggered the session's best audit work and surfaced the U9 inconsistency that would otherwise have shipped. The readability challenge (line 4138) was a sharp "is this actually the right approach" prompt that produced a measured diagnosis. Approvals were explicit and scoped ("push all and move onto U10," line 2130; "Go ahead and fix that title," line 5311).

**Human — weaknesses.** "ok let's move to the next unit" (line 2715) was treated as authorization to author, commit, *and* push U11 including a new solution branch and tag (line 4100-4115), with no explicit per-change push go-ahead in that window. The project's own working agreement says approval is per-change, not standing, and "Never push … without explicit confirmation." The author's loose phrasing invited the inference. This is process more than my core lens, but it bears on "was done proven and authorized before acting." The author also didn't push back on the "verified review-cmds" framing — though that nuance was buried enough that I wouldn't expect them to.

## Bottom line

The human verified well by *asking the right skeptical questions* at the right moments; the one soft spot was letting "move to the next unit" double as push authorization. Net: verify-don't-trust was the operating norm, with a few assertions that wanted one more grep.
