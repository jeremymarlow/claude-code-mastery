## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This was a disciplined, plan-first execution. It restored context from the canonical state files in the prescribed order, derived the next slice from the dependency graph rather than guessing, and treated each unit as a bounded, reviewable unit of work with its own check-and-commit cycle. State hygiene was the standout: every landing was reflected in `tasks.md`, §3, the decisions log, and the open-loops ledger, and Claude twice went back to reconcile state with reality (the "refs created" doc fix; the stale-memory audit). It pre-cleared its work against the enforcement suite and verified the lab artifact behaviorally. It was also honest about what it *couldn't* do: it refused to flip `search-refs` from `unverified` because it couldn't confirm `@`-mention syntax headlessly (R12.AC3), and refused to fabricate a session log it had no authentic source for.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little to fault on process. The only gap is the unstated rationale for using `make check` rather than `check-strict` as the slice gate — correct in substance, but a process-auditor would prefer it named once. There's also a faint risk in how freely Claude self-initiated state edits and a second "docs" commit; here it was all in-scope continuity hygiene and the human had standing approval to commit, but the volume of self-driven edits is the kind of thing that, in a less faithful session, could outrun review.

**Human — strengths.** Held the commit gate per-change without exception, and actively *tightened* the push gate mid-session with a standing instruction. Let Claude propose-then-confirm the next slice rather than dictating, and closed with a deliberate state-and-knowledge-capture checkpoint that is exactly the right end-of-session ritual for a spec-driven build. The out-of-band remote setup was communicated clearly so Claude could reconcile local vs remote state.

**Human — weaknesses.** Almost none within this session's scope. The push gate had to be (re)asserted, implying it may have been ambiguous before — but the human caught it and fixed it explicitly, which is the right move. The terseness of instructions worked because Claude reliably surfaced a plan to approve; with a less careful collaborator that brevity could leave the gate under-specified.

## Bottom line

A clean, well-sliced, gate-respecting session that reads like a worked example of the project's own spec-driven discipline. Work was planned from canonical state before it was built, cut into one-unit slices, verified against the enforcement suite and behaviorally, and — most impressively — the project's state files (§3, `tasks.md`, decisions log, open-loops ledger, even out-of-repo memory) were kept honest in real time, including two unprompted reconciliations of doc-vs-reality drift. The only quibble is a single unstated rationale (why `check`, not `check-strict`), which doesn't dent the through-line: both parties treated state as the source of truth and behaved accordingly.
