## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human — strengths.** The author drove this session with crisp, incremental instructions and reserved the genuinely consequential calls for himself. The memory-uniformity question itself was a high-quality elicitation prompt: it stated the *problem* (cross-machine divergence), a *proposed solution* (memory off + migrate to CLAUDE.md), and asked for a *sanity check* ("Is that a common pattern?") rather than ordering a blind execution — inviting exactly the strategic pushback that improved the plan. He answered both AskUserQuestion forks decisively, and his "Be careful not to batch race conditions again" was a sharp, specific course-correction grounded in the session's own observed failures. The final "leave as-is and add the note ... commit and /capture-session" was a clean, bounded close.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very little to fault on the alignment axis. The only friction was inherent: "clean up the merged spec branches too" was literally scoped to merged spec branches, so the `quality-pass-p7` edge case required Claude to interpret intent — but that's the nature of terse delegation, not a real defect, and the human resolved it instantly when asked. He also overrode Claude's recommended disable scope (user → project) without stating his reasoning, leaving the collaborator/teaching tension as a known, accepted cost rather than a discussed one — fine, but it means the rationale for that fork lives only in Claude's caveat, not the human's words.

**Claude — strengths.** This is a strong intent-alignment performance. It consistently settled *what* before doing, reframed an imprecise ask into a better model, asked clarifying questions only at true value-forks (and recommended without dictating), and verified the one version-specific fact instead of assuming it. It caught the one foreseeable misalignment (branch not in literal scope, not merged) and the one structural gap (bucket B / dotfiles). It executed the human's overruling choice without friction and kept the repo-vs-outside-git boundary explicit so "uniformity" couldn't be falsely declared complete.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The execution craft showed two self-inflicted process lapses that the *content* of the session ironically diagnosed: it duplicated an entire batch of memory-file Reads (8 wasted "file unchanged" calls) and ran a grep against pre-write state in a parallel batch — both exactly the `feedback-small-tool-batches` failure mode, which Claude itself flagged twice in-session ("the very thing feedback-small-tool-batches warns about"). These are tool-discipline faults, not intent-alignment faults — the *what* was never in doubt — but they are worth noting as the session's only blemish. On the pure alignment axis, the lone soft spot is the semi-unilateral commit-on-`main` decision (insight 6), which was transparent and defensible but not confirmed.

## Bottom line
