## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Momentum was relentless and never thrashed: P2 (eight sub-tasks) and P3 (five) each executed in a single clean sweep with TaskCreate/TaskUpdate scaffolding tracking progress, self-verification gates at each phase boundary, and honest commit messages. The check-then-fix loop on its own bugs was fast and undramatic. Communication was consistently structured — every phase closed with a "what got built / what to flag / where we are / proceed or pause?" summary that gave the human a real decision point. It surfaced discrepancies proactively (the corrected permission-modes set, the 7 unverified keys, the deferred hook) rather than smoothing them over. The continuity self-critique was genuinely candid, and the end-of-session memory curation correctly distinguished "already durable in the repo" from "genuinely new" — it declined to duplicate repo facts into memory, which is the right discipline.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little to fault. The single rejected venv call is the only misstep, and it was recovered cleanly. One could argue Claude could have *asked* about the environment before assuming it needed to build a venv, but given the rule "source of truth is the files," probing first was defensible too — this is a marginal call, not a pattern.

**Human — strengths.** Delegated at the right grain: phase-level goals, not keystroke instructions, and never micromanaged the execution. The corrections were minimal and surgical — one environment correction, delivered once, with the forward-looking rule attached ("moving forward just use uv pip install"). The continuity-audit question was the masterstroke: an open, non-leading prompt that invited diagnosis rather than dictating a fix, and the follow-up ("Is there any knowledge... pertinent for subsequent sessions?") showed the same forward-thinking stewardship. The human trusted but verified at sensible gates.

**Human — weaknesses.** Almost none for this session's scope. If anything, the human relied heavily on Claude's self-assessment of correctness (e.g. accepting "make check is green" and the bug fixes without independently inspecting the tool code), which worked here because Claude was honest and the checks were real — but it's worth noting the verification load was carried almost entirely by Claude. For a higher-stakes slice the human might inspect the artifacts before the "continue" rather than after.

## Bottom line

A model pairing session. The through-line is *well-judged autonomy meeting effective delegation*: the human handed over whole phases and probed at the right moments; Claude executed two large slices with sustained momentum, verified its own work, surfaced its own bugs, refused to fabricate what it couldn't verify, and absorbed the one correction permanently. Trust widened monotonically and was never betrayed. The human's mid-session continuity audit and Claude's candid, restraint-laden response together turned a clean build into a genuinely durable handoff. No thrash, no circling, no defensiveness — this is what the correction loop and the autonomy dial look like when both are tuned right.
