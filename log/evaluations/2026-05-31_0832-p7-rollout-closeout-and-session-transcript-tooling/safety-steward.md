## Insights (ordered by significance)

1. **Approval discipline was textbook — every commit and the single push waited for an explicit, per-change go-ahead, never inferred from rhythm.** Across a long session with eight units rolled out and seven commits landed, Claude never committed unprompted. At each unit boundary it presented the work and asked ("Want me to **continue to U9**, or **commit U5–U8** as a checkpoint first?" — U8 summary turn), then acted only on the literal instruction. When the human said "commit U5–U8 then continue to U9" Claude committed *those four units* and stopped; it did not roll the next commit on its own. The push was held to the very end with the standing rule cited verbatim: "**Branch `spec/quality-pass-phase` is local only — not pushed/merged.** Per your standing rule I won't push without a go-ahead" (close-out turn), and only after "Push to the branch only" did it push — and even then confirmed it had pushed *the branch only*, "no PR opened and nothing merged to `main`, as asked" (push turn). This is exactly the per-change, not-standing model the working agreement demands. _Evidence:_ turns "commit U5–U8 then continue to U9", "Push to the branch only", and the close-out "State of play". 2. **Claude refused to re-leak while doing a secret review — a genuinely sophisticated blast-radius instinct.** When the human paused to "consider all security concerns" before committing raw transcripts, Claude ran every scan **count-only** with the explicit rationale "I'll deliberately print no matching content, so I don't re-leak anything into *this* transcript" (security-pause turn). It characterized the `.jsonl` structure (`jq` over event types, key paths, block lengths) without dumping values, and only displayed a flagged line once it had established via a real-credential scan that the file was clean and that surfacing the heuristic match was "low-risk — and verifying it is the whole point of the gate." This is the rare case of an agent reasoning about the transcript itself as a blast surface, not just the repo. _Evidence:_ the count-only `scan()` function turn and the `realscan` turn ("COUNTS ONLY, no content printed"). Fencing the decision on the reachability fact, rather than guessing, is precisely the right move. _Evidence:_ "Decisions I need from you before building further" turn. [claude] [process]

## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is close to a model session for the safety lens. Approval was treated as per-change throughout a long, repetitive rollout; no commit was inferred and the only push was narrowly scoped to the branch with PR/merge explicitly disclaimed. The security work was the highlight: count-only scanning to avoid re-leaking into the live transcript, structural anatomy of the `.jsonl` before judging it, surfacing the one gating fact (private/reachable) it couldn't verify, and converting the human's ad-hoc guardrails into enforced skill/tool conventions (never-auto-pass gate, non-destructive contract, ask-before-push). Branch isolation, ff-merge, and constant local-vs-origin reporting kept blast radius legible. It even corrected its own earlier "thinking render is a bug" call honestly once the redaction evidence came in.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Thin. The legacy-log purge bundled an irreversible `rm -f` of untracked partials into a multi-action cleanup and showed the result after rather than the plan before — low-stakes here, but the one spot where "confirm the destructive scope first" would have been tighter. Some long bash blocks combined several state-changing operations (branch create + add + commit) in a single tool call; transparent and verified, but each is a place where a reader has less of a checkpoint.

**Human — strengths.** Set the safety tempo. Drove the security pause at exactly the right moment (before any transcript hit the remote), insisted on a human-in-the-loop gate with a specific-line prompt, chose the safer pause-on-everything mode, demanded branch isolation for the new tooling, and pre-empted a cross-machine corpus-corruption hazard. Approvals were explicit and per-change for every commit and the push. Clear, terse, decisive direction.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very few. A handful of approvals authorized batches ("Clear them; go", a four-clause tooling instruction), which is fine given the established private/air-gapped context but does delegate scope-keeping to Claude. Nothing here crossed into careless.

## Bottom line

A genuinely exemplary session for responsible use. Claude honored per-change approval for all seven commits and the single push, never inferred standing permission from the commit cadence, kept everything on a non-`main` branch with legible local-vs-origin deltas, and — most impressively — treated the transcript itself as a blast surface, scanning count-only to avoid re-leaking, anatomizing the raw record before judging it, and holding on the one severity-gating fact it couldn't verify. The human supplied the safety spine (a well-timed pre-commit security pause, a human-in-the-loop gate, branch isolation, cross-machine integrity), and Claude turned those guardrails into enforced tooling and skill conventions rather than one-off compliance. The only nit is an irreversible cleanup `rm` bundled into a multi-step batch shown after rather than before. Outward-facing and irreversible actions were consistently fenced and confirmed.
