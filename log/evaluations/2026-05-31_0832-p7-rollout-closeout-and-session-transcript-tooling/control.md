Minor nit: it briefly proposed an mtime-based session finder before its own probe showed a better signal — but it self-corrected within the same turn, which is the desired behavior, not a flaw.

**Claude — communication.** Clear, calibrated, honest. It distinguished what it could verify from what it needed from the human (repo privacy, secret clearance), labeled confidence levels ("0 high-confidence, 37 benign"), and repeatedly told the human things they might not want to hear (the export is lossy; the skill instructions were inadequate; the back-fill mapping is unreliable; the scanner has false negatives — flagging that `secret_key` itself slipped the pattern, line 12036). Summaries were structured and skimmable without being padded. The volume of trailing option-menus is high but appropriate given the stop-and-confirm working agreement.

**Human — process.** Exemplary collaboration discipline. The slice-by-slice cadence with per-change approval, the decision to pause for security before publishing transcripts, and the insistence on a human-in-the-loop gate and cross-machine non-destructiveness all reflect a maintainer who understands both the spec methodology and the real risks of committing raw transcripts to a remote. The human also drove the most consequential realization of the session (the lossy-export problem) entirely on their own observation.

**Human — communication.** Efficient and precise where it counts; the compressed multi-decision instructions were unambiguous in intent. The occasional terseness ("rebase back … when you're done") leaned on Claude to infer mechanism, which worked here but carries some risk on a less careful counterpart. The "I accidentally hit escape, continue please" recovery (line 738) was handled gracefully on both sides.
