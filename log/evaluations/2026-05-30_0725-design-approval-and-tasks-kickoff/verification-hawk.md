## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is among the cleaner sessions I've reviewed for verify-don't-trust discipline. The version layer was built the right way: run the CLI, read the output, correct a design assumption, mark the unverifiable as unverifiable, and prove the verified/unverified split with a script. "Done" was gated on executed checks whose *output was read*, not on exit codes alone — and that reading caught two genuine bugs (one a false-negative the exit code happily masked). Uncertainty was stated plainly (the hooks-schema deferral, the candid continuity self-assessment). Communication was evidence-anchored throughout: claims like "19/26 verified" and "only R8 pending — correct" are backed by visible tool output in the same turn.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The one real gap is that Claude verified its *artifacts* were internally consistent but did not, on its own initiative, verify that the *orientation documents* (`IMPLEMENTATION.md` §3, the open-loops list, the §6 DoD) still told the truth after two phases of work. These were stale/incomplete until the human prompted. For a session whose explicit theme is session-to-session continuity, "is the doc a fresh session reads first still accurate?" is exactly the verify-before-believing check that should have been self-initiated. Also minor: a couple of green-stamp summaries ("All green: make check, the pre-commit hook, render-vd, doctor…") lean slightly on the color — though in each case the underlying output was in fact read earlier in the turn, so this is presentational, not substantive.

**Human — strengths.** The human's continuity-audit question was the highest-leverage verification act in the session and the kind of probing a fresh session would have suffered without. The phrasing ("If not do you think we should?") invited honest assessment rather than fishing for reassurance, and the human acted on the answer. The earlier course-correction on the venv (interrupting the `python -m venv` to redirect to the active venv + `uv pip install`) prevented an environment-state assumption from propagating. Approvals were given per-phase and explicitly ("Yes, let's continue to P3"), keeping the gate cadence honest.

**Human — weaknesses.** Little to fault through my lens. The human trusted Claude's "P2 done / P3 done" summaries without independently spot-reading the generated artifacts — but Claude's summaries were unusually well-evidenced (scripts, counts, diffs of which keys resolved), so this trust was earned rather than blind. A maximally hawkish author might have eyeballed `version-data.yaml`'s 7 unverified entries; in practice the risk was low.

## Bottom line

A model session for verify-don't-trust discipline: version facts were read off the live CLI (catching a real design discrepancy), the unverifiable was honestly labeled rather than fabricated, "done" was proven by scripts and check-suite output whose *values were actually read* — which is precisely how two latent bugs got caught instead of being masked by a green exit code. The one blind spot was Claude's own staleness: it proved its artifacts correct but left the fresh-session orientation docs lying until the human's sharp continuity question forced an honest, well-located self-audit. That exchange — a probing human and a candid, evidence-grounded Claude — is the through-line, and it lands well on both axes for both parties.
