## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The verification reflexes fired where they mattered most: the version fact was checked against the live CLI and the memory-vs-reality gap was named out loud rather than smoothed; the design was re-read end-to-end and two real defects were caught and fixed before "done"; the final state was confirmed with `git status` against the explicit "uncommitted" claim; and the unbuilt remainder was honestly fenced off from the authored work. It also respected every gate (Q1/Q2 before content, design approval before tasks, explicit go-ahead before committing, branch-before-commit on `main`).

**Claude — weaknesses.** The big-ticket correctness claims ("acyclic," "every can-do mapped," "all 9 workflows") are self-reviewed prose, not machine-verified, and a casual reader could over-trust the "I verified" framing — the enforcement tooling that would actually prove them doesn't exist yet, and Claude could have been a hair more explicit that these were eyeball checks. The "units get smaller, not longer" time claim was asserted without evidence.

**Human — strengths.** Governed the process like a skeptic: enforced the spec's gates, declined the rubber-stamp path in favor of review, and held the commit until session end. These are the verification-positive moves a careful author makes.

**Human — weaknesses.** The actual reviews were terse ("all fine," "Approve as-is," "1.") and accepted Claude's self-curated list of "decisions that mattered" rather than independently auditing the design. The human verified the *process* more than the *artifact* — reasonable for a milestone gate, but it means a lot of correctness rode on Claude's own read-through.

## Bottom line

A disciplined spec/design session whose through-line is verify-before-act done well at the seams that carry version and state risk: the CLI version was checked and the memory discrepancy surfaced honestly, the assembled design was actually re-read (catching real defects) rather than assumed-good, and the final git state was confirmed against the claim. The honest caveat is that the heaviest correctness assertions are self-reviewed prose with no machine check behind them yet, and both parties leaned on a single end-of-phase read rather than continuous verification — defensible for authored artifacts, but the "I verified" should be read as "I read it carefully," not "it's proven." Nothing was faked, nothing was declared done before it was, and uncertainty about the unbuilt remainder was stated cleanly.
