## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

The human ran this session the way a senior engineer should: decisive single-line directives that each picked cleanly from a well-framed menu, plus one richly specific data turn that handed Claude exactly the verified facts and — just as valuably — the honest boundaries of what couldn't be verified ("I don't have an enterprise account"). There was no contradiction, no scope drift, no second-guessing. The only thing approaching a weakness is that the verification dump was pasted raw docs without per-key labels, leaving Claude to do the mapping; that's a trivial cost and arguably efficient.

Claude's side is a strong showing for clarity. Replies are lede-first and well-structured; tradeoffs (save-as-file vs stray doc) and options (the three next-actions) are surfaced rather than buried; and partial success is reported with unusual honesty — the two environment-blocked keys are kept open *with notes explaining why*, and the stale JSON twin is caught and named instead of being hidden behind a green `make check`. Claude also respected the per-change commit/push gate from CLAUDE.md to the letter. If there is anything to fault, it is verbosity in the two big summary turns — the post-edit and post-commit recaps are thorough to the point of slight redundancy (the same five-file list appears multiple times) — but each is well-sectioned with headers, so the buried-lede problem never materializes; it's length, not disorganization.

## Bottom line

A clean, honest exchange in both directions. The human gave scoped, decisive instructions and an unusually candid set of verification results; Claude replied lede-first, surfaced its options and constraints, caught its own stale-twin error in the open, and — most importantly — refused to dress up a 5-of-7 result as a full close, documenting *why* the two stragglers stay open. No sycophancy, no hidden tradeoffs, no premature "success." The only minor drag is some repetition in the summary turns. A model session for clarity of exchange.
