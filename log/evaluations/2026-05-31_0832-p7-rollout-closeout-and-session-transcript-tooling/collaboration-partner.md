## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is among the cleaner collaborations you'll see: evidence-first diagnosis (the lossy-export investigation), autonomy that flexed correctly between rote execution and consequential design, unprompted surfacing of the security dimension, defensive-free correction-handling (the "no, the skill wouldn't work" admission), self-caught bugs, and scrupulous respect for the commit/push gates throughout (every commit followed an explicit instruction; nothing pushed until "Push to the branch only," and even then it confirmed it opened no PR). Communication was consistently structured — numbered forks, explicit "I'll hold here," per-commit diffstat-then-commit rhythm, and honest verdicts including against its own prior claims.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little. The only thread worth naming is that on two consequential moves (building the tool once design "felt settled"; choosing the ff-only integration) it acted and announced rather than asked first — defensible given the latitude it had earned, and both were correct, but it's where the autonomy dial sat a notch past the literal per-change-approval convention. The proposal messages are also long; a faster human might have wanted the five forks compressed. Neither is a real cost here.

**Human — strengths.** Exemplary delegation gradient: telegraphic on proven rote work, exact and multi-clause on the infra decisions. The human also did the single most valuable thing a pair-partner can do for a durable artifact — **stress-tested the skill's future behavior** ("will it be clear to you…") rather than accepting it at face value, and insisted the security gate include a human-in-the-loop prompt. The "pause to consider all security concerns" instinct, raised before any sensitive commit, was well-timed. The human also caught the real-world edge case (working from a different machine without the local `.jsonl`) that Claude would not have surfaced on its own.

**Human — weaknesses.** Almost none in-session. The lossy-export problem that triggered the whole second half was a pre-existing workflow gap the human had been living with for 15 committed logs — but they caught it themselves, surfaced it clearly, and turned it into a durable fix, which is the right outcome. The accidental escape was trivial and owned immediately.
