## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session well from a process standpoint. Instructions were incremental and each authorized exactly one bounded step ("delete the merged branch, local only" → "clean up the merged spec branches too" → "delete it with -D"), which kept every destructive action gated behind a fresh approval rather than a blanket grant — this is the per-change-not-standing discipline the repo prizes, and the human modeled it. The big strategic question was framed as a question ("Is that a common pattern?") before any execution, giving room for analysis-first; and when Claude surfaced the U4 tension, the human made a deliberate call and asked for it to be documented. The one place a stricter author might have pushed back: accepting the *project-scope* (committed) disable over Claude's recommended user-scope, knowingly forcing memory-off on every learner who clones a course that teaches memory — the human accepted the caveat, which is their prerogative, but it bakes a teaching contradiction into the shipped repo with only a maintainer note to soften it.

**Claude** was strong on the gates that mattered: strategy-before-mutation, plan-before-edit, scope-boundary flagging, ask-before-push, and version-currency-by-verification all held cleanly. Its audit was accurate and its diagnoses (stale snapshot duplicating §3; superseded WIP branch) were precise and load-bearing. The weakness is purely in execution mechanics, not judgment: it twice fell into the exact duplicate-batch / write-then-stale-read race that the memory it was auditing documents, which is an ironic and avoidable slice-discipline lapse. It also left one acknowledged tension out of the canonical ledger. Communication throughout was excellent — each step was summarized, each diff presented, each open question surfaced with a recommendation rather than a silent default.

## Bottom line

A clean, well-gated maintenance-and-strategy session: there was no spec phase to hold, but the analogous gates — decide-before-mutate, confirm-before-destroy, verify-before-asserting a version detail, present-before-commit, ask-before-push — were all genuinely held, with the human gating each destructive step and Claude refusing to overreach past literal instructions.
