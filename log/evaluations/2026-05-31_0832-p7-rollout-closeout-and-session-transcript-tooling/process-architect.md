## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session like a disciplined gate-keeper. Every commit and the single push were per-instruction; approval was never standing. The redirections were well-timed and protective — pausing for security *before* the exposed commit, pausing for navigation *before* close-out, and pausing for file-cleanup *before* the corpus commit (rejecting Claude's stage-and-commit to say "there is still some file cleanup… raw and rendered should live in separate directories"). The human also pushed the artifact toward higher integrity on its own merits: insisting the back-fill names "best fit the content of the session, not just the first query," and probing the skill's robustness ("make sure raw transcripts are not removed if running from a session that does not have the raw `.jsonl`," and "will it be clear to you that if no name is given… the name should be the session arc?"). That last question caught a genuine ambiguity in the skill spec that Claude then fixed. The only minor friction is cosmetic: a couple of escape-key interrupts and a steady drip of late-session scope additions ("Sorry for the churn") meant a session that opened as a U5 editorial pass closed as net-new infrastructure — but the human owned and tracked each addition rather than letting them blur together.

**Claude** matched the gate cadence without trying to run ahead of it, and — more impressively — *manufactured* spec rigor in the half of the session that had no spec. It reported state faithfully on `prime-context`, sliced relentlessly, gated "done" on render-and-eyeball rather than bare `make check`, and kept the decisions/tasks/§3 trio in sync at boundaries including the awkward post-close one. Its strongest moments were the ones where it refused to proceed on guesswork: erecting design forks for the user before building, declining to fabricate a back-fill mapping, and retracting its own "bug" claim once evidence showed the renderer was correct. The security work was exemplary — count-only scanning to avoid self-leaking, a structural risk anatomy, and a human-in-the-loop gate that was built *and exercised*. If there is anything to fault, it is faint: the volume of "here are your calls" menus is large, and a reader of the rendered transcript has to wade through several long tool-output dumps — but that verbosity is in service of held gates, not a substitute for them, and the new renderer truncates exactly that noise going forward.

## Bottom line

In the first half Claude executed an approved, locked plan with tight slices, real per-change approval, render-and-eyeball verification beyond the mechanical gate, and faithful state hygiene through a clean phase close-out. Scope grew, but every expansion was human-initiated and immediately re-tracked. Both parties earned `did-well`.
