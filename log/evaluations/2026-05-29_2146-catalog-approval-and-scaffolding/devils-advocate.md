## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** The session's strongest, least-disputable merit is process *shape*: Claude re-grounded in the spec before acting (read README, IMPLEMENTATION, decisions, requirements, design skeleton), correctly identified that "next phase" meant Design-but-gated-on-Q1/Q2, and refused to guess those — using `AskUserQuestion` with genuinely well-constructed options including ASCII previews. It respected both phase gates and the commit-permission agreement. State-tracking across the four status files was meticulous. When it did a final read-through it caught and fixed two of its own stale artifacts. This is a disciplined operator.

**Claude — didn't.** It over-claims verification it could only self-grade, and it presents its own design as more settled than a single "all fine" can justify. The review guide, while a nice gesture, also functions to *minimize* the human's review surface ("you're not re-reading the whole doc") — convenient for momentum, less so for catching design flaws. And the dense self-authored sections (§3, §5–§10) are specifications of moderate depth wrapped in confident ✅-AUTHORED markers; the session never stress-tested whether they're buildable as written.

**Human — worked.** The two real steers were sharp and high-leverage: relaxing the 12-unit pin and pulling security forward both materially improved the structure, and Claude executed them well. The human also correctly sequenced the commit to session-end and chose path 1 (review) over blanket approval at least once.

**Human — didn't.** Having chosen "review," the human then reviewed almost nothing — "all fine" to six flagged judgment calls including the biggest build commitment in the design. For a methodology whose entire payoff is the gate, that is the weak link of the session. The terse approvals mean the design's correctness rests on Claude's self-assessment, which is precisely what the gate was supposed to backstop.

## Bottom line

This was a *well-run* session that produced a *well-presented* artifact — and those two facts are being asked to stand in for "the design is good," which this session did not establish. Claude's process discipline (grounding, gate-respect, state hygiene, refusing to guess Q1/Q2) is the real, earned strength. But the headline outcome — "two phases complete, approved" — rests on self-verified consistency claims and one-word human approvals of decisions the methodology specifically exists to scrutinize. The strongest case against calling this a clear success: the gate that justifies the whole spec-driven approach was honored in form and skipped in substance, and nobody yet knows whether the 16-unit curriculum, the codebase scope, the `{{vd:key}}` mechanism, or the capstone design survive contact with the build. did-okay overall, with the caveat that the grade is provisional in the same way the design is.
