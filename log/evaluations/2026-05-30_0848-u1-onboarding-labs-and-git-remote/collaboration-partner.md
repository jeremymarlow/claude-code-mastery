## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-model display of pair-programming discipline against a spec-driven repo. Initiative where warranted (full author/verify/record loops on a single "start U1"), restraint where warranted (stopping dead at the commit and push gates), and a correction absorbed cleanly with the lesson made durable in memory. The verification habit it *teaches* in U1 ("read the diff/behavior, not the wording") it also *practices* — the round-tripped verifier and the live `git ls-remote` confirmation are the same discipline applied to its own work. The closing review went beyond the ask to catch a stale-version inconsistency and honestly refused to fabricate a log it couldn't produce.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little. The `decisions.md` Edit-before-Read stumble was trivial. The only judgment call worth flagging is pushing the unnamed `solution/`/`tag` refs on the strength of an inference at the exact moment the human was asking for *more* push-caution — right answer, slightly under-confirmed.

**Human — strengths.** Delegated at the right altitude with crisp imperatives, trusted the agent to plan, and reviewed at gates. The corrective instruction ("always ask before pushing in the future") was clear, scoped to the future, and paired with the context Claude needed (the remote now existed). The closing ask was well-formed: review, capture status, capture carryover knowledge, "consistent with the existing conventions" — pointing Claude at its own scaffolding rather than dictating.

**Human — weaknesses.** Mild under-specification on the push request (which refs?) forced an inference. Given the simultaneous caution instruction, a one-clause scope ("push the branch and the U1 lab refs") would have removed all ambiguity. Not consequential here, but the kind of gap that occasionally bites.
