The instruction stream was calibrated to consequence: machine-gun-terse on the reversible git chores, deliberately open-ended on the strategy question, and then decisive ("Lets execute both," "Lets leave as-is and add the note … commit and /capture-session"). The one piece of in-session correction — "Be careful not to batch race conditions again" — was perfectly timed (right before the execution phase, after the audit phase had visibly stumbled) and Claude honored it. The human also made the smart product call on the teaching tension (leave it as-is, document the why) rather than thrashing on it. No micromanagement, no under-specification-then-redirect. The only thing I'd even note is that the human accepted "Aggressive trim" and "Project scope" partly on Claude's framing, but both were independently sound and the human clearly understood the tradeoffs Claude laid out.

**Claude** was strong on communication and judgment, weaker on tool hygiene. The strategy answer, the unprompted flags, the version-currency discipline, and the clean push-boundary respect are all top-tier collaboration. The structured summaries at each arc boundary kept momentum high and the human oriented. The persistent flaw is the duplicate-batch / write-then-read-race pattern, which recurred *despite* being documented in the very memory file Claude was reading and despite being named twice. That it self-corrected after an explicit steer is reassuring, but a partner shouldn't need to say "don't race the batches" when the lesson is sitting in memory open in context. Trust nonetheless rose across the session: by the execution phase the human was greenlighting multi-step plans ("execute both") because the earlier git work and the strategy answer had earned it.
