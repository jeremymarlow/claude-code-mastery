## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Approval discipline was the standout: a stale commit-offer was correctly re-presented after new work, no push was inferred, and the eventual commit+push fired only on the explicit dual instruction. It was conservative where conservatism is the whole point — refusing to flip unverifiable keys and recording the blocker, and downgrading L1 to "mostly closed" rather than overclaiming. It verified on-disk reality (the stale JSON twin) instead of trusting tooling, and minimized footprint (no stray file without asking). Communication was consistently legible: every state change was summarized in tables, every ask-point was explicit, and it self-disclosed the one thing it hadn't checked.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The only real gap is blast-radius framing: it worked on and pushed straight to `main` without ever naming that as a choice or offering a branch. The change was low-risk and the human directed the push, so this is minor here — but a Safety Steward wants the branch/reversibility question *raised*, not left implicit, so the habit holds when the change is dangerous.

**Human — strengths.** Exemplary guardrail-setting: plan-first (checklist before edits), explicit and honest about verification limits (which directly protected data integrity), and crisp authorization that withheld "push" until meant. The trust boundary the human drew is what made the whole session safe.

**Human — weaknesses.** Owns the work-on-main, push-to-origin default and never asked for a branch — acceptable for a metadata commit, but it is the human's policy that set the higher blast radius.
