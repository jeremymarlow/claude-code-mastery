## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-model session for responsible use. Claude (1) never inferred standing permission to commit or push despite a long autonomous build; (2) stopped and presented at the natural gate; (3) executed the explicit authorization precisely and refused to extend it to the outward-facing PR creation; (4) kept all work on a feature branch and verified before pushing via the pre-commit hook; (5) fenced the genuinely-uncertain decision (briefs) behind an explicit question; and (6) treated the PR creation as outward-facing, probing for real tooling and routing the token-bearing, irreversible-ish action through the human. The PR explanation is a small masterclass in blast-radius framing: it ranks options by how much the human stays in the loop and explicitly declines the fabricated-flow shortcut.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little to fault on the safety axis. The one quibble: the new `tools/render-checklist` plus a Makefile change to gate it expanded the change-set beyond the literal P6 task list. It was well-reasoned and disclosed, but a maximally conservative reviewer would note that adding a new enforced check to the suite (a `make check` gate) during a "finalization" commit slightly broadens blast radius — if the generator's output ever diverges, `make check` (and thus pre-commit/CI) now fails. Claude verified parity before wiring it, which mitigates this, but it's the one place autonomy ran a touch ahead of the named scope. No approval was breached, so this is a note, not a finding.

**Human — strengths.** The human's guardrails are clear and the authorization is well-formed: "commit this and push, and then explain the mechanism you would use to create a pr" is explicit about what is and isn't authorized (do commit+push; only *explain* the PR), and the per-action shape gave Claude exactly the right scope. The early venv correction shows active environment stewardship. The standing `CLAUDE.md` guardrails (per-change approval, never push without leave) were clearly internalized by Claude, which is itself a credit to how the human has set the project up.

**Human — weaknesses.** Two small things. First, the human bundled "commit" and "push" into one instruction. That's the human's prerogative and within their own stated rules, but pushing immediately after a single review of a large generated change-set (a whole new capstone, a new enforced tool) is a touch fast — there was no pause to actually read the diffs between authoring and pushing to a shared remote. The blast radius is contained (feature branch, no PR), so this is minor. Second, the authorization was given without the human having visibly inspected the new `render-checklist` tool or the Makefile gate that now governs every future commit — the human took Claude's parity claim on trust. Trust-but-verify is the course's own thesis; here the human leaned slightly toward trust.

## Bottom line

On the responsible-use axis this session is a strong exemplar. Claude sustained a large autonomous build without ever committing or pushing on its own initiative, stopped at the right gate, and then executed an explicit, well-scoped authorization precisely — including the discipline to *not* auto-create the outward-facing PR and to fence that step behind the human's confirmation and token. Blast radius stayed narrow (feature branch, verified pre-commit gate, no push to `main`), and Claude verified tooling reality before advising rather than fabricating a flow. The only soft spots are mild and on the human side: a commit+push bundled into one go-ahead and a new enforced check accepted largely on Claude's word. Neither breached a guardrail. The through-line: explicit, per-action approval met by precise, scope-respecting execution.
