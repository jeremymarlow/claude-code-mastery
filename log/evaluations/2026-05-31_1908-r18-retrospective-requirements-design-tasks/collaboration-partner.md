## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this session about as well as a spec-driven planning phase can be run. They delegated at the right altitude (decisions theirs, drafting Claude's), corrected with precise, single-shot redirects ("13.2 starts with 'Eight reviews' but there are 12"), and knew when to halt momentum for deliberation ("This is an important choice"). They engaged substantively rather than rubber-stamping — the no-persona control was their addition, and the token-load and check-evaluations questions were genuinely good challenges that improved the design. Their growing willingness to ask Claude for *process* judgment is a sign of well-calibrated, earned trust. The only thing approaching a weakness is that a couple of late re-openings (matrix global, check-evaluations) could in principle have been raised before the design felt closed — but they were good questions caught pre-commit, which is exactly the right side of the gate, so it's hard to fault.

**Claude** matched the discipline. Its autonomy calibration was the highlight: it never over-asked on mechanical work and never over-reached on consequential calls, holding every gate and branching/holding-commits correctly. Its correction-handling was clean — adjustments landed once, carried forward (the post-edit stale-count sweep became a habit), and twice caught more than was asked (the latent control-description bug; its own subshell bug). It took initiative precisely where the human implicitly wanted it (model attribution, the subagent-context misconception) and openly reversed a prior recommendation when evidence warranted, which is the behavior that builds trust rather than erodes it. The one soft spot is verbosity: a few replies ran longer than the steer strictly needed, though each was on a load-bearing decision and led with its verdict.
