## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The verification posture was genuinely good for a spec-authoring session, where the temptation to just assert "looks consistent" is strong. Claude ran machine checks to back its consistency claims, read the output, and reasoned about why the surviving grep hits were benign rather than treating an empty/expected result as automatic proof. On the highest-stakes verification axis — version facts about a CLI past the training cutoff — it did the right thing decisively: it never fabricated a command or flag, named the risk to itself explicitly, and built the verify-against-installed-CLI rule into the spec as a hard prohibition with a provenance requirement. The boundary handoff was also verified end-to-end (re-read the files, re-ran integrity after writing the new ones).

**Claude — weaknesses.** Targeting precision lagged the verification instinct. The session is littered with "Wasted call — file unchanged" reads and at least one no-op edit, meaning the model was sometimes acting on a remembered file state and being corrected by the environment rather than by its own check. The early parallel-Bash cancellation cascade (and a second near the end) shows the same theme: batched actions where one failure invalidated state Claude then had to reconstruct. And the wrap-up phrase "cross-references valid" over-claims — the checks covered ID tokens, not the semantic correctness of the many inter-AC references it authored. None of these caused a wrong artifact, but each is a case where the tooling, not Claude's own verification, was the safety net.

**Human — strengths.** The human drove the verification-relevant structure: pressure-testing every requirement ("let's discuss each requirement in turn and some alternatives"), insisting the reasoning be captured for a memoryless successor session, and giving approval as an explicit, separate act rather than letting it be inferred. This is good skeptical-collaborator behavior — it forces claims and decisions into durable, checkable artifacts.

**Human — weaknesses.** Mostly stylistic for this lens: the human frequently approved Claude's "leans" by quoting them back verbatim, which is efficient but means the human leaned heavily on Claude's framing of the alternatives rather than independently probing whether a "lean" was right. That's a trust posture, not a verification one — fine here because the domain is preference-driven spec design with no ground truth to check, but worth naming.

## Bottom line

For a session with almost no runnable code, this scored well on my lens precisely because it treated a prose spec as something to *verify*, not just assert: integrity was proven by grep/count/read with the output actually reasoned about, the boundary handoff was re-checked end-to-end, and — most importantly — the one place real verification discipline was load-bearing (never author a version fact from memory about a CLI past the cutoff) was honored proactively and baked into the spec as a hard rule. The blemishes are precision, not honesty: imprecise edit/read targeting that let the environment be the backstop, and a wrap-up that called cross-references "valid" when only ID tokens were checked. The human's insistence on capturing rejected alternatives for a memoryless next session and on giving approval explicitly, not implicitly, reinforced exactly the verify-then-act discipline this course is meant to teach.
