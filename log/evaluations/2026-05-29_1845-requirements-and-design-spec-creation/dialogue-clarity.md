Crucially, Claude didn't just comply — it surfaced rejected alternatives and named ripple effects every time ("_Ripple:_ The ladder is load-bearing for R6.AC2"). _Evidence:_ R1 review ("**My lean:** Keep R1; adopt alternative 3's naming style. It let a 15-requirement review stay decisive instead of devolving into a checklist slog. _Evidence:_ R8 review: "The one I'd flag for your judgment is (3) — the mid-course checkpoint slightly complicates the clean 'capstone is the only assessment' story." ## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

The human author ran this session with rare discipline. Instructions were scoped and decisive: the opening brief was specific (audience, goal, methodology, target version, intended use of the spec for ongoing maintenance), and every subsequent decision was returned in a form that eliminated ambiguity — often by restating Claude's exact enumerated changes. The one place the human diverged from rubber-stamping was the most valuable moment in the session: the R1 pushback on ranking labels was a clean correction that named the problem and its rationale and trusted Claude to redesign. The human also made a strong process call at the end — anticipating the session boundary and asking for an implementability/context review rather than barreling into design. The only thing approaching a weakness is that the human accepted Claude's "leans" near-verbatim across all 15 requirements, which could read as under-scrutiny; but given the quality of Claude's tradeoff surfacing, deferring to well-argued recommendations is a defensible (not lazy) stance, and the R1 correction proves the human was reading critically.

Claude's communication was excellent on structure and honesty, very good on tradeoff-surfacing, and slightly blemished by reflexive agreement openers. The per-requirement template was genuinely reader-serving: lede-first, alternatives always surfaced (including ones Claude recommended *against*), ripples named, and a single forced decision per turn. Failure reporting was honest and unembellished — cancelled batches, wasted reads, and missed edits were all called out plainly, and the version-accuracy self-risk was surfaced proactively against Claude's own convenience. The handoff design showed real context-management judgment and a clean refusal to assume approval permission. The deductions are minor: the "Good —"/"Agreed —" openers are a validation tic (substance followed, so cost was low), and one option-framing leaned toward speed without a symmetric cost label. The early parallel-Bash failure (a glob that cancelled an entire batch, twice) is a process nit, but Claude diagnosed and recovered transparently both times.

## Bottom line

This is a high-functioning collaboration where both parties communicated clearly in their respective directions: the human gave scoped, decisive instructions and one sharp, well-reasoned correction; Claude answered lede-first with a disciplined, repeatable review structure that surfaced rejected alternatives, named ripples, isolated the one decision that mattered, and reported its own failures and even its own model-version limitations without dressing them up. The only real blemish is a recurring "Good —/Agreed —" affirmation tic that flirts with sycophancy but rarely cost candor because substance always followed. A genuinely strong session for dialogue clarity.
