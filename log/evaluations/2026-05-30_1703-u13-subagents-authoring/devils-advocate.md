## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked.** The version-currency instinct is the best thing in the session: rather than author hook event names from memory, Claude reached for `update-config` to get the verified enum (line 1631), and when `claude --help` didn't surface hook schema it said so plainly (line 1629). The hook dogfood was constructed with real engineering care — timed `make check` at 0.18s before deciding to run it on every edit (line 5037), pipe-tested green/unrelated/broken-file paths (lines 5113-5159), `jq -e`-validated the merged settings (line 5233). State-file synchronization via the `close-unit` chore was thorough and consistent. The branch-naming research (checking real history over the textbook default) was exactly right.

**Claude — what didn't.** It never once pushed back on the four-unit marathon despite having read the one-unit-per-slice protocol minutes earlier — process compliance lost to momentum. It verified the easy flags and authored the hard subagent-dispatch mechanism from prior knowledge while checking the R12.AC3 box. It closed L2 on a proxy proof. It absorbed a 3,400-line skill dump without remarking on the cost. And it inferred its way past a real bug in its own dogfooded command rather than flagging it.

**Human — what worked.** Clear, decisive direction; the prime-context kickoff was disciplined; the branch-nomenclature question and the follow-up correction showed genuine engagement with project convention and a willingness to overrule Claude's first recommendation on sound grounds. Per-change push authorization was at least stated explicitly each time ("the user explicitly authorized the push," line 1369) rather than assumed.

**Human — what didn't.** The human is the one who set the four-unit pace, against the project's own protocol, and never asked Claude to slow down or work one slice. Spec/state edits (decisions.md, tasks.md, the open-loops ledger) were committed and pushed without review across three units — the CLAUDE.md "review spec edits before the gate" agreement was effectively skipped. And the empty `/close-unit` invocation went unremarked by the human too.

## Bottom line

A high-throughput, competently-executed session that an over-generous panel will grade on its output — four units, P5 complete, every can-do traced, all checks green — and miss that the output was bought by overriding the project's own one-unit-per-slice context protocol, by checking the version-currency box on peripheral flags while authoring the central subagent mechanism from memory, by closing an open loop on a proxy proof, and by stepping over a live bug in a dogfooded command. The work is real and mostly sound; the *process* repeatedly chose momentum over the disciplines the course exists to teach. The honest grade is did-okay, not did-well: the green checks are a low bar (`make check` can't catch an unverifiable prose lab or a hook that never fired), and the things that went right — the `update-config` verification instinct, the hook engineering, the branch-naming exchange — are genuine but don't offset a session that quietly contradicted its own curriculum.
