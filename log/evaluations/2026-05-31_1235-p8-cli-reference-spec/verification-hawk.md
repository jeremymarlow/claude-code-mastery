## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** On the verification axis this was a strong session. Claude reflexively confirmed git state before acting and after acting (branch-merge check, post-push `status -sb`), read tool source to validate a claimed gap instead of intuiting it, and treated the no-memory-version-fact rule not as a hurdle to route around but as a load-bearing constraint it wove into both new requirements with explicit provenance ACs. It surfaced uncertainty honestly — the `check-traceability` gap, the WebFetch/provenance boundary, the byte-stability tradeoff — rather than smoothing it. When caught committing an unreviewed design, it owned it cleanly and verified the recovery steps rather than declaring them.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two soft spots, both low-severity for a spec-only session. First, it leaned on the 2KB `make check` preview and the hook's exit status without ever opening the full persisted output to confirm no late failure — trusting a truncated green tail. For docs commits with human review downstream this is fine, but it is the "green = proof" reflex in miniature. Second, several "Done" recaps paraphrased the *content* of edits confidently without echoing the diff; the Edit tool's success is the real proof, so the substance was sound, but the communication was assertion-shaped where evidence was cheap.

**Human — strengths.** The author was the session's verification engine. He rejected a design commit that had slipped past the review gate, forced a brittle enforcement design (hardcoded `R16` bump) into a resilient one (dynamic discovery), and insisted a durability rule live in a verifiable, checked-in location rather than volatile machine memory. Each intervention was a *check* — of process discipline, of design durability, of where-truth-lives — not just a preference.

**Human — weaknesses.** Few on this axis. He approved requirements, design, and tasks largely on Claude's well-structured summaries; he did not (in-transcript) independently read back, say, the §12 design diff before approving "design approved. commit and push" — though he had explicitly asked to review first and the gate structure gave him the opening. Trust in the summary is reasonable given the quality, but the approvals are summary-mediated rather than diff-verified.

## Bottom line

A spec-authoring session (R16 + R17 through requirements → design → tasks gates) that scores well on verify-don't-trust precisely because it had little executable behavior to over-claim and handled the claims it did make with discipline: git state confirmed before and after every action, a real enforcement gap proven by reading the tool's regex, and the no-version-fact-from-memory rule promoted into the spec's own provenance ACs. The human author supplied the sharpest verification — catching an unreviewed-design commit and steering a brittle check toward a resilient one. The only real softness is Claude trusting truncated-green `make check` previews and paraphrasing edit content instead of echoing diffs — minor for a human-gated docs session, but the seeds of "green = proof" worth watching once P8 moves from spec to executable code.
