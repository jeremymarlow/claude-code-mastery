## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-exemplary verification performance. Every substantive claim is backed by an executed check whose *output is read*, not just its exit status: the two-phase lab battery with right-reason-red analysis, the doctor round-trip, the drift-gate both-ways proof, the resolved-token value dump. It honored the no-version-from-memory rule consistently and pulled facts from `meta/`/source files. Its standout virtue under this lens is *honest re-grading*: it openly retracted a premature "Lens 8 ✅" when a deeper check contradicted it, and it correctly escalated that finding from "polish" to "real defect" with a quantified blast radius. It verified before acting on the `taskflow_app/` scare and before answering the front-matter-placement question. It distinguished its own residue from product defects and restored state.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Light. The recurring pattern of asserting a unit "clean" and *then* running the residual-code scan (U2) inverts the ideal order — the proof should gate the claim, not follow it; the self-catch saved it, but a missed scan would have shipped a bare code. Separately, the session built real infrastructure (`render-units`, `render-index`, two `make` gates) mid-"polish" pass; Claude flagged this honestly, but it is a scope-discipline note more than a verification one.

**Human — strengths.** The author set a verification-forward frame ("inspect from a wide variety of quality perspectives," "full functional run") and then enforced it at the gate by refusing to approve U1 from a summary — "Show me the rendered U1 before I approve." He also caught the two methodology drifts (infra-mixed-with-polish; spec/tasks discipline lapsed) that Claude had only lightly flagged, and insisted on fixing them before proceeding. That is a reviewer who checks rather than trusts.

**Human — weaknesses.** Minor. The initial open-ended "make every improvement you suggested" could have authorized a large blind sweep; the user partially mitigated by adding "Pause to ask me… if needed," and Claude's pilot-first discipline did the rest. The author leaned on Claude to surface methodology drift rather than naming it up front — though he did catch and act on it once raised.

## Bottom line

A model session for the verification lens. The functional run is textbook — labs executed in both directions with the *reasons* for red read, not just the colors — and the defining moment is Claude honestly retracting a premature "accessibility clean" verdict when a deeper check exposed an R15 rendering defect, then proving the fix with a both-directions drift gate. The only blemish is a recurring assert-then-scan ordering on the unit cleanups, always self-caught. Both parties treated "green" as the floor and "done" as something to be proven.
