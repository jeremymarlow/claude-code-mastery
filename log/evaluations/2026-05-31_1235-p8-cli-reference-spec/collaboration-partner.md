## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Excellent grounding-before-proposing: it read `check-version-drift`, `render-vd`, `version-record.md`, and the requirements before drafting anything, and explicitly identified that the feature was non-duplicative of existing machinery. Its recommendations were opinionated and evidence-tied rather than menu-presenting. Correction-handling was uniformly clean — it owned the design-commit slip without defensiveness and never repeated it. It also surfaced a real downstream landmine proactively (the `check-traceability` R15 cap) before it could bite, and turned a vague human principle ("resilient process") into a concrete, scoped engineering change.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two avoidable misjudgments, both about autonomy calibration in opposite directions. It *over-asked* with a heavy structured-question tool where it should have led with a recommendation (the human had to redirect to get there), and it *over-reached* by committing the design in the same turn it authored it — violating a gate sequence it had itself articulated minutes earlier. Neither was costly because the human caught both quickly, but a more calibrated partner would have led with "here's my recommendation, confirm?" and stopped at "design is in the tree — review before I commit" without being told.

**Human — strengths.** Masterful delegation calibration: short leash early, then progressively terser high-trust instructions as Claude earned it. The redirects were surgical and pedagogically sharp — the "machine-readable intermediate?" question elicited a better design than any menu would have, and the "repo not machine-memory" correction fixed a durability bug in Claude's own learning process. The human consistently corrected the *system* (where rules live, how the process generalizes) rather than just the immediate output, which is the highest-leverage kind of steering.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very little to fault. The git choreography turn ("stage … commit … push … rebase … wait") was efficient but dense enough that a slightly slower hand-off would have left less room for error on a push-to-main; it happened to land fine because Claude executed carefully. That is a minor nit against an otherwise exemplary steering performance.

## Bottom line

Claude's two stumbles were both autonomy-calibration misses — over-asking with a structured tool, then over-reaching by committing a design before review — but it recovered from each without defensiveness and carried the lessons forward, including reversing its own instinct to hide a lesson in machine-local memory. The human's steering was the standout: it corrected the *system* (where rules live, how enforcement generalizes) rather than just outputs, and delegated at exactly the altitude Claude had earned. The through-line is a correction loop that worked as designed — clean steering met clean adjustment, and the relationship was visibly stronger at the end than the start.
