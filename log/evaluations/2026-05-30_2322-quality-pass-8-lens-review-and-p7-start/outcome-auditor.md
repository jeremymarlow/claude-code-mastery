## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — worked.** The defining strength this session was that Claude treated "done" as something to be *demonstrated*: the drift gate was tested in both directions, the verifiers were actually executed in two phases against real branches, the substrate suite was run (36 tests green), and the version token render was eyeballed line-by-line after each unit. When its own earlier verdict (Lens 8 "clean") proved wrong, it said so plainly and reclassified the finding as a real R15 defect. The decision to mine `version-data.yaml` for "garble-prone" values (backtick-containing or sentence-shaped) before grinding through 12 more units was a smart, outcome-protecting move — it converts a per-unit discovery into a pre-emptible class. The accuracy of its claims against delivered files is high: every "GREEN," every "0 tokens remain," every "fail-clean/pass-on-solution" is backed by visible tool output.

**Claude — didn't.** The original 8-lens audit produced a false "clean" on accessibility that would have shipped a 109-token, 14-unit defect had the human not happened to paste raw token text from Gitea. That is a real gap in the first-pass audit rigor — the lens that was *supposed* to catch graceful-degradation failures scanned only for emoji/color. The session also leaned on direct edits to U5–U16 `unit.md` before migration, committing them with raw tokens still present; defensible as a checkpoint, but it means a chunk of the delivered tree is in a known-inconsistent state that still needs render-and-read (where, on U3, two garbles per unit were the norm, not the exception).

**Human — worked.** The human was the quality conscience of the session: catching the raw tokens, catching the methodology drift, demanding to see rendered output before approving, and holding the front-matter line against an over-eager change. Each intervention targeted the *artifact a learner experiences*, which is precisely the auditor's concern. The instruction to do "a full functional run, but also consider the actual learning content from the perspective of the human learner" set the right altitude and directly produced the most valuable findings.

**Human — didn't.** Little to fault. The one structural risk the human accepted is the size of the Stage C rollout relative to context — but they explicitly chose the resumable-checkpoint path and asked for state to be persisted, which is the correct mitigation.
