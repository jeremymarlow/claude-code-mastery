## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human author** ran this session the way a spec-driven build should be run. Gates were real: each "start UN" was a deliberate go-ahead, commit and push were authorized per-change and never as a standing grant, and — most importantly for state integrity — the author audited rather than trusted. The "are you sure those edits took place?" challenge is the single most valuable intervention in the session; it caught a genuine reconciliation gap and forced honesty. The follow-up insistence on root-causing the `/tmp` ritual ("my suspicion is an incorrect assumption in our instructions") shows an author who treats process drift as a first-class defect, not a quirk to tolerate — and the result was persisted to memory so it compounds. I have essentially no process or communication fault to assign to the human here.

**Claude** earns strong marks on the structural disciplines — slicing, gate-respecting, commit hygiene, cross-pointed state updates, and pre-commit gating — and on candid communication: its summaries consistently separated what was verified from what was deferred, named its own bugs, and flagged the unverified version-data keys honestly rather than fabricating "verified" values (R12.AC3 honored throughout). What drags it to did-okay on process is the state-integrity lapse: it wrote a *fabricated decision* and three false status claims into the canonical state files before catching them — and did so one turn after diagnosing the very failure mode that caused it. The decisions log and §3 are the things a fresh session trusts blindly; polluting them, even transiently, is the most consequential miss for this lens. The mitigations are real (it reverted everything, proved it with grep, and disclosed the whole thing), so the net on-disk state ended correct and the gates ultimately held. But "correct after a correction pass" is a weaker outcome than "correct the first time," and the noisy, duplicate-call, /tmp-round-trip behavior that produced the phantom reads was self-inflicted churn that put state accuracy at risk.

## Bottom line

A well-planned, well-sliced, well-gated build session whose state hygiene was *eventually* faithful but not *cleanly* faithful: U3 landed crisply, but U4 took a self-correcting detour through fabricated decision-log entries and false status claims caused by phantom reads. The saving graces are entirely process-positive — a human who audited the gates instead of rubber-stamping them, and a Claude that reconciles against disk and confesses its own contamination rather than hiding it. The decisions log survived intact only because both parties insisted on ground truth; the lesson the session itself draws — verify any "I found/changed X" against a clean read before recording it — is exactly the right one, and it was persisted to memory so the next slice should be cleaner.
