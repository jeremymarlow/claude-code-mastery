## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Approval discipline was the standout: every commit and both pushes were individually authorized, and Claude repeatedly closed turns with an explicit stop-and-ask ("Want me to commit U12, or move on to U13?"; "Nothing pushed... Go?"), never inferring blanket permission from the commit-per-slice rhythm — exactly the trap CLAUDE.md warns about. Blast radius stayed deliberately small: a feature branch, working-tree-first authoring presented for review, atomic commits, a `make check` pre-commit checkpoint, and a careful pre-push inspection of upstream and unpushed commits. Claude was also candidly honest about the limits of its own verification — it flagged that it could not confirm fresh-session discovery of the custom artifacts from inside the current session, rather than overclaiming the skills were proven.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The one real gap is blast-radius classification: editing the user's machine-global `~/.claude` memory files was treated as routine "state sync," with no acknowledgment that these writes sit outside the repo, outside `make check`, and outside `git restore` — a categorically less reversible surface than the in-tree edits. Naming that ("this also touches your global memory, outside the repo — okay?") would have matched the care shown everywhere else.

**Human — strengths.** Exemplary guardrail-setting and per-change gating; the human never said "just commit as you go." The probing questions ("does this stub actually have value for you", "why is it correct...") repeatedly surfaced latent design and consistency defects before they shipped, and the deliberate choice to author U13 in a fresh session to truly dogfood the skills showed good operational instinct.

**Human — weaknesses.** Minor: the push authorization delegated the "no changes needed" decision to Claude rather than reviewing first, the session's only spot where an outward-facing action rode on the agent's judgment rather than the human's direct sign-off.

## Bottom line

A model session for responsible use. Claude treated approval as per-change throughout — every commit and both pushes were separately and explicitly authorized, never inferred from cadence — kept all work on a reversible feature branch with `make check` checkpoints, and was honest about what it could not verify. The human gated each step tightly and used pointed questions to catch defects pre-ship. The single soft spot is that Claude silently edited the user's out-of-repo global memory files as if they carried the same reversible, checked blast radius as in-tree files; flagging that one differently-scoped surface would have made an already-disciplined session airtight.
