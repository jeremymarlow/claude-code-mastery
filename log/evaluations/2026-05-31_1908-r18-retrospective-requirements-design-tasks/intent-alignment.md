## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human** ran this like a disciplined product owner who knows the difference between a question and an instruction. Strengths: opened with a rich but coherent brief; corrected fuzzy requirements with concrete, testable language (the AC1 verbosity demand is a model of "say what you actually want"); reserved the expensive decisions (panel composition) for explicit deliberation *before* lock-in; and used Claude as a thinking partner ("it's your evaluation that matters") without abdicating the calls. The only soft spots are mild: the breadcrumb requirement and the late synthesis-topology questions arrived after their natural gate, meaning a couple of "what" decisions were discovered slightly later than ideal — but the human absorbed each consequence cleanly (deferring R19, re-opening AC6) so nothing actually drifted out of alignment.

**Claude** was disciplined about not building before the what was agreed, and about distinguishing inferable-from-context (the playbook, append-only IDs, the model split) from genuinely-the-human's-call (audience, panel, global topology). It asked clarifying questions at the right granularity — never the obvious, always the load-bearing — and when it inferred, it stated the inference and offered the off-ramp ("if you'd rather it be its own requirement, say so"). It also surfaced rather than buried two real spec tensions (the control vs. AC5; completeness vs. traceability-only) and corrected its own errors in flight (the net-zero arithmetic; stale counts). Its one recurring risk is over-thoroughness — long pre-amble before each AskUserQuestion — but in a spec-authoring session that verbosity is the substance being agreed, not noise, so it reads as appropriate here.

## Bottom line

This is a near-exemplary alignment session: the entire point was to converge on *what* to build before building, and both parties did exactly that across three gates without a single silent assumption that later proved wrong. Ambiguity was surfaced early (the opening two-question gate), requirements were elicited concretely and tightened iteratively (the AC1 verbosity correction), the most expensive scope decision was deliberately re-opened while it was still cheap (the panel rebalance), and spec tensions were named rather than glossed (control vs. AC5). The few late-arriving "what" refinements (breadcrumbs, synthesis topology) were handled with deferral and clean re-amendment, so convergence stayed sharp throughout. Both human and Claude earn did-well on both axes.
