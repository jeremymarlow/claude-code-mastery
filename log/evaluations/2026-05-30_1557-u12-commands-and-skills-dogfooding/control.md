## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human author** ran this session unusually well as a reviewer-driver. Rather than accepting plausible-looking output, they probed it with a consistent standard — "does this artifact have a real consumer?" — applied first to the skill, then generalized to the command, then to the close-unit/L7 contradiction. Each probe surfaced a genuine defect Claude had not caught on its own. The questions were short but load-bearing ("i want to understand if this stub actually has value for you"; "why is it correct for close-unit leave those open in L7"), and they were calibrated to Claude's own prior statements (the error-proneness of Edit, the dogfooding mandate). Approvals were explicit and scoped, honoring the repo's per-change permission rule. The one thing the human could have done to reduce churn: hold the artifact-quality bar *before* authorizing the first U12 commit, since the critiques that triggered two reworks were available in principle at the planning stage.

**Claude** executed the mechanics near-flawlessly: principles-first authoring matched to an existing unit's depth, repeated `make check` gating, honest distinction between expected `PEND` and real `FAIL` (it correctly read C13 dropping out of "not yet practiced" as the proof the lab landed), and disciplined permission behavior. Its communication was a standout — it consistently led with "Honest answer:", named the wrinkle in its own proposals (prime-context being "command-shaped"), quantified churn cost before incurring it, and flagged the one thing it couldn't verify (fresh-session artifact discovery). Where it fell short was *judgment at the front of the loop*: it shipped two artifacts that failed the unit's own authenticity bar, and only recognized that under questioning. A reviewer with the dogfooding requirement in hand might have asked "do these have a real consumer?" before writing them into a committed unit. Claude reasoned beautifully about the problem once raised, but the raising was the human's contribution.

## Bottom line

A high-functioning collaboration in which a sharp human reviewer repeatedly converted plausible-but-hollow output into genuinely authentic artifacts, and Claude responded with disciplined execution, honest self-assessment, and a willingness to fix its own work — including its own dogfooded command. The through-line is a good division of labor: the human supplied the skeptical "does this actually serve anyone?" lens, Claude supplied rigorous sourcing and clean execution. The cost was real churn — one unit landed across three commits because the first two artifacts were props — which better up-front judgment by Claude (and a slightly earlier quality gate from the human) could have trimmed.
