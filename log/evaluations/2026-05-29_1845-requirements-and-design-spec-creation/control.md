## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Human author.** The human ran this session about as well as a spec author can. The pivotal move was refusing to rubber-stamp: the "discuss each requirement in turn" request converted a decent first draft into a rigorous, internally-consistent spec. The R1 intervention on ranking-labels was a real design improvement Claude had missed. Decisions were communicated unambiguously (often by quoting Claude's own option list back), which kept velocity high across 15 rounds. The final instruction — "review everything… from spec-driven and context-management perspectives… subdivide for context management… so the next session can implement" — was precisely the right ask at the right moment and is what produced the strong handoff. Minor: the empty "chat" / "I" stray messages and one accidental AskUserQuestion decline were small friction, all self-corrected.

**Claude.** The core intellectual work was excellent: the review template was consistent and genuinely additive (it repeatedly caught cross-requirement coupling, DRY violations in the spec itself, and version-volatility risks tied to the F3 decision). Communication was a standout — every turn ended with a clear decision point and a flagged judgment call, never a wall of undifferentiated options. The weak axis was tool execution: two parallel-batch cancellations and several wasted reads. These didn't corrupt the output (Claude verified integrity with grep/awk sweeps at the end and caught its own stale references), but they're a recurring mechanical pattern — queuing a fragile shell command (a glob that can exit non-zero) in the same batch as important writes. The model-mix detail (first two turns Sonnet, then Opus) is invisible in the substance; the deep reasoning quality is consistent with the Opus majority.

**Together.** This was a high-functioning collaboration: the human supplied judgment and direction, Claude supplied structured analysis and rigorous bookkeeping, and the gate discipline was mutually respected. The session over-delivered on context-survival (the handoff package is better than most "finished" specs) while under-delivering on the literal scope (design deferred) — but that trade was made openly and was the right call.

## Bottom line

A strong requirements-engineering session whose value came from the human's decision to pressure-test every requirement and Claude's disciplined, decision-oriented review template — capped by an unusually thorough cross-session handoff that correctly treated "the reasoning lives only in this chat" as the central risk. The blemishes are mechanical (fragile parallel-Bash batches, redundant reads) rather than substantive, and the one scope gap (design not authored) was a transparent, jointly-agreed deferral rather than a miss. The spec that survives to disk is internally consistent, traceable, and genuinely implementable by a fresh session.
