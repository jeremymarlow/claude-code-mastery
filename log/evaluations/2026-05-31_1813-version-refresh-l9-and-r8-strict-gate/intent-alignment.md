## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This was a near-textbook alignment performance for a maintenance session. Claude grounded every step in file state before acting ("Let me ground the review in the current file state rather than just the ledger prose"), surfaced judgment calls before editing rather than after, and was scrupulous about not letting scope creep contaminate a clean commit (the stash-and-prove-pre-existing maneuver). It elicited the concrete shape of fuzzy asks ("perform the refresh" → an explicit bump/leave/unverified breakdown) and consistently ended turns with a precise confirm-or-redirect question. When the human's interrupt rewrote its understanding, it pivoted instantly and gracefully.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Genuinely little to fault on the alignment axis. The closest thing: before the human's interrupt, Claude was several investigative steps into scoping the refresh on a model of the world (2.1.159 as honest drift) that was incomplete — it had not yet asked the human "why is the recorded version being held behind?" That question, asked up front, would have surfaced the armed-trigger context before the human had to interrupt to supply it. In fairness, the L9 ledger entry framed it as "decision P8-no-bump," which reasonably looked already-decided, so Claude's read was defensible — but a one-line "before I refresh, confirm: was 2.1.158 held deliberately as a trigger, or is this genuinely unverified?" would have been the ideal pre-emptive question.

**Human — strengths.** The human's interrupt is the single best alignment move in the session: recognizing that Claude was about to operate on missing context and supplying exactly the fact that resolved it, at the cost of one interrupted turn. Beyond that, the human's directives were terse but never under-specified relative to the context already shared, the human read Claude's option-menus and picked decisively (often extending scope intelligently, as with the strict-gate ask), and the human respected the per-change approval rhythm — explicit "commit and push" each time. The prime-context skill itself is a human-authored artifact that paid for itself here, establishing shared ground fast.

**Human — weaknesses.** The one real gap is the flip side of insight #1: the deliberately-armed upgrade trigger was a piece of intent that lived only in the human's head (and was framed in the ledger as a generic "no-bump decision"), not as "this anchor is intentionally held to fire the refresh later." Had that been written into the L9 entry, the interrupt would have been unnecessary. It's a minor under-documentation of intent, caught and fixed in real time, but it is the one place where an earlier-recorded "what" would have saved a round-trip.
