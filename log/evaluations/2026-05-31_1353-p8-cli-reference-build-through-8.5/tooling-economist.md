## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Strong capability fit on the search/fetch/edit axis: `ToolSearch` to lazy-load web tools, `WebFetch` with redirect recovery and no-memory provenance discipline, targeted `Grep`/`sed` to locate exact spec anchors before editing rather than re-reading whole files, and `Read`-before-`Edit` on every file (no blind edits). Verification was real and largely reused the production tooling (byte-stability diffs, schema validation, tamper/offline gate cases, a synthetic `added_in` bump). Turn batching was good. Communication was excellent throughout — each slice closed with a crisp, accurate change-list and an explicit "nothing committed; want me to…" handoff, and the 8.4 violation was owned without deflection.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The economy gaps are about *what wasn't reached for*: no subagent for any of the rote/parallelizable work, no model-tiering or `/effort` adjustment across a long session that mixed hard and trivial work on one flagship model, and no self-initiated approval pauses (the human supplied those). A couple of clever inline-Python probes cost a turn each to shell-quoting/loader stumbles, and the too-loose changelog matcher needed a fix-after-the-fact.

**Human — strengths.** The human ran the session like a disciplined product owner with sharp, cheap interrupts: catching the premature 8.2 edit, the missing version history, and the 8.4 approval slip — each a one-turn correction that prevented multi-turn rework. The "how is make check green if the CLI bumped?" probe forced a correct, documented explanation of the drift architecture rather than letting a false assumption ride. And the human turned the 8.4 miss into a durable fix (sharpen CLAUDE.md) instead of a one-off scolding.

**Human — weaknesses.** Minor: the human never asked Claude to delegate or down-tier, so the model-economy leverage went unused on both sides; and the per-change approval gate had to be discovered through a violation rather than restated up front. Both are small relative to how well the human steered.

## Bottom line

A high-quality, correct build whose *output* economy was excellent (production tooling reused as gates, no rework that shipped) but whose *process* economy left clear leverage on the table: one flagship model, serial execution, zero delegation, and approval gates that the human — not Claude — supplied. Claude's tool-and-feature selection on the search/fetch/edit/plan axis was genuinely strong; its missed opportunities were subagents, model-tiering, and self-gating. The human's cheap, well-timed interrupts are what kept the session economical, which is praise for the human and a quiet indictment of Claude's self-regulation.
