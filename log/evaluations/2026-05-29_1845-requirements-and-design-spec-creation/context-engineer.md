## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** Strong priming (orient on dir + memory before acting), and an exemplary session-boundary handoff: it correctly identified that all reasoning would evaporate at the boundary and built `decisions.md` + `IMPLEMENTATION.md` + chunked design/tasks specifically to carry state forward in files. It fought duplication aggressively (matrix-as-canonical, integrative references, grep sweeps for stale terms) and verified integrity with real tool calls rather than assertion. It honored the source-of-truth principle, including the meta-point that the spec itself must not pin a version from memory (R12).

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two recurring mechanical lapses: (1) redundant reads of unchanged files, repeatedly flagged by the harness and even acknowledged in-band; and (2) brittle batch composition, where a single failing shell command (a `.bak` glob, a `mkdir`) cancelled an entire batch of writes — twice — costing rework. Neither harmed the final artifact, but both are precisely the context-economy and tool-discipline issues my lens exists to catch. These cluster late, when the window was long.

**Human — strengths.** The human set the context-engineering agenda directly: they called the close a checkpoint, explicitly invoked "context management," asked for subdivision, and chose the most window-hygienic completion option. They drove the per-requirement slicing and kept their decisions unambiguous. The non-ranking-labels objection (line 1087) was a genuinely good intervention that improved the spec's durability.

**Human — weaknesses.** Very few in this lens. The session ran as one long window when an earlier compaction or boundary might have pre-empted the late-session slips — but the human did ultimately impose the boundary deliberately, and the externalized state made that cost negligible.

## Bottom line

A strong context-engineering session whose through-line is "the files are the memory." The human deliberately treated the session boundary as a first-class design problem, and Claude responded with a genuinely excellent externalization of state — rationale, read-order, chunked work plan, and machine-checkable single-source artifacts — so a fresh, zero-memory session can resume without recollection. That handoff, plus disciplined priming and anti-duplication sweeps, is what earns the high marks. It is held back from spotless by avoidable mechanical waste: repeated reads of unchanged files and fragile tool batches that cancelled real work twice. The substance of context management was excellent; the housekeeping was merely okay.
