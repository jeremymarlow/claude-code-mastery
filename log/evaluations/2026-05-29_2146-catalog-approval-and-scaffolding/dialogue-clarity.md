## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**The human.** Communication was lean and high-signal. Every instruction was scoped and decisive, and the one substantive correction (units, security placement, onboarding) was specific where it needed to be and explicitly delegated where it didn't — which is the hardest balance to strike. The deferred-commit instruction doubled as a session success criterion. The only soft spot is the accidental bare "Please" that triggered an interrupt, but the user immediately disambiguated, so no harm. There is nothing to fault here on either axis.

**Claude.** This is a strong showing on the dialogue-clarity lens. Replies led with the decision, options were presented as genuine forks with named alternatives and Claude's own lean stated ("I lean toward keeping it separate"), the version discrepancy was reported honestly, and the approval gates were respected to the letter without inferring blanket permission. The weaknesses are cosmetic: end-of-phase summaries are longer and more repetitive than a senior reader needs, and a lone decorative checkmark brushes against the repo's emoji convention. Neither cost candor or clarity. One could also argue the long string of status-marker Edits (README/IMPLEMENTATION/tasks/decisions all nudged repeatedly) was narrated turn-by-turn more than necessary — but that narration was honest about what each touched, so it reads as transparency rather than noise.

## Bottom line

A clean, well-gated spec-and-design session where both parties communicated like professionals who trust each other. The human gave terse, decisive, properly-scoped instructions with explicit delegation; Claude led with the lede, turned its own discretionary calls into reviewable forks with real alternatives, reported a version mismatch honestly, and never once treated a prior approval as standing permission. The only blemishes are verbosity in the closing recaps and a stray checkmark — trivial against an otherwise exemplary two-way exchange.
