## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a strong session on my axis. Claude's defining move throughout was *go to the source*: it caught a stale spec claim about version debt by checking `version-data.yaml` and the installed CLI; it read both shipped artifacts and verified their internal anchors against the live repo rather than trusting `make check`; it re-derived the `close-unit` bug from the actual L7 ledger data when challenged; and it consistently re-ran checks and grepped for stale counters before claiming state was current. Crucially, it never fabricated a version specific — every `vd:` claim traced to provenance, and the one factual unknown (fresh-session artifact discovery) was named as unknown rather than smoothed over. The commit messages are evidence-backed and honest, including a deliberate historical note in `decisions.md` documenting the unit-check→prime-context→... swaps so the record isn't laundered.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two soft spots, both on the communication axis. First, `close-unit` was praised and shipped as "correct" behavior it had never actually executed end-to-end — its gating logic is asserted-by-construction, and statements like "close-unit will correctly leave those open in L7" carried more certainty than a never-run prompt-command earns (Claude did, to its credit, defer the real test to the next session). Second, there's mild verbosity-as-confidence: the summaries are long and assertive, and a reader skimming the "✅ ready" tables could miss that the readiness rests on a still-unverified harness behavior. The substance underneath is honest; the packaging occasionally outruns the proof.

**Human — strengths.** The human is the verification engine of this session. Rather than accepting working, green-checking artifacts, they repeatedly asked whether the thing was *actually* the right thing and *actually* useful — surfacing that both `unit-check` and `new-unit` were props with no real consumer, and that `close-unit`'s "leave it open in L7" framing hid a contradiction. They also designed the ultimate verification: authoring U13 in a genuinely fresh session to dogfood the skills for real, refusing to declare the toolkit proven from inside the session that built it. That is verify-don't-trust as a working style.

**Human — weaknesses.** Few on my axis. The human authorized the push contingent on "if no changes are needed," which delegated the final readiness judgment to Claude on exactly the point Claude couldn't fully verify (fresh-session discovery) — a slightly looser gate than the human's own standards elsewhere in the session. Minor, and arguably appropriate given the next-session test was already planned.

## Bottom line

A high-water-mark session for verification discipline, driven by a human who refuses to let "it passes the checks" stand in for "it's the right thing and it's proven." Claude consistently went to the source — catching a stale version-debt claim against the installed CLI, reading its own shipped artifacts and validating their anchors against the live repo, and re-deriving a real bug in its own command from the ledger when challenged — and it named the one thing it genuinely couldn't verify instead of bluffing. The only real soft spot is confidence outrunning proof on `close-unit`, a command shipped as "correct" without ever being run; fittingly, the human had already set up the fresh-session dogfood that will actually test it.
