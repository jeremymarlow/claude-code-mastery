## Insights (ordered by significance)

1. **The session's headline "accuracy win" shipped a factual defect into the committed spec — and it was a defect the session's own evidence already exposed.** Claude framed the per-session model attribution as a deliberate honesty requirement and made it load-bearing: it became R18.AC5 (later AC7), the commit message, and a recurring talking point. But the *content* of that claim was wrong. The probe Claude ran returned `5875 "model":"claude-opus-4-8"` vs `2 "model":"claude-sonnet-4-6"` (_Evidence:_ "→ Bash … distinct model ids across all raw transcripts", showing the 5875/2 split). From two sonnet lines out of ~5877, Claude concluded the entire foundational session "ran on **`claude-sonnet-4-6`**" (_Evidence:_ "the very first and most foundational one … ran on **`claude-sonnet-4-6`**") and committed that into the requirement and the commit body ("Accurate per-session model attribution (sonnet-4-6 for the foundational session, opus-4-8 for the rest)", commit `51d27fe`). The truth, as the project's *own subsequent* `decisions.md` records, is that the foundational session was **opus-dominant and merely mixed** — "its first **2** assistant turns were `claude-sonnet-4-6`, then it switched to `claude-opus-4-8` for the remaining **222**," and the earlier "foundational session = `claude-sonnet-4-6`" was "an *assumption and it was wrong*" (_Evidence:_ `decisions.md` P9-model-attr). This is the worst possible failure for *this* reviewer's lens: an artifact that was loudly advertised as the verified-not-assumed choice was the one place an unverified inference slipped through. The 2-vs-5875 ratio was sitting in Claude's own tool output and should have triggered "two turns, not the whole session," not "ran on sonnet." That the defect rode through a requirements *and* a commit before a later session caught it is exactly the "hand-wavy 'done' over a flawed result" my mandate exists to flag.

## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — what worked:** The spec artifacts are well-constructed and would hold up to an independent reviewer: clean EARS, a coherent matrix model, consistent counts after rebalancing, honest deferral tracking, and a build plan that mirrors the established P8 precedent. The internal-consistency sweeps were thorough and caught genuine latent bugs (the control-lens-description bug, the stale `_global.md` path). The cost/feasibility note was grounded in real measured numbers rather than asserted, and Claude even caught and corrected its own bash subshell bug that zeroed the totals (_Evidence:_ "The `TOTAL` printed 0 — a bash subshell gotcha").

**Claude — what didn't:** The one externally-verifiable fact in the whole deliverable came out wrong and got committed, while being marketed as the verified-not-assumed choice. The 2-vs-5875 evidence that contradicted "ran on sonnet" was in Claude's own hands. The repeated "correctness passes" created an impression of rigor that the substance didn't fully earn — they verified the easy surface (counts, links) and never the hard fact. For a session that produced a *requirement about candor and evidence-grounding*, getting the evidence wrong inside that very requirement is an ironic and real outcome defect.

**Human — what worked:** Every intervention improved the product — the AC2 verbosity split, the panel rebalance, the token-cost grounding demand, the `check-evaluations` reconsideration, and a manual count-check that beat Claude's automated sweep. The human held gates properly and approved per-change.

**Human — what didn't:** Approved R18 (including the flawed attribution claim) without independently checking the model fact — understandable, since Claude presented it as already-verified, but it means the defect cleared the human gate too. Minor.

## Bottom line

The process was exemplary and most of the artifacts are sound, consistent, and claim-matching — but my lens judges the product, and the product carries one consequential defect: the model-attribution claim, sold as the session's signature accuracy win, was an unverified inference that contradicted Claude's own probe output and shipped into both the committed requirement and the commit message before a later session caught and corrected it. A clean, ceremonious process — multiple "correctness sweeps," green checks, secret scans — masked the fact that the single hardest-to-verify, easiest-to-get-wrong claim was never actually verified. Everything else holds up; this one thing is exactly the kind of flaw that a tidy path can hide and an independent check exposes.
