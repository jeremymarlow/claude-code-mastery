**Option C — Video/workshop scripts**
Instructor-facing scripts, talking points, and slide outlines for live or recorded delivery. Built for a *presenter*, not a solo reader.

## Q2 — Spec methodology: how we structure *this spec*

You used "requirements phase / design phase" language, which is the vocabulary of formal spec-driven development. Confirming which flavor.

**Option A — Kiro-style (requirements / design / tasks + EARS)** *(my recommendation)*
Three files with approval gates between them:
- `requirements.md` — user stories + acceptance criteria in **EARS** notation (a disciplined format: *"WHEN [trigger] THE SYSTEM SHALL [behavior]"*, *"IF [condition] THEN THE SYSTEM SHALL…"*). Every requirement gets a stable ID.
- `design.md` — architecture, structure, decisions, traced back to requirement IDs.
- `tasks.md` — an ordered implementation checklist, each task traced to a design component.
- **Pros:** Maximum rigor and traceability; nothing gets built on unreviewed assumptions; and it doubles as a *worked teaching example* inside the course (the course teaches spec-driven dev, so the course's own spec becomes Exhibit A).
- **Cons:** More ceremony; EARS feels stiff at first.
- **Best when:** you want the spec itself to be exemplary — which fits, since we're teaching this.

**Option B — Lightweight PRD + design doc**
One requirements/PRD document plus a design doc, in normal prose — no EARS, looser IDs.
- **Pros:** Faster, less formal, easier to skim.
- **Cons:** Looser traceability; easier for requirements to drift from what gets built.
- **Best when:** speed matters more than rigor.

**Option C — You decide / hybrid**
I pick the structure that best fits "curriculum-as-product" and justify it (likely Kiro-style for the requirements rigor but with a lighter tasks file).

## Q3 — Mastery proof: how the course validates "elite"

Determines the core acceptance criteria around assessment.

**Option A — Per-module checks + capstone** *(my recommendation)*
Every module ends with a **Definition-of-Done checklist** of *objectively verifiable* items (a command runs, a file exists, a test passes, an artifact matches a rubric). The course ends with a realistic **capstone** project graded against a rubric tied to the capability ladder.
- **Pros:** Continuous feedback (learner always knows if they're ready to move on) *plus* a final end-to-end proof. Works for solo learners without a grader.
- **Cons:** Most assessment artifacts to design.
- **Best when:** you want defensible evidence of mastery at every step.

**Option B — Capstone only**
Modules are purely instructional; one big final project is the sole assessment.
- **Pros:** Less to build; the capstone is realistic and motivating.
- **Cons:** A learner can drift for many modules before discovering a gap; no early signal.

**Option C — Self-assessment rubrics**
No graded artifacts at all; each module gives the learner a rubric to score their own work.
- **Pros:** Lightest weight; no "grading" machinery.
- **Cons:** Weakest proof; relies entirely on learner honesty/skill to self-evaluate — risky for someone *new* to the domain who may not know what "good" looks like yet.

## R7 — Hands-on labs against a real codebase

**What it says:** One shared, version-controlled **sample codebase** of non-trivial-but-tractable size as the lab substrate (AC1). Every lab states goal, starting state, exact steps/prompts, and **objective self-check success criteria** (AC2). A **reference solution** for every lab, separated so Sam can try unaided first (AC3). A **clean reset path** between labs (AC4). **Offline/no-credential fallback** for any lab needing external services (AC5).

**Why it's a requirement:**
- This is your **Q1 (hands-on labs) decision** made concrete. Reading about subagents ≠ building one; the labs are where competence is actually forged. It's the substrate the whole capability map (R1) is practiced against.
- AC2's *objective* self-check is the linchpin of **solo learning** (R9): with no instructor, the learner needs an unambiguous "did it work?" — this is what replaces a grader for everything except the capstone.
- AC3 (separated solutions) protects the **productive struggle** that makes skills stick, while still giving a safety net — important for a self-paced learner who can otherwise get stuck and quit.
- AC4 (reset path) is what makes a *single shared* codebase survive ~12 sequential labs without state corruption — the practical cost of choosing one codebase over per-module repos.
- AC5 (offline fallback) protects R15.AC3 (no paid deps) and keeps labs completable on a plane.

**What breaks without it:** Labs become unverifiable ("did I do it right?"), the shared codebase rots across labs, or labs become un-runnable without secrets — any of which kills the solo experience.

**Worthy alternatives / variations:**
1. **One codebase vs two (greenfield + legacy).** We flagged this earlier: R3 now includes "onboarding to an unfamiliar/large codebase" as a workflow. A *deliberately gnarly legacy* codebase is the natural substrate for that, and one clean app doesn't serve it well. Options: (a) one codebase, simulate "legacy" via a messy module within it; (b) **a primary codebase + a small secondary "legacy" repo** for the onboarding/refactor/debug labs. *I lean (b)* — the onboarding workflow is much more authentic against genuinely unfamiliar, messy code; the cost is one extra small repo to maintain. This is the biggest real decision in R7.
2. **Sample-codebase stack/domain** — still Open Q1, a Design deliverable. No change needed here, but R7 should state the *selection criteria* (widely readable, realistic, has tests, has seeded bugs/smells for debug+refactor labs). *I lean: add an AC with selection criteria; keep the actual pick in Design.*
3. **Solution format** — prose walkthrough vs a git branch/tag with the finished diff vs both. For a coding course, a **reference branch/commit** is far more useful than prose. *I lean: require solutions be inspectable as real artifacts (branch/diff), not just prose.*
4. **Verification rigor / auto-checks.** AC2 says objective self-check — could go further: where feasible, ship an **automated check** (a test, a script) the learner runs to confirm success. Ties to dogfooding + R13 automation. *I lean: require automated verification "where feasible," prose self-check otherwise.*
5. **"Bring your own codebase" optional stretch.** Great transfer, but breaks objective self-check (AC2). *I lean: allow as explicitly-optional, clearly marked non-verifiable — never the required path.* (We discussed this earlier; worth pinning.)
6. **Codebase as a maintenance liability under F3.** The sample app must itself stay runnable as toolchains move. *I lean: add it to R12/R13's maintenance surface — note here, enforce there.*

**My lean:** Keep R7 with five changes — (2) add codebase selection criteria (pick stays in Design); (3) require solutions as inspectable real artifacts (branch/diff); (4) require automated verification where feasible; (5) allow optional BYO-codebase, clearly marked non-verifiable; and the big one, (1) **a primary codebase + a small secondary legacy repo**.

Number (1) is the one to decide deliberately — it's the difference between authentically teaching the "onboard to unfamiliar code" workflow (R3) and faking it. The cost is a second small repo in the maintenance surface (F3/R13). Want the two-codebase approach, or keep it to one with a simulated-legacy module inside it? And are you good with changes 2–5?

## R8 — Capstone (sole assessment)

**What it says:** Exactly one capstone, the sole graded assessment, requiring an end-to-end realistic use case that combines context engineering + ≥1 custom extension (command/subagent/skill/hook/MCP) + a non-trivial workflow + explicit verification of Claude's output (AC1). A rubric whose dimensions cover **every can-do statement** in the capability map, grading the *work product* (demonstrated / partially / not yet) — **not** ranking the learner (AC2). Self-applicable so a solo learner can grade their own (AC3). Deliverable includes the learner's **reflection** on where they verified/corrected/overrode Claude (AC4).

**Why it's a requirement:**
- It's your **Q3 decision** (capstone-only) as the keystone. With no per-unit grading, *this is the entire assessment* — so it has to be load-bearing enough to prove mastery across the whole map. That's why AC2 ties it to *every* can-do statement: the capstone is the one place R1 gets verified end-to-end.
- AC1's required ingredients force **integration**, not recall — the elite bar is combining context + extension + workflow + verification under realistic conditions, which no single unit exercises.
- AC2's "grade the work, not the person" is the **direct enforcement of the R1 reframing** we did — the rubric can't sneak person-ranking back in through the assessment door.
- AC3 (self-applicable) is non-negotiable for **solo** (R9) — there's no evaluator, so the rubric must be unambiguous enough to self-apply honestly.
- AC4 (verification reflection) operationalizes the course's **thesis** — "learn to verify, not trust" (R10). It makes the learner *show their skepticism*, which is the single most important elite habit for the "new to AI" persona.

**What breaks without it:** With capstone-only assessment, a weak capstone means *no* credible mastery signal at all. Drop AC2's full-map coverage and "elite" becomes unprovable; drop AC4 and the course's central verification thesis goes unassessed.

**Worthy alternatives / variations:**
1. **Fixed project vs choose-your-own brief** (Open Q3). Fixed = trivially self-gradable against a known solution; choose-your-own = more realistic/motivating but the rubric must be robust to variation. *I lean: choose-your-own brief from a small menu, plus a provided worked exemplar* — realism without a blank page. Keep the actual brief(s) as a Design deliverable.
2. **AI-assisted self-grade.** Have the learner use Claude Code itself to score their capstone against the rubric — reduces self-grading blind spots *and* is pure dogfooding (R14). Risk: learner over-trusts the AI grader — but that's a *teachable moment* perfectly aligned with the verify-don't-trust thesis (they must critique the AI's grade). *I lean: add as an optional, explicitly-critiqued step.*
3. **Does one capstone over-concentrate risk?** With no other graded checkpoint, a learner could reach the capstone and discover big gaps late. Mitigation already exists (lab self-checks + R5.AC3 skip-checks give continuous signal) — but we could add an **optional mid-course "checkpoint" challenge** (ungraded, self-scored) so gaps surface earlier without violating capstone-only. *I lean: add as optional/ungraded.* Your call whether that's worth it or scope creep.
4. **Verification reflection format** (AC4) — freeform vs structured prompts ("one place you caught Claude wrong; one place you accepted with evidence; one place you overrode"). *I lean: structured prompts* — better for a novice who may not know what "good verification" looks like yet.
5. **Capstone scope vs the two codebases (R7).** Should the capstone run against the existing sample codebase, the learner's own, or a fresh brief? *I lean: allow either sample codebase or BYO (marked non-verifiable), defined in Design.*

**My lean:** Keep R8 with four changes — (1) choose-your-own brief + worked exemplar (briefs to Design); (2) optional AI-assisted self-grade that the learner must critique; (3) optional ungraded mid-course checkpoint; (4) structured verification-reflection prompts.

The one I'd flag for your judgment is (3) — the mid-course checkpoint slightly complicates the clean "capstone is the only assessment" story, even though it's ungraded. It's genuinely optional insurance against late-discovered gaps. Want it in, or keep the assessment story maximally clean? And are you good with 1, 2, 4?
