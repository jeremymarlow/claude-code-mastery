## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a model of how to run a delegated phase. Orientation before action, artifacts treated as the absent human's voice, autonomy on the reversible and a check-in on the consequential, single-cycle self-correction without defensiveness, real verification rather than asserted completion, and faithful state-keeping. The final summary was genuinely useful to a reviewer: it led with what landed, separated the two judgment calls into their own section with reasoning and an explicit invitation to override, and stated plainly that nothing was committed. The commit handling respected the per-change-approval rule to the letter.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Very little to fault in-lens. The closest thing: the ~1.65k-LOC outcome diverges from a target the spec explicitly wrote ("~2–4k"), and while Claude flagged it well, it *chose* the divergence unilaterally and proceeded to a finished, committed state with it baked in — a partner could argue that a target the spec bothered to quantify deserved a check-in *before* building 50 files, not a flag after. The render also shows two no-op-looking Edits (auth.py, several where old==new in the digest) that are likely render artifacts rather than real churn; not enough to fault, but a hair of motion the transcript can't fully account for.

**Human — strengths.** Delegated cleanly and at the right altitude — a whole coherent phase, not a babysat task list — and the delegation was *backed* (the artifacts were strong enough to carry it). Said "commit" only when asked to commit, never pre-authorized a push, and used a terse, trusting register that let Claude maintain momentum. This is effective senior delegation, not abdication: the guardrails were pre-loaded into the repo.

**Human — weaknesses.** The communication was minimal to the point that we can't observe judgment being exercised. Claude explicitly teed up two spec-divergent calls and invited pushback; the human engaged with neither, moving straight to "commit." If the LOC shortfall or the extra entity later proves wrong, the correction loop won't have been the human's. Effective delegation, light attention.

## Bottom line

A high-trust delegation that paid off. The human handed Claude an entire phase in three words and got it back correct, verified, and cleanly recorded — because the spec and working agreements did the steering the human didn't have to. Claude calibrated autonomy almost perfectly: silent on the reversible, a well-placed check-in on the two spec-divergent judgment calls, single-cycle self-correction throughout, and scrupulous respect for the commit/push boundary. The only soft spot is on the human's side and is invisible-by-design: a near-silent partner can't be distinguished from an attentive one, and the two flagged judgment calls sailed through unexamined. As a working relationship this session is close to the ideal of trust earned and momentum sustained.
