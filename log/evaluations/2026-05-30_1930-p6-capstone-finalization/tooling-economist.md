## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** This is a near-model run on the tooling-economy axis. The defining behavior was *reading the enforcement code to derive the work specification* — letting `check-traceability` and `make check-strict` define "done" rather than improvising. Sequencing respected the link-validation hooks so forward references never caused a failed gate; the one forward reference was deliberately deferred-as-plain-text and later upgraded. The false-generated-header catch is the kind of integrity find that pays for itself, and the fix was proportionate (build the real generator + drift-gate it, proven by a byte-for-byte diff) rather than gold-plated. Batching was consistent, the state-sync chore (IMPLEMENTATION §3, tasks.md, decisions ledger, P6 task boxes) was thorough, and the PR explanation correctly adapted to a Gitea remote instead of parroting `gh`.

**Claude — weaknesses.** Two small ones. The rejected venv-activation call was an avoidable assumption about the environment that cost a turn. And there's a latent feature-economy gap: this finalization phase involved several independent authoring jobs (case study, rubric, briefs, maintainer guide) that were *serialized* in the main thread — a subagent could plausibly have parallelized the independent prose artifacts. In practice the strict dependency ordering and link-validation made serialization the safer choice, so I won't fault it hard, but it's leverage left on the table worth noting given this is the course that *teaches* subagents.

**Human — strengths.** Tight, correct gate discipline: primed context via the dogfooded `/prime-context` skill, let Claude plan, answered the one real fork decisively, corrected the venv misstep immediately and precisely, and authorized the irreversible steps explicitly. The "explain the mechanism you would use to create a pr" ask was a smart, low-cost way to extract the PR reasoning without actually firing an outward action — good economy.

**Human — weaknesses.** Communication was terse to the point of leaving choices implicit: model selection was never discussed (Opus on rote state-sync is the costly default), and commit+push were bundled. Neither is wrong, but both are places where a word of direction would have tightened the economy.

## Bottom line

A disciplined, gate-driven finalization where Claude treated the enforcement suite as the source of truth for "done," sequenced authoring to respect the validation hooks, and caught a genuine authenticity defect (a false "generated" header) that it fixed with a right-sized tool rather than over-engineering. Turns were economical and well-batched; the only frictions were one rejected venv assumption and the always-present question of whether Opus-grade reasoning was needed for the rote half of the work. The human steered the gates cleanly and asked for the PR mechanism rather than the action — keeping the irreversible step in their own hands. Strong on both sides.
