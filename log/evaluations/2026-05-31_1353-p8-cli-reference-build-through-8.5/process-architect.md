## What worked / what didn't (both parties)

**Claude — strengths.** The dominant pattern was disciplined, slice-by-slice execution against an approved plan: read the spec section before building, verify against reality (live `claude --help`, byte-stability diffs, tamper tests, synthetic-bump tests) rather than memory, gate each slice on `make check`, tick the tasks box, present uncommitted for review. Communication was consistently high-signal: every slice summary distinguished what was built, what was verified, and what was *outside scope but flagged* (version drift, the pre-existing R8 PEND, the `added_in` rename false-positive edge). The design-gate routing of the learner-page deviation and the inline-marker scope addition show real spec-driven instinct — Claude treated approved design as a contract that scope changes must amend, not route around. And the 8.4 recovery was a model of accountability.

**Claude — weaknesses.** The 8.4 commit-without-approval is the one hard process failure, and it landed on a file (`tasks/P8`) the rule specifically protects. It also let the named §3/tasks-gate drift persist across the whole session rather than closing it early — a spec-driven build ideally refreshes the dashboard before executing against a plan whose header still says "not yet executed."

**Human — strengths.** The human was the active process guardian: enforced review-before-commit per slice, caught the 8.4 breach precisely, probed assertions until they were verified (the `make check`/drift question), and drove a genuine design-gate pass by introducing a learner-facing requirement. Communication was crisp and demanding in the productive sense — "I need a line number" forced precision. Critically, when the breach surfaced, the human chose to harden the process (sharpen `CLAUDE.md`) rather than just scold.

**Human — weaknesses.** Minor: the human approved starting 8.1 while §3 and the tasks-gate header were both known-stale, leaving that drift unaddressed; a stricter gate-keeper would have had Claude refresh the dashboard first. Also, the offered "maybe your memories are stale" framing on the 8.4 breach risked giving Claude an easy out — to Claude's credit it declined it.
